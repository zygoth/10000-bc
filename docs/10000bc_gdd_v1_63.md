## 1. Game Overview & Vision

An isometric turn-based grid-based survival foraging game set in 10,000 BC Indiana. The player and their partner — a couple who have left their tribe to start a new family group — must learn the land, feed a growing family, and build a small tribe across five epochs spanning roughly 25 years. Real-world plant knowledge is the primary mechanical advantage. The core loop is daily foraging, nightly camp management, and long-term seasonal planning.

Setting note: modern Indiana flora is used as a practical baseline. 10,000 BC Indiana was recently deglaciated with somewhat different plant communities in reality, but the game prioritizes playability and botanical education over strict historical accuracy.

Built with a clean separation between game logic and rendering, enabling fast functional testing and easy AI-assisted development.

### 1.1 Player Origin

The player and partner are a young couple who left their tribe voluntarily — the group had grown too large for the local territory. They bring practical knowledge from their upbringing (reflected in starting unlocks and plant identification) but must now establish their own territory, learn their new land, and grow their own family group from scratch.

### 1.2 Pillars

- **Real knowledge matters** — players who know plants get tangible, mechanical advantages
- **Every day is a decision** — the tick budget creates constant meaningful tradeoffs
- **Stakes are real** — any tribe member death ends the game
- **Seasons define life** — winter demands fall preparation; spring is renewal

### 1.3 Win & Loss

**Win:** Survive 5 epochs (~25 years). First child reaches adulthood, tribe is self-sustaining.

**Loss:** Any tribe member (player, partner, or child) dies from starvation, injury, illness, or other cause. No single bar drops instantly — minimum degradation rate ensures the player always has at least one overnight warning cycle before any death.

**Warning gradient:** Each tribe member has three independent status bars — Hunger, Thirst, and Health. The HUD icon for each member shows the worst of the three (green → yellow → orange → red). At yellow, a text warning appears at day start (e.g. "Partner is hungry"). At orange, the nightly debrief forces acknowledgment before proceeding.

If the Hunger or Thirst bar reaches zero, the Health bar begins draining immediately at a rate of **1/3 bar per 400 active day-ticks** for each depleted bar (up to 2/3 bar per 400 ticks if both are empty). The drain begins the moment the bar hits zero and stops as soon as food or water raises it above zero. Death occurs when the Health bar reaches zero. Injuries and other direct damage hit the Health bar immediately. Health recovers naturally at **5% per 400 active day-ticks** when both Hunger and Thirst are above 25%.

**Night freeze:** All three bars (Hunger, Thirst, Health) tick only during the active day (ticks 0–400). No hunger, thirst, or health ticks occur during the night. The "at least one overnight warning cycle before death" guarantee holds because a full bar at maximum drain rate lasts a full active day — the nightly debrief warning system always has at least one cycle to alert the player before health depletion can occur.

**Long game / sequel hook:** A larger world map with new biomes that the oldest child can explore once the first game is won.

## 2. Calendar & Time System

### 2.1 Time Units

| Unit | Value | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tick | Smallest unit | 1 move = 1 tick; all actions cost variable ticks |
| Day | 400 ticks total | Player tick budget is 200 base (see 2.2); remaining ticks represent night |
| Season | 10 days | Short seasons create urgency |
| Year | 4 seasons / 40 days | Indiana climate model |
| Epoch | 1 survival year + 5-year skip | Year runs mid-spring to mid-spring (spring day 5 to spring day 5); landscape evolves during skip |
| Full game | 5 epochs / ~25 in-game years | |

### 2.2 Dynamic Tick Budget

The day has two separate time values that the UI tracks independently:

- **Day tick counter** — the global clock, starting at 0 and running to 400. All players and events reference this value. The UI displays it as a progress bar showing where in the day it currently is.
- **Personal tick budget** — how many ticks of *action* each person can take before their energy is spent. Separate from the day clock.

**Player base budget:** 200 ticks
- +20 ticks if the previous night's stew met the household protein and fat thresholds (see Section 9.1)
- +10 ticks if sleeping in a comfortable shelter (Camp Comfort, see Section 7.3)

**Partner budget:** Same as player (200 ticks base). Fire-tending and cooking are auto-scheduled first from this pool (see Section 7.4). The protein/fat tick bonus applies to the partner on the same condition as the player — based on the previous night's stew.

**Foraging child (Epoch 5):** 200 ticks base, same bonuses apply on the same stew-based condition.

**Budget overdraft:** A player can spend ticks beyond their personal budget — every tick past the budget is an overdraft tick. Overdraft ticks are deducted from the *following* day's budget. The next day begins with the day tick counter already advanced to the overdraft amount — as if the player slept late — and can only act from that point forward.

**Pass-out threshold:** If the player accumulates more than 40 overdraft ticks in a single day, they pass out. The nightly debrief still runs normally, but the player is absent from the stew — they do not eat from the pot regardless of its contents. Their hunger bar degrades as if they received zero calories from the stew; any calories already consumed via field eating that day count normally and offset the shortfall as usual. The protein/fat tick bonus is unaffected for the rest of the household. The next day begins at tick 40.

**Passing out outside camp:** If the player passes out while outside the 3×3 camp area, they suffer an additional −30 ticks on top of the standard tick-40 day start — the next day begins at tick 70. Sleeping rough without shelter is simply worse than overdrafting at camp.

**Passing out without cold protection in winter:** If the player passes out while outside camp during Cold or Freezing temperature bands without a coat equipped, they do not survive the night — exposure kills before morning. This is an immediate game over. A player who passes out at camp is safe regardless of season, since the fire keeps them warm.

The darkness threshold — the tick at which night penalties begin — varies by season to model longer summer days and shorter winter ones (see Section 6.4). A player whose budget is exhausted before dark should return to camp; a player who pushes past dark faces the night penalty regardless of remaining budget ticks. Overdraft and the night penalty are independent — a player can be in overdraft during the day without triggering night penalties, and can incur night penalties without being in overdraft if they simply stayed out late with remaining budget.

This creates a virtuous cycle: good nutrition and camp investment lead to more foraging time. It also makes the winter slide dangerous — poor nutrition means missing the bonus ticks and accumulating nutritional deficiency debuffs (see Section 9.1) that cut into your effective daily capacity when you can least afford it.

**Action interruption policy (runtime):** when the acting character becomes unavailable mid-action (e.g., pass-out/health collapse), the action system either preserves progress or keeps legacy atomic resolution based on action class.

- **Resumable player actions (progress preserved):** `process_item`, `tool_craft`, `camp_station_build`, `dig`. Runtime stores remaining ticks and resumes later; outputs/unlocks apply only on completion.
- **Atomic actions (current behavior):** all other actions still resolve as immediate-effect + tick spend in one step. If these need interruption-safe behavior later, they require explicit per-action conversion and regression coverage.

### 2.3 Seasons (Indiana Climate)

| Season | Key Characteristics | Primary Challenges |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Spring | First greens, possible flooding, cool temps | Limited calories, wet terrain |
| Summer | Peak plant diversity, long days | Nausea risk from repetition, heat TBD |
| Fall | Fruiting, nut harvest, critical stockpile window | Time pressure, winter prep |
| Winter | Minimal foraging, frozen ground, short days | Calorie deficit, dependence on stockpile & traps; good time for hunting, camp building and research if stocked |

### 2.3a Seasonal Window Labels

Plant sub-stage availability windows use named thirds of each season. Each season is 10 days; the thirds map to day ranges within that season:

| Label suffix | Days in season | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `early_X` | Days 1–3 | `early_summer` = summer days 1–3 |
| `mid_X` | Days 4–7 | `mid_fall` = fall days 4–7 |
| `late_X` | Days 8–10 | `late_spring` = spring days 8–10 |
| `X` (bare season name) | Days 1–10 (full season) | `"end": "fall"` = through fall day 10 |

A bare season name used as an `end` value means through the last day of that season — it is shorthand for `late_X` when the intent is "this sub-stage persists until the season ends." Using a bare name as a `start` value is equivalent to `early_X`.

The absolute day of year is always derivable: `(season_index × 10) + day_within_season`. Season indices: spring = 0, summer = 1, fall = 2, winter = 3.

Sub-stage windows use `start` (inclusive, first day the sub-stage is active) and `end` (inclusive, last day). A sub-stage with `"start": "early_summer", "end": "late_fall"` is available from summer day 1 through fall day 10. Sub-stages transition instantaneously at day boundaries — there is no overlap between consecutive sub-stages on the same part.

### 2.4 Epoch Transitions

After surviving year 1: fast-forward 5 years. The epoch skip is a narrative compression, not a reset. The game resumes at **mid-spring (spring day 5)** of the new epoch with all player state intact: health, inventory, stockpile, and camp carry over exactly. The 5-year gap is treated as if the year simply continued. Surplus stockpile is a genuine head start; arriving depleted means beginning the new epoch already under pressure.

Each epoch year runs from mid-spring to mid-spring — the survival year begins at spring day 5 and ends at spring day 5 of the following year, covering a full 40-day cycle with no days omitted. This means the player always has a few spring days of foraging behind them before the epoch ends, and always resumes with the land already warming rather than facing the tail end of winter cold snaps at the start of a new epoch.

**Epoch skip rules:** Nothing changes to the player's inventory or stockpile during the skip — all items carry over exactly as left. The only things that change are the landscape (based on the player's actions during the year; see Section 3.7) and the family (children age 5 years; a new child is born). The epoch transition screen shows the new landscape state and family composition before the player resumes. Epoch 2+: player can choose new camp location at epoch start — relocation is free, handled during the skip. All camp equipment and stations travel with the camp at no cost.

- Epoch 1: player + partner only. First child born during 5-year skip, arrives at epoch 2 already age 5.
- Each subsequent epoch: +1 additional child born during the skip

## 3. Map Generation & Inter-Epoch Influence

### 3.1 Dimensions & Visibility

300 x 300 tiles, hard border. Camp roughly central — 100-tile radius reachable in a full foraging day. Full map visible from start — no fog of war. Challenge is knowing what you're looking at.

### 3.2 Generation Pipeline (Layered Noise)

- **Step 1: Elevation** (Perlin noise) — determines drainage, floodplains, ridges
- **Step 2: Water** — see §3.2a for full water generation spec. High-moisture band applied to all tiles within 2 of any water tile after placement.
- **Step 3: Soil type** — derived from elevation + water proximity
  - Sandy/well-drained on slopes and ridges
  - Clay/poor drainage in low flat areas
  - Rich loam on floodplains
  - Rocky on hilltops
- **Step 3b: Rock tile placement** — see §3.4b. Placed after soil type is established; uses soil class and elevation as density inputs. Two rock subtypes: glacial erratics (map-wide scatter, biased toward hilltops) and flint cobble scatters (concentrated on rocky-soil hilltops and ridge lines). Rock tiles are permanent landscape features.
- **Step 4: Shade/canopy** — seeded by soil, self-reinforcing as trees grow
- **Step 4b: Dead and decaying logs** — see Section 3.4a for full rules; placed after canopy layer so host species are known
- **Step 5: Species pool selection** — 70 species drawn from the master list for this run; see §3.9a for selection rules
- **Step 6: Founder placement** — sparse starting populations placed for each pool species at soil-appropriate locations; see §3.9b
- **Step 7: Pre-game dispersal simulation** — §3.9 runs for N virtual years (suggested 20–30), producing the naturalistic epoch 1 plant distribution; world events (lightning, beaver activity, flood dispersal) fire during this phase; see §3.9c
- **Step 8: Post-simulation viability validation** — functional guarantees checked; fallback placement injected where needed without re-rolling; see §3.3

### 3.2a Water Body Generation

Water generation uses a **river-spine approach**: one main river provides the geographic backbone of the map, fed by one or two tributaries, terminating either at the map edge or in a pond at the lowest basin. The result varies meaningfully run to run in layout and proportions while remaining geographically coherent.

**Generation pass:**

1. **Trace the main river.** Starting from a high-elevation point on or near a map edge (selected from the top quartile of elevation values), trace a path downhill using a biased random walk: each step moves toward the lowest available adjacent tile with moderate lateral wander to produce natural meanders rather than a straight diagonal. The river walks until it either reaches the opposite map edge or arrives at the lowest basin on the map. At the lowest basin, roll: 60% chance the river exits the map edge (continuing off-screen), 40% chance it terminates in a pond instead.

2. **Assign river tile types by gradient.** After the path is traced, calculate the elevation drop per tile along the course. Steep sections (high drop-per-tile) become `fast_river`; shallow gradient sections become `slow_river`. Transitions are smoothed over 3–5 tiles to avoid abrupt changes. Wide meander sections in flat terrain are always `slow_river`.

3. **Paint river width.** The traced centerline is expanded into a band of tiles. The centerline and 1–2 tiles each side become `deep` water; 1 tile further each side becomes `shallow` water. This produces a total river width of 5–6 tiles (1 shallow + 3–4 deep + 1 shallow). Tile type (`fast_river` / `slow_river`) is inherited from the nearest centerline tile. At meander bends, the outside of the curve gains an extra deep tile (eroded bank) and the inside loses one (deposited silt bar) — this is a one-tile adjustment applied to each bend, adding naturalism without significant complexity.

4. **Place tributaries.** From 1–2 secondary high-elevation points elsewhere on the map, trace smaller streams downhill using the same algorithm but with less lateral wander. Each stream flows until it meets the main river or the map edge. Stream tiles are always typed `stream` and are always `shallow` — they are narrow enough to step across without penalty. A run may produce 1 tributary, 2 tributaries, or occasionally none if terrain doesn't support a natural secondary high point.

5. **Place fishing hotspots.** After all water tiles are placed, run a noise pass over them to assign `hotspot: true` to roughly 10–15% of water tiles. Hotspots are biased toward: deep-to-shallow transition tiles, the outside of river bends, river confluences (where a stream meets the main river), and pond centers. Hotspot tiles apply a 1.75× multiplier to catch rates for any fishing method on that tile. Hotspot tiles display a surface-activity visual cue (fish jumping) visible to the player without any identification requirement — the signal is purely spatial.

6. **Place pond (if terminus roll succeeded, or as secondary feature).** The terminus pond is placed at the lowest basin at the end of the river walk. Its size is rolled from a small range (roughly 8–20 tiles of open water in an irregular shape). Additionally, a small independent pond may be placed at any secondary low basin not connected to the river system — this is optional and occurs on roughly 50% of runs, providing a second fishing water body in a different map region. Ponds receive the same hotspot pass as rivers.

7. **Apply moisture band.** All land tiles within 2 tiles of any water tile receive elevated moisture. Floodplain soil quality bonuses are applied to tiles within 3 tiles of `slow_river` segments (rich loam — see Step 3 of the main pipeline).

**Resulting tile type distribution (approximate per run):**

| Tile type | Depth | Typical count | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| `fast_river` | deep | 60–120 tiles | Steep upper reaches; wide band; impassable without bridge or ice |
| `slow_river` | deep | 120–240 tiles | Broad middle and lower reaches |
| `slow_river` / `fast_river` | shallow | ~2× the deep tile count | One tile either side of the deep band |
| `stream` | shallow | 30–80 tiles per tributary | Narrow; no crossing penalty |
| `pond` | deep (center) + shallow (edge) | 8–20 tiles terminus + 0–15 secondary | Still water; no weirs; edge tiles are shallow |

These ranges are intentionally wide — a run might have a short river dominated by fast water, or a long meandering slow river with minimal fast sections. This variance creates meaningfully different fishing and movement geography run to run.

### 3.2b River Crossing & Fishing Access

**Water tile depth** is assigned during generation (see §3.2a). Crossing costs are per-tile movement through water:

| Tile type | Depth | Movement cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| `stream` | always shallow | 1 tick (no penalty) | Treated as normal terrain |
| `slow_river` / `fast_river` | shallow | 2 ticks | Wadeable |
| `slow_river` / `fast_river` | deep | 4 ticks | Swimming |
| `pond` | shallow (edge) | 2 ticks | |
| `pond` | deep (center) | 4 ticks | |

**Frozen water (winter):** when the daily temperature falls into the Freezing band, all water tiles freeze and become walkable at normal movement cost (1 tick/tile). Ice persists until temperature rises above Freezing. Ice fishing: a frozen tile can be accessed for fishing by cutting an axe hole (see §15.5).

**Fishing cast range:** the player (or auto-rod) fishes all cardinally and diagonally adjacent water tiles — range 1, meaning the 8 tiles surrounding the player's standing tile. The player does not choose which tile to fish; the catch roll samples the adjacent water tiles weighted by their individual fish density. Standing on a wooden platform tile over water extends reach accordingly — each platform tile is a new standing position.

**Natural fish-density hotspots (emergent):** water tiles are not manually flagged as hotspots. Instead, each eligible tile receives a deterministic equilibrium density value based on fish habitat compatibility plus seeded per-tile variation. This produces naturally stronger and weaker fishing patches without explicit hotspot tags.

**Future depletion rule (when player fishing actions are implemented):** on each successful catch, reduce local fish population in a radius-2 area around the catch tile (strongest at center, tapering outward). This creates a movement incentive: repeatedly fishing one spot lowers short-term returns and rewards rotating between locations.

**Wooden Platform**

A craftable tile-spanning structure placed over water tiles. Serves as both a river bridge (spanning the full width to eliminate crossing costs) and a fishing pier (extending into a pond or river to reach naturally high-density fishing patches beyond normal cast range). Bridge and pier are the same item — the distinction is purely what the player builds it toward.

- **Recipe:** 2 poles + 2 cordage units per tile, 20 ticks per tile. Crafted in place — the player stands at the water's edge and builds tile by tile outward.
- **Placement:** the player targets an adjacent water tile to place the next platform section. Each section must be adjacent to either a land tile or an existing platform tile — no floating sections.
- **Movement:** platform tiles are walkable at 1 tick/tile regardless of the water depth beneath them. A complete bridge spanning a 6-tile wide river costs 12 poles + 12 cordage + 120 ticks and saves 3 ticks per crossing (vs deep water at 4 ticks) on each tile replaced. A full 6-tile crossing saves ~18 ticks per full crossing; ~10 round trips pay back the build cost in ticks alone, before accounting for new fishing access.
- **Fishing from platform:** the player can fish from any platform tile, treating it as a normal standing position. A 2-tile pier into a pond puts the player 2 tiles from the bank, giving access to tiles 3 tiles from the original bank — including deep center tiles and naturally high-density patches within range.
- **Durability:** platform tiles degrade from current stress on `fast_river`. A `fast_river` platform tile lasts ~15 days before needing repair (1 pole + 1 cordage, 10 ticks per tile). Platform tiles on `slow_river`, `stream`, or `pond` do not degrade.
- **Sprites:** platform tiles use a simple plank-over-water sprite. Platform visual fish-activity effects, if present, are derived from underlying density rather than manual hotspot flags.

### 3.3 Viability Guarantees

Viability is checked after the dispersal simulation completes (Step 8), not during placement. The old approach of re-rolling the entire map on failure is replaced with **fallback placement**: if a functional requirement is unmet, the validator injects a small naturalistic cluster of an appropriate species at the nearest suitable tile rather than discarding the map. The map is only re-rolled for hard terrain failures (no water source near camp, insufficient soil diversity) that cannot be fixed by plant injection.

**Terrain guarantees (re-roll if unmet):**
- Water source within 10 tiles of starting camp
- Minimum soil type diversity represented on the map

**Functional guarantees — Tier 1 (must be within ~80 tiles of starting camp):**

These are required close to camp because the player cannot choose their starting location and will need them before they can range far. If any are absent from the 80-tile radius after the simulation, the validator places a cluster of 3–5 mature plants of a qualifying species on the nearest soil-suitable tile within that radius.

| Function | Requirement | Why proximity matters |
|---|---|---|
| High-quality cordage | At least one species with high `cordage_quality` (long fiber + strong modifier) | Coat and reliable snares are impossible without it |
| Calorie staple | At least one starchy tuber or nut species | Carrying winter nutrition |
| Fever treatment | At least one species with `treatment_tag: "fever_tea"` | Fever kills; partner needs this in stockpile |
| Wound treatment | At least one species with `treatment_tag: "antibacterial_poultice"` | Wound infection is near-certain across a full run |
| Insulation | At least one species with craft tag `insulation_material` | Coat is required for winter survival |
| Hallucinogen | At least one species with a `hallucinogen` effect in its `dose_response` | Vision system is a core research mechanic |

**Functional guarantees — Tier 2 (must exist anywhere on the map):**

These can be found during mid-game ranging, or acquired via the traveler event as a backstop. If absent map-wide, the validator places a small cluster at any soil-suitable tile.

| Function | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Cordage source | At least one `cordage_fiber` species |
| Sprain treatment | At least one species with `treatment_tag: "anti_inflammatory_poultice"` |
| Dietary variety | At least one fruit or berry species with `edibility_score ≥ 0.85` |

**Fallback placement note:** injected clusters are placed after the dispersal simulation completes, so they have not gone through any dispersal passes. They will be static — no seedlings, no spread pattern around them, no associated pH history. This is a minor ecological inconsistency but acceptable given that fallback placement should be rare on well-tuned maps. Injected clusters use a fixed small size (3–5 mature plants) and are placed on the nearest soil-suitable tile to camp, not necessarily the ecologically ideal location.

### 3.4 Deliberate Terrain Features

- Dead standing snags and fallen logs — scattered throughout forested areas; immediate mushroom sources from epoch 1 (see Section 3.4a)
- Glacial erratics and flint cobble scatters — permanent rock tiles placed at map gen; block germination, cast permanent shade, and are the sole source of `flat_stone` and `flint_cobble` materials (see Section 3.4b)

### 3.4a Dead Trees & Pre-Existing Logs

Not all fallen logs on the map are player-created. The world generates with a naturalistic scatter of standing dead snags and fallen logs already at various stages of decay — the remnants of trees that died before the player arrived. These are **immediately present in Epoch 1**, giving the player mushroom sources before any felling occurs. They reward exploration and give a reason to scout forest interiors early. This should happen naturally as a result of the map gen--given the old perennial 5% death rate and enough time for trees to establish before the player enters the map this shouldn't need to be an extra pass.

#### Standing Dead Snags

deprecated--all trees transition to a universal 'dead tree' sprite after death, and decay to nothing 4 years afterwards.


### 3.4b Rock Tiles

Indiana at 10,000 BC was in the tail end of the Laurentide Ice Sheet retreat. The glacier deposited glacial erratics and till across the entire state — boulders and flint nodules scattered on the surface wherever the ice dropped them. Rock tiles represent this landscape. They are permanent features: they are never removed, do not decay, and cannot be relocated by the player.

#### Rock Subtypes

**Glacial Erratic** — large rounded boulders of mixed lithology dropped by the glacier. Common; scattered across the whole map with density biased toward hilltops and ridges. No crafting value on their own, but yield a `flat_stone` material item when the player harvests from them (see below). Erratics are the source material for the shovel's `flat_stone` component and for the dead-fall trap's `heavy_rock` component.

**Flint Cobble Scatter** — surface concentrations of chert/flint nodules redistributed by glacial action. Rarer; concentrated on rocky-soil hilltops and ridge lines. Yield `flint_cobble` material items. Flint cobbles are required for the flint knife and axe. A flint cobble scatter tile has a finite surface supply (3–5 cobbles total across the full game, rolled at map gen from the seed) — once exhausted, no more cobbles can be harvested from that tile, though the tile remains and still blocks germination and casts shade.

#### Generation Rules

Rock tiles are placed during Step 3b of the map generation pipeline, after soil type is established.

| Subtype | Density rule | Preferred terrain |
|---|---|---|
| Glacial erratic | ~3–5% of all non-water land tiles | Hilltops and ridges at higher rate (~8–10%); floodplains at lower rate (~1%) |
| Flint cobble scatter | ~0.5–1% of all non-water land tiles | Rocky-soil hilltops and ridge lines only; never on floodplain or clay soil |

Rocks are placed with a minimum spacing of 2 tiles from each other to prevent clustering that would block large areas of germination. They are never placed on water tiles, camp tiles, or within 1 tile of the starting camp position.

#### Tile Effects

**Germination blocking:** a rock tile cannot host any plant. Seeds scattered onto a rock tile are wasted (the scatter spiral skips rock tiles automatically, same as occupied tiles). Underground parts cannot be dug on a rock tile.

**Shade casting:** rock tiles cast shade onto adjacent tiles using the same shade model as tree canopy, but at reduced strength. This is permanent shade — it does not vary by season.

| Adjacency | Shade contribution |
|---|---|
| Cardinally adjacent (N/S/E/W) | 0.4 shade units (same scale as tree canopy) |
| Diagonally adjacent | 0.2 shade units |

Rock shade stacks with canopy shade on the same tile. Shade-tolerant plants can establish in the shadow of a rock just as they would under a tree.

#### Harvesting

The player harvests material from a rock tile by selecting the **Harvest** action on it. No tool is required. The rock tile itself is permanent — harvesting removes a material item from the tile's supply, not the tile.

| Subtype | Yield item | Supply | Tick cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Glacial erratic | `flat_stone` (1 per harvest) | Unlimited (permanent feature) | 3 ticks |
| Flint cobble scatter | `flint_cobble` (1 per harvest) | 3–5 total (rolled at map gen; tracked on tile) | 5 ticks |

When a flint cobble scatter's supply is exhausted, the harvest action is no longer available on that tile. The tile info on mouseover shows remaining cobbles for identified flint scatter tiles (always visible — the player can see surface stones).

#### Viability Guarantee

At least one flint cobble scatter must exist within 60 tiles of the starting camp. The flint knife is a no-research starting recipe and the player's first critical tool. If map generation places no flint scatter within this radius, the validator injects one on the nearest rocky-soil tile within range (Step 8 viability check).

#### Visual Style

Rock tiles use a distinct sprite in the botanical drawing aesthetic: glacial erratics are large rounded grey-brown boulders with lichen texture detail; flint cobble scatters are clusters of smaller dark grey angular stones with a slightly glassy surface quality. Both are immediately visually distinguishable from each other and from soil tiles. Rock tiles have no moisture-based background color variation — they render as stone regardless of surrounding moisture.

### 3.5 Camp Placement

Camp occupies a 3×3 tile area on the world map.

**Epoch 1 (auto-placement):** The algorithm scores all candidate 3×3 areas and selects the best tile meeting all of the following:
- Within 10 tiles of a water source
- At least ~3,600 tiles of contiguous land reachable without crossing a deep river tile (see §3.2b). If no candidate meets this threshold, the largest available contiguous zone is used.

**Epoch 2+ (player-chosen):** Player selects a new camp location at epoch start from tiles within 70 tiles of the original camp. The same water proximity and isolation constraints apply — the UI highlights valid placements. All camp stations and equipment travel to the new location at no cost during the epoch skip.

### 3.6 Seed Scattering

The player selects the **Scatter Seeds** action, picks one or more seed types from inventory, then clicks a target tile. Cost: **1 tick per scatter action**, regardless of how many seeds are thrown at once.

**Placement algorithm:** each tile is a single plant occupancy site (see Section 5.4). The player clicks a target tile as the scatter origin. Seeds fill outward in a nearest-first spiral from that origin tile to adjacent tiles. Every seed attempts to claim one tile; occupied or invalid tiles are skipped and the spiral continues.

**Map border handling:** If the spiral encounters the map border (tiles beyond the 300×300 boundary), those out-of-bounds positions are skipped and the spiral continues searching inward for the next valid tile. Seeds are never lost to out-of-bounds placement — the algorithm simply skips invalid positions and keeps filling available slots.

For precise single-seed placement, the player repeats the action once per seed at the exact target tile. For a broadcast scatter of many seeds, selecting them all in one action fills outward from the target tile automatically. Cost scales with intentionality: scattering 20 seeds at once costs 1 tick; placing 20 seeds individually at specific tiles costs 20 ticks.

- Seeds only germinate if soil conditions match the plant's requirements: pH, drainage, shade tolerance, and moisture. No guarantee of growth — seeds scattered into unsuitable soil produce nothing.
- Over multiple epochs, strategic scattering near camp can densify desirable plants in accessible areas.
- Combines naturally with tree felling: fell a tree → light gap opens → scatter pioneer or edge-species seeds into the newly sunny tile.

### 3.7 Inter-Epoch Influence — Landscape Shaping

The landscape changes during the 5-year epoch skip based on what the player did during the year. Actions with long-term consequences:

- **Seed scattering** — plants establish where conditions match their requirements
- **Tree felling** — creates light gaps, encourages pioneer plants and forest edge species; felled trunk may develop fungi over 5 years
- **Heavy harvesting** — depletes slow-recovering plant populations; potential local extinction of over-harvested species in an area
- **Transplanting whole plants (shovel)** — deliberately move plants closer to camp
- **Trapping pressure** — reduces animal populations, which may help browsed plants recover

**Tree Felling details:** Requires axe; felling is a deliberate action with a tick cost that scales with tree size. Immediate effect: tile loses shade contribution; adjacent tiles gain light; plants in the felled tile are destroyed. The felled trunk becomes a log tile at decay stage 1 and is added to the log tile list; it will receive its first fungus colonization roll on the following winter day 1. Surrounding tiles gain pioneer plant communities via the dispersal simulation (§3.9 disturbance bonus during the daily growth pass); creates forest edge if done at the boundary of a stand. Strategic use: a player who fells trees over several epochs can gradually expand forest edge near camp, which has the highest plant biodiversity in the game.

**Tree felling tick costs and pole yields:**

| Tree Size | Tick Cost | Pole Yield | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Small (sapling / shrub) | 15 ticks | 1 pole | Young willows, shrubby understory |
| Medium | 40 ticks | 3 poles | Most understory trees |
| Large | 80 ticks | 6 poles | Mature oaks, hickories |
| Very Large | 130 ticks | 10 poles | Old-growth specimens |

### 3.8 Log Fungus Spawning — Data Specification

This section defines the data structures and rules used by the winter day 1 fungus colonization roll inside `advanceDay` (see §3.9). It is a reference spec, not a trigger description — the winter day 1 calendar event is the single place in the simulation that calls these rules. The spawn logic applies identically whether a log was created by the player, by a world event (lightning, beaver), or was pre-existing at map generation.

#### 3.8.1 Species Selection

Fungus species are matched to the log using two criteria: **host tree compatibility** and **log decay stage**. Candidates are drawn exclusively from the `run_fungus_pool` (see §3.9a) — species not in the pool for this run are never eligible regardless of host or decay match.

**Host tree compatibility** is the primary filter. Each fungus species carries a `host_trees` array in its data object — a list of tree species IDs it can colonize. Only species whose `host_trees` list includes the felled tree's species ID (or the special wildcard value `"any_hardwood"` / `"any_softwood"`) are eligible to roll.

Indiana species and their realistic host pairings:

| Fungus Species | Host Trees (Indiana) | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chicken of the Woods (*Laetiporus sulphureus*) | Oak, cherry, locust, sassafras | Rare; spectacular yield when present |
| Hen of the Woods / Maitake (*Grifola frondosa*) | Oak only | Base of trunk or large root flares; very nutritious |
| Oyster Mushroom (*Pleurotus ostreatus*) | Elm, cottonwood, beech, box elder, any dead hardwood | Common; grows in shelf clusters |
| Lion's Mane (*Hericium erinaceus*) | Oak, beech, maple, walnut | Single fruiting body, distinctive; rare |
| Turkey Tail (*Trametes versicolor*) | Any dead hardwood | Ubiquitous bracket; low calories but medicinal |
| Reishi (*Ganoderma curtisii*) | Oak, hardwood | Very slow grower; prized medicinal |
| Giant Puffball (*Calvatia gigantea*) | Near any dead hardwood, moist soil | Ground-adjacent, not directly on log; requires moist tile |
| Velvet Foot (*Flammulina velutipes*) | Elm, box elder, willow | Late fall and winter species; cold-tolerant |
| King Stropharia (*Stropharia rugosoannulata*) | Wood chip/debris contact, moist humus | Rare natural occurrence on disturbed log debris |

**Decay stage filter:** Each log tracks a `decay_stage` value (1–4), advancing by 1 each in-game year the log remains. Each yearly decay tick enriches soil fertility on the log tile and all 8 adjacent tiles. After a log completes stage 4, it crumbles and is removed from the tile. Fungus species have a `preferred_decay_stages` array. Only species compatible with the current decay stage are eligible.

| Decay Stage | Description | Preferred Species |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 (fresh, bark intact) | First year after felling | Chicken of the Woods, Hen of the Woods |
| 2 (bark loosening, wood soft) | Second year | Oyster, Lion's Mane, Turkey Tail |
| 3 (deeply punky, partially collapsed) | Third year | Turkey Tail, Reishi, Velvet Foot, Puffball (adjacent) |
| 4 (crumbling, merging with soil) | Fourth year (final visible stage) | Turkey Tail, Reishi only; log yields no more poles |

A log at decay stage 1 cannot spawn Oyster mushrooms; a stage 4 log cannot spawn Chicken of the Woods. This creates a time-sensitive relationship with old felled logs — late-decay logs have fewer but more exotic options.

#### 3.8.2 Spawn Roll

Once the eligible species list is filtered by host compatibility and decay stage, the game rolls for colonization:

1. **Primary species roll** — each eligible species rolls independently at its `base_spawn_chance`. Base chances are tuned so that common species (Oyster, Turkey Tail) appear frequently while rare species (Lion's Mane, Reishi) are genuinely uncommon finds.

| Species | Base Spawn Chance (per eligible log per year) |
| --- | --- |
| Turkey Tail | 55% |
| Oyster Mushroom | 40% |
| Chicken of the Woods | 15% |
| Hen of the Woods | 12% |
| Velvet Foot | 20% (eligible only fall/winter window) |
| Lion's Mane | 8% |
| Giant Puffball | 10% |
| Reishi | 6% |
| King Stropharia | 4% |

2. **Moisture modifier** — the tile's moisture level at epoch transition modifies all spawn chances: `actual_chance = base_chance × moisture_multiplier`. Moisture multipliers: Waterlogged ×0.6 (anaerobic, suppresses most fungi), Wet ×1.4, Moist ×1.2, Dry ×0.5, Bone dry ×0.1. Puffball is an exception — it prefers adjacent moist soil and does not roll the tile's own moisture.

3. **Co-colonization:** Multiple species can colonize the same log. Each rolls independently. There is no exclusion between species unless otherwise noted in species data. A log can host Turkey Tail brackets alongside an Oyster flush — this is realistic and rewarding for the player.

4. **Maximum species cap:** A single log can host at most 3 species simultaneously. If more than 3 pass their rolls, the extras are dropped (lowest base spawn chance dropped first).

#### 3.8.3 Yield Per Log

Each colonized species receives a `base_yield_grams` value determined at spawn time, scaled by log size:

| Tree Size at Felling | Yield Multiplier |
| --- | --- |
| Small | ×0.4 |
| Medium | ×0.8 |
| Large | ×1.0 (reference) |
| Very Large | ×1.5 |

Base yields (at Large tree reference) per fruiting season:

| Species | Base Yield (g, fresh weight) | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Turkey Tail | 120–200g | Dried weight much lower; mainly medicinal value |
| Oyster Mushroom | 400–900g | Highest caloric yield of common species; worth seeking |
| Chicken of the Woods | 600–1,400g | Exceptional yield when it appears; can carry a stew |
| Hen of the Woods | 500–1,000g | Large single fruitbody at base; calorie-dense |
| Velvet Foot | 150–350g | Small clusters; useful as late-season fresh food |
| Lion's Mane | 200–600g | Single large body; distinctive; excellent eating |
| Giant Puffball | 300–800g | Whole body edible when pure white inside; dramatic find |

Yields are rolled as a uniform random value within the stated range at spawn time, multiplied by the size modifier, and stored on the log tile object. The stored yield is consumed on harvest.

#### 3.8.4 Harvest Windows

Each species has a `fruiting_window` in its data object, defined using the same seasonal window notation as plant sub-stages (see Section 2.3a). Yield is driven by `advanceDay` exactly like a plant sub-stage: on the first day of a species' `fruiting_window`, `advanceDay` rolls a fresh `yield_current_grams` value (from the species' base yield range × log size multiplier) and makes the fungus harvestable. The player depletes `yield_current_grams` by harvesting. When the fruiting window closes, `yield_current_grams` resets to 0 — the fruitbodies are gone. The following year the window opens again and a new yield is rolled. Outside the window, the spawn is present but dormant — the log tile is visually marked as fungus-colonized but shows no harvestable body.

Representative fruiting windows for Indiana:

| Species | Fruiting Window |
| --- | --- |
| Chicken of the Woods | `early_summer` → `mid_fall` |
| Hen of the Woods | `mid_fall` → `late_fall` |
| Oyster Mushroom | `mid_fall` → `early_winter`; secondary flush `early_spring` → `mid_spring` |
| Lion's Mane | `early_fall` → `mid_fall` |
| Turkey Tail | `mid_spring` → `late_fall` |
| Reishi | `early_summer` → `late_summer` |
| Giant Puffball | `late_summer` → `mid_fall` |
| Velvet Foot | `late_fall` → `late_winter` (cold tolerant; active in freezing temps) |
| King Stropharia | `mid_spring` → `early_summer` |

**Multiple fruiting windows:** species with a secondary window (Oyster's spring flush) have two independent `fruiting_window` entries. Each rolls its own yield when it opens. A player who harvested the fall flush to zero can still find a fresh spring flush on the same log — the two windows are fully independent.

#### 3.8.6 Log Fungus Data Object

### 3.8.7 Ground Mushroom Zones — Data Specification

Ground mushrooms are a second fungus category distinct from log fungi. Rather than colonizing fallen logs, they fruit from persistent mycelial zones fixed in the soil. Zones are placed at map generation and remain in exactly the same tile locations for the entire run — they do not move, die out, or relocate between epochs. A player who finds a morel patch in epoch 1 can return to that same patch every spring for the rest of the game. This is the intended core experience: learning your spots over years.

#### 3.8.7a Zone Placement (Map Generation)

After the main plant pool and log fungus pool are established, a **ground fungus pool** of 4–7 species is drawn from the master ground fungus list and stored on game state as `run_ground_fungus_pool: ["id1", ...]`. Species are filtered for map compatibility: each species carries a `soil_requirements` object (see §3.8.7b) and must have at least one qualifying tile on the generated map (soil match score > 0 on at least one tile) to be eligible for the pool.

For each species in the pool, the map generator places **15–40 zones** (drawn from the species' `zone_count_range`) distributed across the map using a clustered random walk seeded from the map seed. Zones of the same species tend to appear in the same general regions rather than scattering uniformly, reflecting realistic mycelial territory — a map may have a dense morel corridor along an old-growth elm area and nothing elsewhere.

Each zone has a **center tile** and a **radius** of 2–5 tiles (drawn from the species' `zone_radius_range`). Every tile within that radius whose soil profile satisfies the species' `soil_requirements` (evaluated using the same soil match scoring as plants, §5.1b) becomes an active **fruit tile** for that zone. Tiles failing the soil match are silently skipped — a zone may have irregular edges where conditions drop off, and two zones of the same species can overlap if their radii intersect.

**Tile cap:** A tile can host at most one ground fungus species. If zone placement would assign a second species to an already-occupied tile, the second is skipped for that tile only.

**Permanence:** Ground fungus zones are written into the tile object at map generation and never modified by `advanceDay` or epoch skips. The zone is invisible outside its fruiting window and fully present within it — there is no colonisation, spread, or die-out mechanic.

#### 3.8.7b Soil Requirements

Each ground fungus species carries a `soil_requirements` object using the same continuous tolerance range format as plant `soil` fields (§5.1), so the existing soil match scoring in §5.1b applies without any new code:

```json
"soil_requirements": {
  "ph_range": [5.5, 7.0],
  "drainage": { "tolerance_range": [0.35, 0.85] },
  "fertility": { "tolerance_range": [0.2, 0.8] },
  "moisture": { "tolerance_range": [0.4, 0.75] },
  "shade": { "tolerance_range": [0.4, 1.0] }
}
```

This replaces the concept of a discrete habitat type. Species with broad tolerances (Russula, Destroying Angel) place successfully across many tiles; fussy species (Chanterelle, Morel) produce more irregular, sparse patches even within their zones.

#### 3.8.7c Fruiting

Each fruit tile in a zone rolls independently each year when its species' `fruiting_window` opens. `advanceDay` fires this roll on the window's first day:

1. **Fruit chance roll** — the tile rolls against the species' `annual_fruit_chance`. Tiles that fail produce nothing that year. A zone of 20 tiles at 40% fruit chance yields roughly 8 fruiting tiles in a typical year; in a dry year the moisture modifier may thin this further.

2. **Yield roll** — tiles that pass roll a `yield_current_grams` value from the species' `per_tile_yield_range`, modified by the standard moisture multiplier (Wet ×1.4, Moist ×1.2, Dry ×0.5, Bone dry ×0.1, Waterlogged ×0.6).

When `yield_current_grams` is 0 (either because the tile failed its fruit roll or the window is closed), the tile has no sprite and is indistinguishable from bare ground. When > 0, the species' tile sprite is shown and the tile is harvestable. The sprite disappears and `yield_current_grams` resets to 0 when the window closes. There is no "going bad" state — mushrooms either fruit or they don't, and harvested items follow the standard item decay path (converting to rotting mass if not eaten or dried in time).

#### 3.8.7d Identification & Inspection

Ground mushrooms use the same partner identification system as plants and log fungi. The partner can research an unknown species to unlock the full inspect panel. No additional identification tiers are used.

**Free examination:** The player can examine any adjacent mushroom tile (ground or log) for free (0 ticks) as a passive inspect. This reveals the `field_description` of the current sub-stage whether or not the species is identified. For unidentified species, the field description provides only visual and sensory cues — cap color, gill structure, smell, bruising reaction, spore color where relevant — with no edibility guidance. For species where field identification is genuinely ambiguous (Galerina vs. Velvet Foot being the hardest case), the identified field description calls this out explicitly and names the distinguishing feature.

**Spore color:** Where spore print color is a meaningful field ID feature, it is included as descriptive text in the `field_description` (e.g. "gills produce a rust-brown spore deposit"). No in-game mechanic for collecting or examining a spore print is required — this is reference information for players with real mycological knowledge, consistent with the game's real-knowledge-matters pillar.

#### 3.8.7e Toxicology

Ground mushrooms introduce four poison tags not present in the log fungus system. All are defined in the species' `ingestion` object using the standard `dose_response` schema (§5.2a); this section describes their gameplay behaviour.

**`amatoxin`** (*Amanita bisporigera*, *Galerina marginata*)

The most dangerous poison in the game. No antidote exists.

Timeline after ingestion:
- **Day 0 (ingestion day):** No symptoms. Poison absorbed silently. No nausea bar movement, no health change, no UI indicator. If consumed in stew, the responsible ingredient may not be obvious.
- **Day 1:** Still asymptomatic. Nausea accumulates internally but remains invisible to the player.
- **Day 2:** Severe nausea becomes visible — nausea bar spikes to the high band. Health bar begins draining at 15% per day. The nightly debrief reports the illness; the partner cannot identify the cause unless she has researched the species.
- **Day 3+:** Health drain accelerates to 25% per day. At standard health the affected tribe member dies within 1–2 days. No recovery path exists.

Design intent: the Destroying Angel is not a puzzle. It is a lesson. A player who loses a tribe member to it will not misidentify a white Amanita again.

Partner protection: if the partner has identified Destroying Angel or Galerina, she refuses to add either to the stew and flags any attempt to feed them to a child. She cannot prevent the player from eating either manually.

**`orellanine`** (*Cortinarius rubellus*)

A slower kidney toxin. Symptoms appear 5–8 days after ingestion as persistent health drain (8% per day) and a general fatigue debuff (−15 ticks/day). The delay makes the cause very hard to trace without prior knowledge of the species. The condition lasts 6–10 days and is not fatal at standard health if the tribe member is otherwise well, but creates compounding vulnerability. No antidote.

**`gyromitrin`** (*Gyromitra esculenta*, False Morel)

Raw or undercooked ingestion causes severe nausea (nausea bar jumps to high band) and a significant health hit (−20% health immediately). Not reliably fatal from a single exposure at standard health, but dangerous if eaten repeatedly or if health is already low. **Safe when properly dried and then cooked** — the `dried` + `cooked` preparation path produces an edible item with the toxin neutralised. This makes the False Morel a genuine knowledge-gated food: deadly for an uninformed player in spring, a useful calorie source for one who knows the preparation. The identified inspect panel explains this explicitly.

**`gi_irritant`** (*Omphalotes olearius* Jack-o'-Lantern, *Scleroderma citrinum* Earthball)

Severe gastrointestinal distress. Nausea bar spikes sharply; no lasting health damage in an otherwise healthy tribe member. Effectively a punishing nausea event that clears within 1–2 days. Informative rather than lethal — the player learns these species are not edible without fatal consequences.

**`psilocybin`** (*Psilocybe caerulipes*)

If consumed raw or in stew outside the partner-administered vision ritual (§11.3), the affected tribe member loses 20% of their tick budget for the day and the foraging UI displays mild visual scrambling (tile field descriptions occasionally show a neighbouring tile's text instead of the correct one). No health damage. The effect lasts until the following day start. #### 3.8.7g Ground Fungus Data Object

Ground fungi share the full part/sub-stage/ingestion schema with plants and log fungi. Top-level differences from plant objects:

- `type: "ground_fungus"` — distinguishes from plants and log fungi at the data level.
- No `dispersal`, `life_stages`, `growth_behavior`, `longevity`, `ph_effect_on_soil`, `host_trees`, or `preferred_decay_stages` fields.
- `soil_requirements` uses continuous tolerance ranges (identical format to plant `soil` fields) rather than enum arrays — this integrates directly with the existing soil match scoring in §5.1b.
- `zone_count_range` and `zone_radius_range` control map generation placement (§3.8.7a).
- `annual_fruit_chance` (float 0.0–1.0) is the per-tile per-year fruiting probability, modified by the moisture multiplier at roll time.
- `per_tile_yield_range` is the yield rolled per fruiting tile per year (grams), before moisture modifier.
- `fruiting_windows` is a top-level array of `{ start, end }` objects. Multiple entries are independent.
- Parts and sub-stages follow the normal plant part schema exactly. Typical ground fungus parts: `fruiting_body` (harvestable). No stalk/cap split needed unless the species has meaningfully different stalk and cap edibility — for most ground mushrooms a single `fruiting_body` part is sufficient.

**Toxic species — `ingestion` placement:** Toxic ground fungi carry their `ingestion` object on the `fruiting_body` sub-stage directly (sub-stage override), not at the top level. This is because the top-level `ingestion` is inherited by all parts — a species with `ingestion: null` at top level and a toxic sub-stage override is the cleanest pattern for fungi that have no safe parts at all.

**`cooking_detoxifies`:** Set to `true` on the `fruiting_body` sub-stage of *Gyromitra esculenta* only. For all amatoxin species, `cooking_detoxifies` is `false` — amatoxins are heat-stable and cooking does not reduce their toxicity. The data pipeline must not set `cooking_detoxifies: true` on any `amatoxin`-tagged species.

### 3.9 Epoch Plant Dispersal Simulation

The same simulation serves two purposes: **initial map generation** (running N virtual years before the player arrives) and **epoch skips** (running 5 virtual years between the player's active years). The code path is identical; only the input flags differ. This means there is no separate plant placement logic — the epoch 1 map is produced by the same system that evolves it.

**The simulation is `advanceDay` called in a loop.** The same `advanceDay(gameState, context)` function that fires once per in-game day during live gameplay is what the simulation calls repeatedly — 40 times per virtual year, for N or 5 years — with `context.rendering = false` and no player input. World events, weather, plant growth, seed germination, and terrain changes all run through this single function regardless of whether a player is present. This means:

- The epoch handoff state is exactly the same kind of state the live game uses: plant instances at their current sub-stage, dormant seeds in tile seed pools, logs at their decay stage. No translation layer or pruning pass needed.
- All plants — annuals and perennials alike — handle their full lifecycle via `advanceDay`. Sub-stage transitions, dormancy, flowering, seeding, and die-off all emerge from the same code the player sees. Annuals are distinguished only in that their instance is removed when their final sub-stage expires, freeing the sub-slot; perennials persist through dormancy.
- Annual succession works correctly without special handling. A spring ephemeral germinates, matures, sets seed, and dies within a single simulated spring. Its seeds enter the tile's dormant pool; a summer annual germinates into the freed sub-slot weeks later in the same simulated year. Two full annual lifecycles per tile per year emerge naturally.
- The epoch always begins at spring day 5 by definition — the simulation stops at that point and hands off state. Whatever sub-stage each plant is in on spring day 5 is what the player sees.
- World events — storms, beaver activity, floods — use identical logic in both contexts. During live play they are experienced as challenge events (§15.6); during simulation they fire as the same probability rolls in `advanceDay` with rendering off.

The simulation executes year-by-year, calling `advanceDay` for each of the 40 days within each virtual year. This captures compounding effects across years.

#### advanceDay Context Flags

| Flag | Live gameplay | Initial generation | Epoch skip |
|---|---|---|---|
| `rendering` | `true` | `false` | `false` |
| `player_seed_queue` | n/a (player acts in real time) | empty | player's scattered seeds from the active year |

#### Calendar-Triggered Events (fire inside advanceDay on fixed days each year)

Some processes have no meaningful daily resolution and fire once per year on a fixed calendar day. All fire inside `advanceDay` — the simulation calls the same code as live gameplay, just without rendering.

**Winter day 1 — log decay, fungus colonization, soil pH shift:**

- **Log decay:** each log and snag tile has its `decay_stage` incremented by 1. Logs at stage 4 with pole yield fully depleted and no remaining fungus yield crumble — the tile becomes bare ground and receives the disturbance flag.
- **Fungus colonization:** for each log and snag tile, filter `run_fungus_pool` to eligible species per §3.8.1 (host compatibility + decay stage). Run spawn rolls per §3.8.2 with moisture modifier. Species that pass are added to the log's `fungi` array if not already present and below the 3-species cap. Colonization is permanent once established.
- **Soil pH shift:** each plant instance with a non-zero `ph_effect_on_soil` modifies its tile's pH by ~0.02–0.05 units. Changes persist and affect germination match scores throughout the following year.
Winter day 1 remains ecologically accurate for these three annual maintenance passes — decay, fungal establishment, and pH drift all compound year over year.

#### Step 1 — advanceDay: Daily Growth Pass

Each call to `advanceDay` advances every plant instance and dormant seed pool on the map by one day. This runs identically during live gameplay and simulation; only `context.rendering` differs.

**Sub-stage advancement:** each plant instance checks whether its current sub-stage has expired based on its `seasonal_window.end` date. If so, it transitions to the next defined sub-stage. When a perennial's final sub-stage of the year ends, it enters dormancy and waits for the following spring. Annuals and biennials die when no valid life stage exists for the current day (see §5.1a Annual Death).

**Plant instance `age` field:** every plant instance carries an `age` integer (in-game days since germination) that `advanceDay` increments by 1 on every call, regardless of season or year boundaries. All age-dependent behavior — life stage transitions, seedling maturation, `seeding_window` activation — is driven by comparing `age` against the relevant thresholds in the plant's data. `age` is the single source of truth for where an instance is in its life.

**Seedling maturation:** each seedling instance checks whether `age >= life_stages[next_stage].min_age_days`. If so, it transitions to the next stage. Slow-growing species (trees, large shrubs) will still be seedlings at year-end and carry forward as seedlings into the following virtual year — a tree felled in epoch 1 could have a knee-high sapling on that tile by epoch 3. Because `age` accumulates continuously across year boundaries (in in-game days), a species with `min_age_days: 150` for its mature stage correctly requires ~3 simulated years to mature without any special cross-year logic.

**Dormant seed germination:** for each tile's `dormant_seeds` pool, for each seed entry, check whether the current simulated day falls within the species' `germination_season` window. If yes, run the germination check:

```
soil_match = pH_match × drainage_match × fertility_match × shade_match
germination_probability = germination_rate × soil_match
```

Apply the 2× disturbance bonus if the tile carries the disturbance flag and the species has `pioneer: true` or `requires_disturbance: true`. Roll per seed. Seeds that pass become seedling instances if a sub-slot is free; seeds that fail are discarded. If all sub-slots are occupied, seeds remain in the pool until a slot frees (from annual die-off or perennial removal earlier the same day or a later day in the same window). Seeds remaining in the pool after their `germination_season` window closes are discarded — dormancy is not indefinite.

**Overcrowding:** a tile's 4 sub-slots are finite. If all 4 are occupied, no new seedling can establish regardless of germination success. Dense established patches resist invasion naturally. A mature tree claims all 4 slots; when it is felled the slots free simultaneously, which is why a felled tree gap is such a strong germination opportunity.

**Seed production and dispersal:** when the current simulated day falls within a plant instance's `seeding_window`, it immediately produces and disperses seeds (see Step 2 below). Seeds land in target tiles' `dormant_seeds` pools. This replaces the former single yearly batch dispersal — seeds land when the plant actually sets them, and the simulated day's wind vector affects spread direction and radius. The `seeding_window` is a top-level plant field separate from the harvestable parts system — the plant reproduces biologically regardless of whether its parts have been harvested. Seed output is scaled by the plant's current `vitality` value (see §5.1a Seeding) — over-harvesting reduces vitality, which in turn reduces seed output.

#### Step 2 — Seed Dispersal (fires per-plant when current day is within seeding_window during Step 1)

Roll `seeds_per_mature_plant` for this plant instance, multiplied by the plant's current `vitality` (see §5.1a Seeding). For each seed, determine target tile based on `dispersal.method`:

**`gravity`** — random tile within `base_radius_tiles`, weighted toward nearer tiles (inverse-square falloff). If the simulated day's wind is High or Very High, add `wind_radius_bonus` to radius.

**`wind`** — same as gravity but `base_radius_tiles` is inherently larger (8–20 tiles) and direction is biased toward the simulated day's wind vector.

**`water`** — seeds from tiles adjacent to rivers or ponds enter the watercourse and deposit 1–30 tiles downstream, weighted toward nearer points. Seeds reaching the map border are lost.

**`animal_cached`** — seeds placed in clusters within a radius proportional to local squirrel/jay density. Cluster size 3–8 seeds. Radius scales up to 15 tiles at high density.

**`animal_eaten`** — seeds scatter randomly within the species' home range radius. Effective germination rate ×0.7.

**`explosive`** — 2–4 tile scatter, random direction per seed.

**`runner`** — no seeds placed. Instead, expand the plant's footprint: up to `base_radius_tiles` adjacent tiles are colonized vegetatively if soil conditions match. Vegetative expansion ignores `germination_rate`.

**Player-scattered seeds** (from `player_seed_queue`, epoch skip only) are placed directly into their target tiles' dormant pools at their exact positions, skipping dispersal logic entirely.

All dispersed seeds land in the target tile's `dormant_seeds` pool. Germination occurs during the `advanceDay` daily growth pass on the appropriate day of the current or a following virtual year.

#### Over-harvesting & Recovery

Over-harvesting consequences flow through the vitality system. A harvested plant's `vitality` drops incrementally on every harvest action for sub-stages with non-zero `harvest_damage` (see §5.1a). Lower vitality reduces seed output proportionally — a plant at vitality 0.3 produces roughly a third of normal seeds, and a plant killed before its `seeding_window` produces none. Population thinning over epochs is the natural downstream result.

- **Sustainable harvest** — plants survive with vitality intact, set seed normally, population holds steady or grows from seedling recruitment.
- **Heavy sustained harvest** — vitality declines year over year; fewer seeds each season; smaller dormant pools; smaller populations the following epochs. After 2–3 epochs of heavy pressure a species can locally disappear from a zone.
- **Cultivation** — player scatters seeds near camp into favorable soil, protects patches, fells trees to create light gaps. Desirable species densify around camp over epochs. By epoch 4–5, a well-managed foraging ground near camp is meaningfully more productive than the wild map average.
- **Recovery** — a zone left unharvested refills from surrounding dispersal. Short-lived annuals and fast perennials recover within one epoch; large trees are effectively permanent losses within the 25-year game window.

### 3.9a Species Pool Selection

At run start, before founder placement, a **run species pool** of 70 species is drawn from the master plant list and stored on the game state as `run_species_pool: ["id1", "id2", ...]`. Every system that references plants at runtime — founder placement, dispersal simulation, partner research queue, medicine treatment, hallucinogen candidates, viability validation — reads exclusively from this array. The master list is never consulted after pool selection.

**Selection is divided into three tiers:**

**Functional slots (~25 species):** drawn to satisfy the Tier 1 and Tier 2 viability guarantees defined in §3.3. For each functional requirement, one qualifying species is selected from all master list candidates that meet it, weighted toward species whose soil-match potential overlaps strongly with the generated terrain. Multiple requirements can be satisfied by the same species (a plant with both `fever_tea` and `antibacterial_poultice` treatment tags fills two slots). The goal is to guarantee function, not guarantee specific species — the nut species might be hickory in one run and black walnut in another.

**Biome-weighted slots (~30 species):** drawn from species appropriate to Indiana ecology, weighted by each species' soil tolerance overlap with the generated terrain. A wet map with extensive floodplain pulls more moisture-tolerant species here; a dry ridge-heavy map pulls more drought-tolerant species. These slots are what makes terrain shape feel meaningful — the land determines the palette.

**Wild card slots (~15 species):** random draws from the full master list with no biome weighting, subject only to a minimum viability check: at least some tiles on the map can support them (soil match score > 0 on at least one tile). These are the surprise species that create unusual runs and reward replay. A player might encounter plants they have never dealt with before and must reason through from physical clues.

The pool is serialized into game state and carries through save/load. Sharing a seed reproduces the same pool.

**Log fungus species pool:** Fungi are selected separately from the 70-species plant pool — they cannot participate in founder placement or soil-based dispersal, so including them in the main pool count would distort the balance of harvestable plants. Instead, after the main pool is selected, a **log fungus pool** of 6–8 species is drawn from the master log fungus list, filtered to only include species whose `host_trees` array contains at least one tree species present in the run's generated terrain. This ensures every fungus in the pool has a realistic chance of appearing on eligible logs. The log fungus pool is stored on game state: `run_fungus_pool: ["id1", ...]`. The winter day 1 fungus colonization roll in `advanceDay` reads exclusively from this array when rolling colonization candidates.

**Ground fungus pool:** After the log fungus pool is drawn, a separate **ground fungus pool** of 4–7 species is drawn from the master ground fungus list (§3.8.7). Each candidate species must have at least one tile on the generated map where its `soil_requirements` produce a soil match score > 0. Species that find no qualifying tiles are excluded. The selected pool is stored on game state: `run_ground_fungus_pool: ["id1", ...]`. Zone placement for ground fungi runs immediately after this draw, before the dispersal simulation begins, and writes zone tile objects directly into the map (§3.8.7a). Ground fungi do not participate in `advanceDay` dispersal at any point — their zones are fixed at generation.

**Difficulty interaction:** Hard mode draws more wild card slots and fewer functional slots (the viability guarantees still apply via fallback placement, but the player encounters more unknown species). Easy mode draws more functional slots weighted toward well-known foraging plants, reducing the proportion of wild cards.

### 3.9b Founder Placement

After the species pool is selected and before the dispersal simulation runs, founding populations are placed for each pool species. All founders are placed as **mature instances** (not seeds or seedlings) so they produce seeds immediately in simulation year 1.

**Qualifying tiles:** For each species, identify all tiles on the map where `soil_match_score > 0.4` (see §5.1b). Call this count `qualifying_tile_count`.

**Standard species** (seedling → mature `min_age_days` ≤ 25 in-game days, i.e. matures within ~5 in-game years):

- Place **1 founder per 300 qualifying tiles**, minimum 2.
- Example: a species with 3,000 qualifying tiles gets 10 founders; one with 600 qualifying tiles gets 2.
- These species can spread meaningfully across the map during the 25-year simulation from a sparse starting state, so low initial density is appropriate.

**Slow-maturing species** (seedling → mature `min_age_days` > 25 in-game days — primarily trees and large shrubs):

- Place **1 founder per 60 qualifying tiles**, minimum 5.
- Example: a species with 30,000 qualifying tiles gets 500 founders distributed across those qualifying tiles.
- These species cannot mature from seed within the 25-year simulation window, so the founder population must already represent a mature, established landscape. The denser seeding models the old-growth forest that would have predated the simulation window.

**Spacing:** Founders of the same species are placed with a minimum spacing of 10 tiles between them (prevents artificial clustering). If the target count cannot be reached while respecting spacing on the available qualifying tiles, place as many as spacing allows.

Founder placement for standard species produces a deliberately sparse starting state — isolated individuals whose descendants, not the founders themselves, produce the epoch 1 distribution. The simulation runs `advanceDay` 1,000 times (25 years × 40 days) before the player's first epoch begins. Slow-maturing species bypass this dynamic entirely: what the player sees at epoch 1 is the founder population itself, already mature and dispersing seeds.

### 3.9c World Events

Lightning, beaver activity, and floods are not simulation-only events — they are the same daily probability rolls that fire as challenge events during the player's year (§15.6), running inside `advanceDay` with `rendering = false` during generation and epoch skips. No separate simulation event system exists.

**Lightning** fires as part of storm resolution. A storm event that reaches the map has a probability of striking forested tiles proportional to severity. On a strike, the struck tree becomes a standing dead snag at decay stage 1 (§3.4a), adjacent dense canopy has a 25% chance of spreading the strike to 1d4 further trees, and all cleared tiles receive the disturbance flag. During the player year the player experiences the storm; during simulation the same tile mutations occur silently.

**Beaver activity** is a daily probability roll per active territory. Each territory has a small per-day chance of felling one nearby deciduous tree — expected total ~1–3 trees per year, distributed across days rather than batched. Dam formation triggers when `trees_felled_count` crosses its territory-specific threshold, which can happen on any day of the year. During the player year the player can observe fresh stumps and the dam growing; during simulation the same rolls fire silently.

**Floods** are the same event as the §15.6 flood challenge event. The flood rolls for each river section on a probability weighted toward spring, extends water-dispersed seed range to 60 tiles downstream, and raises riverbank moisture. During the player year the player experiences warning and ecological opportunity; during simulation the same tile mutations occur silently.

The compounding interaction between these events — lightning clearing → pioneer colonisation → beaver dam → moisture spread → flood dispersal — emerges identically in both contexts. Two maps with identical terrain seeds but different simulation histories can have substantially different plant geographies.

### 3.9d Dormant Seed Pool

Each tile carries a `dormant_seeds` array as part of its persistent tile state. This is the mechanism that bridges seed dispersal and germination for all seeds — whether produced by the simulation, scattered by the player, or dropped from inventory.

```json
"dormant_seeds": [
  { "species_id": "chenopodium_album", "age_days": 12 },
  { "species_id": "tragopogon_dubius",  "age_days": 3 }
]
```

Each tile holds at most one entry per species. The array is bounded by the number of species in the run pool — in practice a small number per tile.

**How seeds enter the pool:** any seed dispersed to a tile — by the simulation's Step 2 dispersal, by the player's Scatter Seeds action, or by dropping inventory items — lands in the pool rather than germinating immediately. If an entry for that species already exists on the tile, the incoming seed **replaces it**, resetting `age_days` to 0. This models fresher seeds crowding out older ones and caps memory cost to one entry per species per tile. A player who re-scatters seeds on a tile they already seeded simply refreshes the viability clock.

**How seeds leave the pool:** during each day of the daily growth pass (both simulation and live gameplay), each pool entry has its `age_days` incremented by 1, then is evaluated in order:
1. If `age_days` exceeds the species' `dispersal.viable_lifespan_days`, the entry is discarded — the seeds have died without germinating.
2. Otherwise, if the current day falls within the species' `germination_season` window and a sub-slot is available on the tile, a germination roll is made. Seeds that pass become seedling instances; seeds that fail remain in the pool and wait.
3. Seeds outside their `germination_season` window simply wait (age still increments).

Germination crowding: if no sub-slot is available on the tile when the germination roll would fire, the seeds remain in the pool and retry on subsequent days. They do not die immediately from crowding — but they will eventually exhaust their `viable_lifespan_days` if the tile never opens up.

**Multi-species conflict:** If multiple species in the pool pass their germination roll on the same day but only one sub-slot remains, one species is selected to germinate weighted by each passing species' `soil_match_score` at that moment — higher-scoring species are proportionally more likely to claim the slot. All others remain in the pool and retry on subsequent days.

**Player seed scatter** (§3.6) writes directly to this pool. The Scatter Seeds action places seeds into the target tile's `dormant_seeds` array; germination then happens at the appropriate season via the normal daily growth pass. A player scattering seeds in fall won't see results until spring — accurate, and rewarding seasonal investment.

**Visibility:** dormant seeds are not shown to the player. A tile with only dormant seeds looks like bare ground. The Nature Sight does not reveal dormant seeds (no caloric value yet). A player who knows their botany learns over time which bare patches are likely to produce what, just as a real forager would.

**Persistence:** the dormant seed pool persists through the nightly debrief, through epoch transitions, and through save/load. It is part of full game state serialization.

## 4. Environment, Soil & Weather

### 4.1 Soil Axes (Continuous Values Per Tile)

| Axis | Low End | High End | Driven By |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Drainage | Waterlogged | Bone dry | Elevation, water proximity |
| pH | Acidic | Alkaline | Base soil type; modified by plants over epochs (e.g. pine) |
| Nutrients / Nitrogen | Depleted | Rich | Floodplains richest; TBD full model |

### 4.2 Dynamic Soil Properties

- **Shade:** dynamically calculated from nearby plants using the active life stage `size` (1-10). Plants never shade their own tile. Size 1-2 stages cast no shade, size 3-5 affects adjacent tiles (range 1), and size 6-10 affects up to range 2 with distance falloff.
- **Moisture:** last rain event + tile drainage value. **Tile background color is derived from moisture in the rendering layer** — high moisture renders as deep forest green, low moisture as dusty tan, interpolated (see §20.2). This makes terrain character legible at a glance without any UI overlay.
- **pH:** shifts between epochs as plants modify soil

### 4.3 Wind & Scent System

Wind is a daily vector (direction + strength), influenced by season and weather events.

**Wind UI (diegetic, always visible while foraging):** Wind is shown as floating particles drifting across the screen in the current wind direction. Particle speed and density scale with wind strength. These are ambient — present in the background at all times — and serve as a passive orientation tool as much as a gameplay cue. They are not tied to any specific plant; they simply show the player which way the air is moving.

**Scent UI (toggleable overlay, foraging only):** A separate scent panel can be toggled on while foraging. It renders as a circle with a styled border, positioned as a HUD element. Inside the circle, scent particles from nearby plants bob up and down. The panel is a readable signal layer rather than a minimap; it shows particle density and type, not precise source locations.

**Scent particles:** Each particle is a small icon rendered on an 8×8 pixel grid.

- **Shape** is generated per plant by the AI data pipeline, loosely based on the real molecular structure of a primary scent compound from that plant — abstracted and simplified to fit 8×8 pixels, but with a family resemblance to the real compound's shape. The goal is visual distinctiveness and a hint of scientific authenticity, not accuracy.
- **Color** is derived from the plant's `plant_family` string: the family name is hashed to an integer, and that integer is used to select from a predefined palette of highly visible, game-safe colors. The palette is designed to avoid colors that are invisible on dark backgrounds, too similar to UI elements, or indistinguishable from terrain. All plants in the same family share the same particle color. This means a player who learns that alliums are blue-green will recognize a new allium particle before identifying the specific plant.
- **Mouseover on a particle** (while scent panel is open): if the source plant is identified, shows the plant name and current part/sub-stage. If unidentified, shows nothing — the particle is present but anonymous.

**Particle density** scales with patch size, plant `scent_strength`, and wind speed. A large dense patch upwind in strong wind floods the panel; a single plant in a light breeze produces a faint trickle.

**Scent data fields** (in plant JSON):
```json
"scent": {
  "strength": 0.2,
  "primary_compound": "linalool"
}
```
The `primary_compound` field is used by the AI pipeline to generate the 8×8 particle icon at plant data creation time. The icon is baked into the plant's object and can be rendered at will in the render engine.

**Useful for tracking:** wild garlic, ramps, flowering trees, fermenting fruit falls, and any plant with a high `scent_strength` value.

> ℹ️ Implementation: wind particles are a lightweight background system driven purely by the daily wind vector — no plant data required. The scent panel is a separate rendering layer that reads `scent_strength` and particle icons from plant data. Both are rendering-layer concerns and have no effect on game logic simulation.

### 4.4 Water Tile Types

- Flowing water: tile type shown visually, no physics simulation
- Still water: ponds, bogs
- Water depth: shallow / deep (affects fishing, movement)

### 4.5 Decay

Every harvested food item (plant parts, animal parts, sap, fish, fungi) has a continuous decay value from 0.0 to 1.0. This is displayed as a small progress bar on the inventory stack icon. Nothing changes functionally until the bar reaches 1.0 — no partial spoilage, no nutritional degradation, no UI warnings beyond the bar itself. The item's tooltip shows estimated time remaining until full spoilage at the current decay rate, so a very slowly decaying item with a half-full bar still communicates that it is safe for months.

**Decay timing** — decay and drying advance once per day at the day transition, not per gameplay tick. This keeps the simulation cheap regardless of stockpile size: a single pass over all stacks (inventory, stockpile, dropped items, drying rack) runs at end-of-day and applies that day's modifiers. There is no meaningful difference between placing something on the drying rack at the start of the day or the end — the decision that matters is whether it was on the rack at all when the day closed.

**Daily temperature** — a single temperature value is calculated for each day from the seasonal baseline curve plus the current variance offset (see §15.1). This value determines the day's temperature band and is used directly as the decay modifier for that day. All items everywhere — inventory, stockpile, dropped on the ground — use the same daily temperature. Items in the wigwam are not meaningfully warmer than items in the field, so no distinction is made. In winter this is to the player's benefit (cold outdoor temperatures slow decay for carried items too), which is an acceptable simplification.

**Daily sun value** — a single sun exposure value (0.0–1.0) is calculated for each day based on season and weather: a clear summer day = 1.0, overcast fall day ≈ 0.4, rainy day = 0.0. This drives drying progress for that day. Items on the drying rack receive the full daily sun value. Items left on the ground also receive the full sun value, unless it rained that day (rain resets ground drying progress entirely). Items in inventory or the stockpile receive 0 sun — drying requires exposure.

**Decay rate** is driven by `decay_days` defined per sub-stage in plant and animal data, representing days to full spoilage at baseline temperature (Mild band = 1.0× multiplier). The daily decay increment is `1.0 / decay_days` before modifiers. Examples of realistic `decay_days` values the AI pipeline should target: fresh berries 2–3 days, fresh meat 2–3 days, fresh fish 1–2 days, fresh sap 1–2 days (with additional temperature scaling; see §8.6), dried meat 60–90 days, dried berries 30–60 days, acorn flour 180 days, dry honey locust pods 365+ days, extracted locust beans 730+ days. Crafting intermediates (poles, cordage, dried hide, pitch, bone) do not decay.

**Temperature multipliers** scale the daily decay rate multiplicatively:

| Temperature band | Decay multiplier |
|---|---|
| Freezing | 0× — decay fully suspended; item freezes |
| Cold | 0.3× |
| Cool | 0.6× |
| Mild | 1.0× — baseline |
| Warm | 1.8× |
| Hot | 3.0× |

Frozen items are a valid preservation state — frozen meat and fish can be added to stews directly without further processing. Decay and freezing both pause during epoch skips (see §2.4).

**Drying modifier** — items that are actively drying (on the drying rack or in sunlight) accumulate a dryness value 0.0–1.0 in parallel with decay. Dryness scales the decay multiplier down: a fully dried item (dryness 1.0) decays at 0.03× its fresh rate regardless of temperature (except Freezing, which still suspends decay entirely). Intermediate dryness interpolates linearly — a half-dried berry is meaningfully more shelf-stable than a fresh one.

At dryness 1.0 two visual changes apply: the item name gains a **(dried)** suffix in all UI contexts, and the inventory sprite receives a full sepia tint. Both are binary threshold effects — nothing changes visually below 1.0, and both snap on simultaneously at exactly 1.0. If a fully dried stack is combined with a partially dried stack the weighted average dryness drops below 1.0 and both effects are immediately removed, correctly reflecting that the dry material has been diluted.

**Stack merging for dryness** — same weighted average formula as decay: `(dryness_a × qty_a + dryness_b × qty_b) / (qty_a + qty_b)`. Applied simultaneously with the decay merge. The tooltip shows the resulting dryness value and updated time-to-spoil.

Rain resets dryness progress on ground-laid items at day transition (a rainy day has sun value 0.0, and ground drying progress is reset to 0). Items on the drying rack are protected from rain. Drying advances 0 in Freezing temperatures — items freeze rather than dry.

**At decay 1.0** — the item converts to a `rotting_organic` stack occupying one inventory slot, regardless of what it was or how much was in the stack. No nutritional value; cannot be added to stew. The `rotting_organic` item has its own fixed decay timer (~2 in-game days, regardless of temperature — it disappears even if frozen, to avoid indefinite clutter). The player can drop it on any ground tile before it disappears; dropped `rotting_organic` applies a small soil fertility bonus to that tile and then vanishes on the same timer.

**Stack merging** — when two stacks of the same item are combined, the new stack's decay value is the weighted average of the two stacks by quantity: `(decay_a × qty_a + decay_b × qty_b) / (qty_a + qty_b)`. This is shown immediately in the tooltip.

**Non-food items** — tools, cordage, crafting intermediates, and equipment do not decay. The decay bar is never shown on these items.

## 5. Plant System

### 5.1 Plant Object Structure

Three distinct lifecycle concepts are modeled separately and should not be conflated:

| Concept | Where it lives | What it tracks |
|---|---|---|
| **Life stage** | `life_stages[]` | Whole-plant development: seedling → mature. Driven by the plant instance's `age` field. |
| **Part sub-stage** | `parts[].sub_stages[]` with `seasonal_window` | The changing state of a harvestable part within a season (e.g. pod: green → dry). Driven by calendar date. |
| **Seeding event** | `seeding_window` (top-level field) | When the plant reproduces. Drives dispersal. Separate from the parts system — the plant seeds biologically regardless of harvest. |

**Life stage names:** `seedling`, `mature` (all longevity types); `first_year`, `second_year` (biennials only, replacing `mature` — see §5.1a). The `seed` stage does not exist as a plant instance state — seeds are pool entries in `dormant_seeds[]`, not live plant instances.

**Life stage granularity — sprite pipeline and forager readability:** Each life stage maps directly to a distinct sprite in the sprite generation pipeline — one sprite per stage, per plant. Stages should therefore be defined wherever the plant looks meaningfully different at a glance, not just where its botanical lifecycle technically changes. The primary driver is forager utility: a player scanning the map should be able to tell at a glance whether a plant is likely to have flowers, fruit, ripe seed pods, or bare stems. If two phases look the same on a tile, they do not need separate stages; if they look different — and especially if that difference signals harvestable parts — they do.

Typical stage splits to consider (not all apply to every plant):

- **`seedling`** — young plant before it reaches harvestable size or reproductive maturity; visually small and leaf-only
- **`vegetative`** — full-sized but not yet flowering; leaves and stem structure dominant (use instead of `mature` when a distinct flowering stage follows)
- **`flowering`** — visible flowers present; signals the plant is in bloom and flower parts are available; visually distinct from vegetative state
- **`fruiting`** — flowers have dropped and fruit or berries are forming or ripe; plant silhouette changes with visible fruit clusters
- **`seed_set`** — fruit has matured into dry seed pods, cones, or similar; distinct color/texture shift from `fruiting`; seeds or pods are harvestable
- **`senescent`** — post-seed, dying back; bare or browning stems; signals the plant is done for the season but may still have harvestable roots or bark
- **`dormant`** — above-ground parts gone; only underground parts remain harvestable; no sprite shown on tile (rendering layer suppresses sprite when all active parts are below-ground)
- **`mature`** — use only when a plant does not go through visually distinct phases (e.g. an evergreen tree that looks the same year-round); do not use `mature` as a catch-all when finer stages apply

For biennials, `first_year` and `second_year` replace `mature` and may themselves be split further (e.g. a biennial that is vegetative in year 1 and flowering → fruiting in year 2 should have `first_year`, `second_year_flowering`, `second_year_fruiting`).

The data pipeline (see §5.5) should generate one sprite sheet entry per defined life stage. Stages with identical visual representations should be merged into one stage rather than duplicated. When in doubt, err toward more stages — a missed visual distinction is a missed forager cue.

**Parts note — processing outputs:** Parts that are produced by a processing step (e.g. acorn flour, chokecherry mash, pod husks) live in the same plant object as the source part. They have `available_life_stages: []` and no `seasonal_window` or harvest data — they are output-only entries referenced by name in the source sub-stage's `processing_options[].outputs` array.

**Parts note — tannin_level:** Oak and other high-tannin species carry a `tannin_level` float (0.0–1.0) on relevant sub-stages (typically the acorn or nut). This field initializes `tannin_remaining` in a placed Leaching Basket (see §8.4). The data pipeline should populate this from species accounts. Non-tannin species omit the field.

**Parts note — pitch_yield:** Pine, birch, and other pitch-producing species carry a `pitch_yield` int on their bark sub-stage, representing how many pitch units one processing run yields from that bark. The AI pipeline should populate this for relevant species; non-pitch species omit the field. Pitch is a crafting intermediate used for waterproofing and adhesive applications (see §8.3).

**Parts note — injury:** Two optional injury fields attach to plant data: `on_tile_entry_injury` at the top level of the plant object (tile-entry trigger) and `on_harvest_injury` on a part sub-stage (harvest-action trigger). Both use the same injury object shape. Most plants omit both fields entirely.

**Injury types:**
- `scratch` — instant small Health bar hit; has optional `infection_chance` (probability of triggering wound infection debuff if untreated after 1–2 days, same as the standard wound infection in §14.1)
- `sting` — instant Health bar hit; no infection chance; same flat-hit model as bee sting (§13.1); repeated stings on the same trip are independent rolls, not stacking
- `contact_rash` — no instant health hit; instead applies a `debuff` object: slow Health drain over several days, treated by partner with `antihistamine_poultice`. Rash does not stack — a second contact while the debuff is active resets duration but does not double the drain rate.

**Debuff object schema** (contact_rash only):
```json
"debuff": {
  "health_drain_per_day": 0.03,      // Float; Health bar lost per day while active
  "duration_days": [4, 7],           // Array of 2 ints [min, max]; rolled on application
  "treatment_tag": "antihistamine_poultice"  // Partner treatment tag; halves duration and drain rate
}
```

| Plant | Trigger | Type | `base_probability` | `health_hit` | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Honey locust | tile entry | `scratch` | 0.35 | 0.03 | Thorns on trunk and branches |
| Honey locust | harvest (green pod) | `scratch` | 0.25 | 0.02 | Reaching into thorny canopy |
| Hawthorn | tile entry | `scratch` | 0.45 | 0.04 | Dense thorns; higher base probability |
| Stinging nettle | tile entry | `sting` | 0.60 | 0.02 | Very reliable sting on contact |
| Stinging nettle | harvest (leaves) | `sting` | 0.70 | 0.02 | High probability bare-handed; gloves negate |
| Wild rose | tile entry | `scratch` | 0.30 | 0.02 | Canes at leg height |
| Poison ivy | tile entry | `contact_rash` | 0.50 | null | Coat halves; gloves negate hand exposure but not leg |
| Wild parsnip | harvest (leaves/stem) | `contact_rash` | 0.40 | null | UV-activated |

**Equipment modifiers guidance:**
- `"gloves": 0.0` — appropriate for any scratch or sting where hand/forearm contact is the primary mechanism. Use `0.2–0.4` for contact_rash on plants where legs and torso are also exposure vectors (poison ivy whole-body contact).
- `"coat": 0.5` — appropriate when thorns or stinging plants are at torso/arm height. Use `0.3` for very dense shrubs.

**Parts note — inner bark and cambium:** Many trees have two distinct bark layers worth modelling as separate parts: an outer bark (harvested as `bark_sheet`, used for structure and waterproofing) and an inner bark / cambium (edible in spring, and/or workable as `cordage_fiber` or `inner_bark_cloth`). ### 5.1a Plant Lifecycle Rules (advanceDay)

This section specifies what `advanceDay` does to living plant instances and dormant seed pools each day. All rules apply equally during the pre-game dispersal simulation and during live gameplay — no separate code path.

#### Age & Life Stage Advancement

Each living plant instance carries an `age` (int, in-game days since germination). Each day `advanceDay` increments `age` by 1.

`advanceDay` then checks whether the plant's current life stage is complete: if `age` meets or exceeds the `min_age_days` threshold of the next life stage AND the current in-game day falls within that stage's `seasonal_window` (if defined), the plant advances to the next stage. This check runs every day for every living plant instance — it is not deferred or batched.

After attempting stage advancement, `advanceDay` checks whether a valid life stage exists for the current day. If the plant's `age` meets the requirements for subsequent stages but the current day falls outside all remaining stages' seasonal windows, no valid stage exists and the plant dies immediately (instance removed, sub-slot freed). This is how annuals and biennials die: their life stages intentionally leave a gap (typically days 36-40 in late winter) with no valid stage.

On stage transition, the rendering layer checks whether all of the plant's parts in the new stage have `available_life_stages` that exclude any above-ground parts — if the plant has only underground parts in the new stage (e.g. a dormant perennial root), no sprite is shown on the tile.

#### Sub-stage Activation & Expiry

Each day, `advanceDay` updates the set of active sub-stages on the plant instance in two passes:

**Activation:** for each part defined on the plant's current life stage, `advanceDay` checks whether a sub-stage's `seasonal_window.start` day has been reached and the sub-stage is not already active on the instance. If so, it is added to the instance's active sub-stage list. Sub-stages are stored on the instance — they are not filtered at read time. The inspect panel and harvest system read only from this active list.

**Expiry:** `advanceDay` then removes any active sub-stage entries whose `seasonal_window.end` day has passed. Expired sub-stages are not retained in any form — no "depleted" or "closed" state lingers on the instance. The sub-stage simply ceases to exist until its `seasonal_window` opens again the following year (for perennials) or never (for annuals past their window). This keeps the plant instance clean and ensures the inspect panel never shows a sub-stage that is not currently active.

#### Seeding

Seed dispersal fires once per day for each mature plant whose `seeding_window` is currently active. Only plants at the `mature` life stage (or `second_year` for biennials) produce seeds. The seeding event fires regardless of whether the player has harvested any parts — the plant seeds biologically independent of harvest state.

Seed output scales with vitality: `seeds_per_mature_plant` is multiplied by the plant's current `vitality` value (0.0–1.0) before rolling. A healthy plant at vitality 1.0 produces the full seed range; a plant driven to vitality 0.3 by repeated harvest produces roughly a third as many seeds. A plant that dies from harvest damage before its `seeding_window` opens produces no seeds at all.

#### Soil Mismatch Death

Each day, `advanceDay` checks whether the tile's current `ph` falls within the species' `soil.ph_range` and whether the tile's `drainage` class matches `soil.drainage`. If pH is outside the species' range by more than 1.0 pH units, or if drainage has shifted to an incompatible class (e.g. a well-drainage species now sits on waterlogged soil), the plant dies immediately and its instance is removed. This handles long-term ecological drift across epochs as canopy, beavers, and floods alter tile conditions.

#### Perennial Old-Age Winter Mortality

Perennial background mortality is modeled as a winter-only stochastic risk rather than a single day cull.

- Applies only to perennial instances whose `age >= 2 × age_of_maturity`.
- During winter days (day 31-40), each eligible perennial rolls a small daily death chance.
- The daily chance is calibrated so total winter risk equals 5% per year:

`daily_winter_death_chance = 1 - (1 - 0.05)^(1/10)`

This amortizes mortality across the full winter season so losses are temporally distributed instead of clustering on one day, while preserving the intended yearly 5% old-age attrition signal.

#### Vitality & Harvest Damage

Each plant instance carries a `vitality` float (1.0 = healthy, 0.0 = dead). For any harvestable sub-stage with `harvest_damage > 0`, vitality loss is applied **per harvest action** rather than on final depletion.

Per harvest action, apply:

`vitality_loss_per_action = harvest_damage / actions_until_depleted_roll`

This treats `harvest_damage` as the total vitality budget for one full depletion cycle (one round of `actions_until_depleted`) while still giving immediate feedback with each action. For regrowing sub-stages, repeated depletion/regrowth cycles in the same seasonal window can stack additional vitality loss.

If `vitality` drops to 0.0 or below, the plant dies at end of day and its instance is removed.

Vitality recovers passively: `+0.1` per day during spring and summer for perennials; annuals do not recover vitality (their season is too short to matter). Vitality does not recover in winter or fall. Perennials that survive to the next growing season with reduced vitality will heal over time if left unharvested.

This creates meaningful but not punishing consequences: stripping every leaf from a nettle repeatedly will kill it within a season; harvesting pods from a honey locust has no vitality effect at all.

#### Sub-stage Regrowth

Some sub-stages (leafy greens, young shoots) regrow after harvest rather than depleting permanently for the season. This is controlled by two optional fields in the sub-stage schema:

- **`regrowth_days`** — int or null. If non-null, this sub-stage regrows after depletion. After `remaining_actions` hits 0, `advanceDay` keeps the entry in `active_sub_stages` and sets `regrowth_countdown` to this value on the instance entry. Each `advanceDay` call decrements the countdown. When it reaches 0, `remaining_actions` is restored to a fresh roll of `actions_until_depleted` and the sub-stage is harvestable again.
- **`regrowth_max_harvests`** — int or null. How many times this sub-stage can regrow within its `seasonal_window`. Tracked as `harvests_this_season` on the instance entry. When `harvests_this_season` reaches this cap, the next depletion is final: `regrowth_countdown` is not set and the entry is removed. (Vitality loss has already been applied incrementally per action throughout the season.)

`regrowth_countdown` does not decrement while outside the sub-stage's `seasonal_window`. If the window closes while a countdown is still running — the season ended before the plant finished regrowing — the entry is discarded. No additional lump vitality damage is applied at close.

Both fields are required on all sub-stages. Set to `null` for sub-stages that do not regrow (pods, fruit, bark, roots — anything where harvest is a one-time seasonal event). The data pipeline should set non-null values only for vegetative parts that realistically regrow: leaves, young shoots, and similar.

**Typical values for the data pipeline:**

| Part type | `regrowth_days` | `regrowth_max_harvests` |
|---|---|---|
| Leafy greens (lamb's quarters, nettles) | 6–8 | 3–4 |
| Young shoots (cattail shoot, elderberry tip) | 8–12 | 2–3 |
| Pods, fruit, nuts, bark, roots | `null` | `null` |

#### Annual Death

When `advanceDay` finds no valid life stage for the current day (age requirements met but current day outside all remaining stages' seasonal windows), the plant dies and is removed from tile state. The tile sub-slot is freed immediately.

Annuals achieve this by having their life stages cover only part of the year (typically days 1-35 or 1-38), leaving a gap in late winter (days 36-40 or 39-40) with no valid stage. This allows annuals to persist in a `senescent` stage after seeding completes, then naturally die when the gap is reached. The gap ensures annual death occurs once per year regardless of germination timing.

#### Perennial Dormancy

Perennials do not die at end of season. Their above-ground parts simply have fewer active sub-stages in winter (branches only for a tree, or nothing for a plant with only below-ground parts during the winter). The plant instance persists on the tile through winter. In spring, sub-stages with `seasonal_window` starting in spring become active again and the sprite reappears if it was below-ground only before.

#### Biennial Lifecycle

Biennials require two calendar years to complete their lifecycle. Runtime progression is age-driven, and life stages use special stage names with `min_age_days` thresholds:

- `"first_year"` — vegetative stage(s) in year 1. `min_age_days` typically 3-4 in-game days. Can be a single stage with `seasonal_window: null` (always active) or split into seasonal stages (e.g., `first_year_vegetative`, `first_year_dormant`). Parts available are vegetative: tap root, rosette leaves. No `seeding_window` fires.
- `"second_year"` — reproductive stage(s) in year 2. `min_age_days` typically 46+ in-game days (start of year 2). Can be a single stage with `seasonal_window: null` or split into seasonal stages. Flowering and seed parts become available. `seeding_window` fires normally.

Biennials die the same way as annuals: when no valid life stage exists for the current day. **Critical for year 1 survival:** If using seasonal stages in year 1, they must cover all 40 days to prevent premature death. If using a single `first_year` stage, set `seasonal_window: null`. In year 2, stages should leave a gap (typically days 36-40) to trigger death after seeding completes.

### 5.1b Soil Match Score

The `soil_match_score` is a float (0.0–1.0) computed per plant-species / tile pair. It is used in three places: founder placement eligibility (`score > 0.4`), germination rolls (`rand() < germination_rate × soil_match_score`), and the soil mismatch death check (which uses the raw pH distance rather than this score). A score of 0.0 means the species cannot establish on that tile at all.

The score is the **product of three independent component scores**, so a hard mismatch on any single axis zeroes the whole result:

```
soil_match_score = ph_score × drainage_score × shade_score
```

#### pH Score

```
tile_ph          = tile's current pH value
[low, high]      = species soil.ph_range

if tile_ph within [low, high]:
    ph_score = 1.0
elif distance outside range ≤ 1.0 pH units:
    ph_score = 1.0 − distance_outside   // linear falloff
else:
    ph_score = 0.0                       // hard cutoff; consistent with mismatch death threshold
```

#### Drainage Score

Each species defines `soil.drainage.tolerance_range = [min_ok, max_ok]` where tile drainage index is normalized to `[0.0, 1.0]` (`poor=0.0`, `moderate≈0.33`, `well≈0.67`, `excellent=1.0`). Drainage is a strict gate for germination.

```
if tile_drainage_index within [min_ok, max_ok]:
    drainage_score = 1.0
else:
    drainage_score = 0.0
```

#### Fertility Score

Each species defines `soil.fertility.tolerance_range = [min_ok, max_ok]`. Tile fertility is a float in `[0.0, 1.0]` derived from terrain and moisture. Fertility is also a strict gate for germination.

```
if tile_fertility within [min_ok, max_ok]:
    fertility_score = 1.0
else:
    fertility_score = 0.0
```

#### Moisture Score

Each species defines `soil.moisture.tolerance_range = [min_ok, max_ok]`. Tile moisture is a float in `[0.0, 1.0]` derived from water proximity and elevation. Moisture is also a strict gate for germination.

```
if tile_moisture within [min_ok, max_ok]:
    moisture_score = 1.0
else:
    moisture_score = 0.0
```

#### Shade Score

Each species defines `soil.shade.tolerance_range = [min_ok, max_ok]`. Tile shade is a float 0.0–1.0 derived from surrounding canopy. Shade has a strict gate for germination: if tile shade is outside `[min_ok, max_ok]`, germination chance is 0.

**Shade broadcast model (runtime):**

- Shade source is each plant instance's active life stage `size` (int 1-10).
- A plant does **not** apply shade to its own tile.
- Broadcast range by size:
  - size 1-2: no shade broadcast
  - size 3-5: range 1 (adjacent tiles)
  - size 6-10: range 2 (adjacent + second ring)
- Distance falloff for range-2 broadcast:
  - distance 1 weight = 1.0
  - distance 2 weight = 0.5
- Final tile shade is clamped to [0,1] after combining baseline shade and all broadcast contributions.
- For future shade-stress/death checks, very large trees (active stage size 9-10) treat their **own tile** shade as capped (recommended cap: 0.6 effective shade) so canopy peers do not unrealistically shade-kill one another.

```
if tile_shade within [min_ok, max_ok]:
    shade_score = 1.0
else:
    shade_score = 0.0
```

#### Notes

- The score is recomputed on demand each time it is needed (germination roll, founder placement) rather than cached, since tile pH, drainage/fertility, and shade can change between epochs.
- The score does not account for `requires_disturbance` or `pioneer` — those are applied as multipliers on top of the germination roll, after `soil_match_score` has already been applied.

### 5.1c Plant Instance Structure

A plant instance is a small runtime object. It is **not** a copy of the species schema — it holds only a reference to the schema and the fields that change during gameplay. `advanceDay` and all harvest/render logic look up static species data via `species_id` at the point of need.

```json
{
  "species_id": "honey_locust",
  "tile_x": 142,
  "tile_y": 87,
  "sub_slot": 2,
  "age": 47,
  "current_life_stage": "mature",
  "vitality": 0.8,
  "active_sub_stages": [
    {
      "part_name": "pod",
      "sub_stage_id": "green",
      "remaining_actions_ground": 1,
      "remaining_actions_elevated": 2,
      "regrowth_countdown": null,
      "harvests_this_season": 0
    }
  ]
}
```

**Field definitions:**

| Field | Type | Description |
|---|---|---|
| `species_id` | string | Foreign key into the static plant data. All biology, nutrition, descriptions, and dispersal data are looked up here. |
| `tile_x`, `tile_y` | int | Map position. |
| `sub_slot` | int (0–3) | Which sub-slot on the tile this instance occupies. |
| `age` | int | Days since germination. Drives life stage transitions. Incremented every `advanceDay` call regardless of season. |
| `current_life_stage` | string | Cached result of the life stage calculation — `seedling`, `mature`, `first_year`, or `second_year`. Updated whenever `age` crosses a stage threshold. Stored to avoid recalculating on every read. |
| `vitality` | float 0–1 | Plant health. Reduced by harvest damage; recovered passively in spring/summer for perennials. Scales seed output. At 0.0 the plant dies. |
| `active_sub_stages` | array | The sub-stages currently active on this instance. Managed by `advanceDay` (see §5.1a Sub-stage Activation & Expiry). Each entry carries `part_name`, `sub_stage_id`, and either `remaining_actions` (ground-level parts) or `remaining_actions_ground` + `remaining_actions_elevated` (elevated/canopy parts). |

**`active_sub_stages` entry fields:**

- **`remaining_actions_ground`** — how many harvest actions remain that are accessible without any tool (fallen material, drooping branches, low-hanging fruit). Initialized from `floor(actions_until_depleted_roll × ground_action_fraction)` when the sub-stage is activated. Present on all sub-stages where `reach_tier` is `"elevated"` or `"canopy"`; absent (the field does not exist) on `reach_tier: "ground"` sub-stages, which use `remaining_actions` instead.
- **`remaining_actions_elevated`** — how many harvest actions remain that require a stool (`"elevated"`) or ladder (`"canopy"`). Initialized from `actions_until_depleted_roll − remaining_actions_ground`. Decremented only when the player has the appropriate tool equipped/placed. Present on the same sub-stages as `remaining_actions_ground`.
- **`remaining_actions`** — used only on `reach_tier: "ground"` sub-stages (or sub-stages with no `reach_tier` field, i.e. non-tree parts). Initialized to a roll of the schema's `actions_until_depleted`. For elevated/canopy sub-stages, the UI derives a display total from `remaining_actions_ground + remaining_actions_elevated` but the two counters are tracked separately in state. When it reaches 0, behavior depends on `regrowth_days` (see §5.1a Sub-stage Regrowth).
- **`regrowth_countdown`** — int or null. When a regrowing sub-stage is depleted, set to the schema's `regrowth_days`. Decremented by `advanceDay` each day. When it reaches 0, `remaining_actions` (or both `remaining_actions_ground` and `remaining_actions_elevated`) is restored and the sub-stage is harvestable again. `null` when the sub-stage is not currently in a regrowth period.
- **`harvests_this_season`** — int. Counts how many times this sub-stage has been fully depleted and regrown within the current seasonal window. Compared against the schema's `regrowth_max_harvests` to determine whether another regrowth is allowed or final depletion fires. Reset to 0 when the sub-stage is re-activated at the start of a new seasonal window.
- **`harvest_pool_layout_tier`** — optional string (`ground` | `elevated` | `canopy`). Runtime cache: which reach tier was last used to split `remaining_actions_*` / `initial_actions_*` pools for this entry. When effective reach changes (e.g. tree leaf sub-stage moves from ground-reachable young growth to canopy-tier mature growth), the engine remaps remaining and initial action counts into the correct pools.

**What the instance does not contain:** any field from the species schema — names, descriptions, nutrition, soil preferences, dispersal config, life stage definitions, part definitions. These are never copied into the instance. If the species data changes (e.g. a nutrition value is revised), all live instances reflect the change automatically on the next read.

**Key principle:** the harvestable parts list is not fixed. Each plant defines its own parts and sub-stages in the data pipeline. The set of part types that exists in the game is the union of all parts defined across all plant files. Parts with an empty `available_life_stages` array are output-only — they are never harvested directly from the world, only produced by processing.

**`reach_tier` and `ground_action_fraction` for tree species:** All directly-harvested sub-stages on tree species require a `reach_tier` value. When setting `ground_action_fraction`, account for both physical reach from standing height *and* fallen or dropped material — the fraction represents everything accessible to a person standing on flat ground with no equipment. A high-canopy species may still have a non-trivial ground fraction in fall because of heavy dropping (e.g. black walnut). Note also that the same species can have very different fractions across sub-stages: a honey locust's green pods in summer cling tightly and rarely drop (`ground_action_fraction` ~0.15), while its dry pods in fall detach more easily and accumulate on the ground (`ground_action_fraction` ~0.4). Reference anchors for the data pipeline:

| Species | Sub-stage | `reach_tier` | `ground_action_fraction` | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pawpaw | ripe fruit | `"elevated"` | 0.7–0.8 | Small understory tree; fruit hangs at face height |
| Black walnut | ripe nut | `"canopy"` | 0.4–0.5 | Tall canopy tree but heavy dropper in fall |
| Shagbark hickory | ripe nut | `"canopy"` | 0.2–0.3 | Tall; drops less prolifically than walnut |
| Honey locust | green pod | `"elevated"` | 0.1–0.2 | Green pods cling to branches, rarely drop |
| Honey locust | dry pod | `"elevated"` | 0.35–0.45 | Dry pods detach more readily; some ground accumulation |
| Black cherry | ripe fruit | `"canopy"` | 0.1–0.2 | Fruit clustered near branch tips high up; minimal drop |

**Description fields on sub-stages** (required on all sub-stages including output-only parts):
- `field_description` — sensory and visual prose describing this sub-stage as a player would observe it. Always visible on inspect and in inventory tooltips regardless of identification status. Written as if the player is encountering it in the field.
- `game_description` — practical game information synthesized from this sub-stage's actual data: edibility, processing worth, storage, any hazards. Shown post-ID only in the inspect panel and inventory tooltip. The AI pipeline generates this from the sub-stage's stats rather than inventing it independently, so it stays accurate to the data.

**Description fields on life stages** (required on all life stage entries):
- `field_description` — visual description of the plant at this growth stage. Always visible. Does not mention parts that aren't yet present (no describing flowers on a seedling).

**Top-level `game_description`** — a post-ID overview of the whole plant: which parts are useful, when, any hazards, standout properties. Displayed in the inspect panel header once identified. Life stages inherit and display this text; part sub-stages have their own `game_description` instead.

**Underground parts** — some parts are below ground and invisible until dug out. These carry a `dig_ticks_to_discover` field (see §8.5). Above-ground parts on the same plant are always harvestable normally; underground parts require the dig action first regardless of whether the above-ground plant is visible. A single tile holds at most one plant, so discovery reveals at most one underground part per dig site.

**Processing options on sub-stages:** an optional `processing_options` array describes what processing methods are available for a sub-stage. If the sub-stage cannot be processed, omit this field entirely (no null values needed). Most sub-stages have 0-1 processing options; some may have multiple (e.g., acorns can be cracked OR ground whole, offering different outputs and time/tool tradeoffs).

Each processing option object contains:
- **`id`** — a short string label for the processing method (e.g. `"extract_beans"`, `"strip_fiber"`, `"scrape"`, `"crack_shell"`, `"grind_whole"`, `"grind"`). This identifies the action that produces the output parts. The unprocessed sub-stage has its own `edibility_score` and `edibility_harshness` reflecting its current state — a dry pod with hard embedded beans has low edibility and high harshness and will tank a stew (but still give the bean calories). The player learns through feedback (or real knowledge) that processing first is worth the ticks.
- **`ticks`** — how long this processing method takes.
- **`location`** — where this processing can be performed: `"hand"` (player or partner, anywhere — immediate action at tick cost for the player; queued task for the partner), `"camp"` (shared camp workspace, no special station required), or a specific station name (e.g. `"hide_frame"`, `"thread_spinner"`, `"mortar_pestle"`, `"leaching_basket"`).
- **`outputs`** — array of output objects defining what parts are produced by this processing method, and how much. All listed parts are added to the stockpile when processing completes. An empty array means the processing method produces no distinct output items — the part is consumed or transformed in place (rare). Output parts have `available_life_stages: []` and no `seasonal_window` — they exist only as processing products. This is also the mechanism for detoxification via processing: an output part simply carries `ingestion: null` or a reduced potency profile rather than inheriting the source sub-stage's pharmacology.

  Each entry in the outputs array is an object with one of two yield fields, plus `output_unit_weight_g`:
  - `{ "part": "beans", "yield_fraction": 0.35, "output_unit_weight_g": 1.4 }` — output quantity is a fraction of the input's total weight. The system computes `floor((input_weight_g × yield_fraction) / output_unit_weight_g)` to get the integer count added to the stack. Fractions across all outputs do not need to sum to 1.0; the remainder is implicit waste (moisture loss, discarded material).
  - `{ "part": "acorn_flour", "yield_grams": 400, "output_unit_weight_g": 1.0 }` — output quantity is fixed regardless of input mass. Used when the processing step adds water or otherwise produces a quantity not derived from input weight. Count = `floor(yield_grams / output_unit_weight_g)`.

  The two yield fields are mutually exclusive per entry. `output_unit_weight_g` is required on all entries and must match the `unit_weight_g` defined on the corresponding output part's sub-stage.

Processing by the player is an immediate action consuming the stated ticks from the player's budget — no queue, no waiting. The same processing can also be assigned to the partner and goes into the partner task queue. Camp work is therefore not partner-exclusive by default; the main partner-only queue category is research-style work. All output parts are added to the stockpile on completion.

**Shared processing contracts (no per-species duplication):** when multiple species use the same method, equipment, and action path, define that method once in shared sim/spec logic rather than repeating identical `processing_options` entries in every plant file. This follows the same principle as cordage generation below. Tree sap boiling is the canonical example: each sap sub-stage carries only `sap_profile` data (`is_tappable`, `sugar_percent`, `daily_yield_range_g`), while the actual boil method is the global `boil_sap` action at `sugar_boiling_station`.

**`craft_tags`:** an array of abstract material category strings that this sub-stage can satisfy in crafting recipes. Recipes reference tags rather than specific plants, so any sub-stage with the required tag is an acceptable ingredient. An empty array `[]` means the sub-stage has no craft utility beyond food or fiber. The field is required on all sub-stages; the data pipeline should populate it.

Tags are assigned at the sub-stage level, not the part level — the same plant part can have different tags at different life stages. A green willow shoot has `"flexible_shoot"` while a dead dry twig from the same plant would have `"stiff_stick"` instead. This reflects the real reason a material works for a given use, and ensures map variance doesn't strand a player: any plant that biologically fits the role satisfies the recipe.

**Canonical `craft_tags` vocabulary:**

| --- | --- | --- |
| `flexible_shoot` | Pliable green or young woody stem suitable for weaving or basketry | Willow green shoot, young dogwood stem, honeysuckle vine |

The data pipeline is responsible for assigning tags. Adding a new plant that produces a `flexible_shoot` sub-stage automatically makes it a valid basket material with no changes to any recipe data.

**Cordage and bark cloth generation:** The game generates cordage and bark cloth directly from parts tagged with `cordage_fiber` and `inner_bark_cloth`. The `inner_bark_cloth` tag is assigned by the data pipeline only to woody inner bark species — it never appears on herbaceous plants regardless of fiber quality. When a woody inner bark sub-stage carries both tags, the player can use that part to make either cordage (twisted/braided fiber) or bark cloth (pounded felted material). No separate processing outputs or intermediate items are needed in the plant JSON — the craft tags and fiber properties handle everything. Basswood and elm inner bark are the canonical sources with both tags.

**`stew_nutrition_factor`:** present on every part sub-stage. Defaults to 1.0 (neutral - full extraction, no cooking benefit) if omitted. This single field combines both extraction efficiency and calorie gain from cooking. Values <1.0 represent incomplete extraction, value =1.0 is neutral, and values >1.0 represent cooking benefits. Suggested defaults for the pipeline to work from:

| Part type | Factor | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tubers / starchy roots | 1.4 | Cooking breaks down starches significantly |
| Legumes / hard seeds | 1.3 | Boiling unlocks otherwise indigestible calories |
| Leaves and greens | 1.05 | Slight digestibility improvement only |
| Fruit / berries | 1.0 | Full extraction, no cooking benefit |
| Whole pods / unshelled nuts | 0.0–0.1 | Cannot be extracted without processing |
| Cracked acorns (still needing leaching) | 0.2–0.3 | Partial extraction, still needs processing |
| Fiber stems | 0.0 | Inedible even when cooked |
| Meat | 1.1–1.2 | Defined on animal part objects |

The factor is applied by the partner at camp during stew preparation. Food eaten raw in the field uses the base nutrition values modified by `raw_extraction_efficiency`.

**Raw extraction efficiency field:**

- **`raw_extraction_efficiency`** (float 0.0-1.0, optional) — fraction of nutrition extractable when eating raw in the field. Defaults to 1.0 (full extraction). Set to 0.0 for items that have calories but cannot be digested raw (whole dry beans like honey locust, unshelled nuts, hard seeds). Set to 0.1-0.3 for items that are technically chewable but yield almost nothing raw (tough roots, hard pods). Applied when player uses "Eat (field)" action. Example: honey locust beans have calories in their nutrition data but `raw_extraction_efficiency: 0.0` because they're indigestible raw.

**Additional stew cooking fields:** Two optional fields control what happens when a part is added to stew:

- **`cooking_detoxifies`** (bool, optional) — whether cooking in stew deactivates or weakens the plant's `ingestion` effects (pharmacology). Defaults to false. When true, cooking reduces or eliminates toxic/medicinal effects from the plant-level `ingestion` object. Implementation can reduce `potency_multiplier` to near-zero or skip ingestion effects entirely when this part is cooked. Separate from edibility — this affects pharmacological effects, not palatability.

- **`cooked_edibility_score`** (float 0.0-1.0 or null, optional) — edibility score when cooked in stew. If present, stew system uses this instead of raw `edibility_score`. Typically set on items that become more palatable when cooked (toxic greens, bitter roots).

- **`cooked_harshness`** (float 0.0-1.0 or null, optional) — harshness when cooked in stew. If present, stew system uses this instead of raw `edibility_harshness`. Typically lower than raw harshness for items that become milder when cooked.

**Container part nutrition:** Parts that produce other parts via `processing_outputs` should have nutrition equal to the combined total of all output parts. For example, a honey locust dry pod that produces beans (120 cal) + husk (0 cal) should have 120 cal total nutrition. This represents the actual nutritional content present in the item. `raw_extraction_efficiency` and `stew_nutrition_factor` separately determine what fraction is extractable (0.0 for whole pods in both cases, since they can't be digested without processing).

**Hallucinogen field schema:** Hallucinogenic properties are defined as an effect of type `hallucinogen` within a specific `dose_response` band inside `ingestion` — not as a top-level field on the plant or ingestion object. This keeps all dose-dependent behavior in one place: a plant's vision effect is only active when the accumulated dose falls within the band that contains the `hallucinogen` effect entry. For non-hallucinogenic plants, no `hallucinogen` effect entry appears anywhere in their `dose_response` bands. For non-pharmacological plants, `ingestion` is `null` at the top level.

### 5.2a Ingestion & Pharmacology System

The `ingestion` object models everything that happens to the player's body when a plant part is consumed, from therapeutic effects through toxicity and hallucinogenic response. It replaces the former separate `medicinal` and `hallucinogen` fields, which are unified here because they are the same underlying problem — dose-dependent physiological effects over time.

**Units:** all dose thresholds are stored internally in grams. The UI displays weights in ounces by default (toggleable to metric in settings). The game logic layer is unit-agnostic; conversion is purely presentational.

**Inheritance model:** the top-level plant object carries a canonical `ingestion` profile representing the plant's reference compound — typically its most pharmacologically active part. Sub-stages have two options:

- **`potency_multiplier`** — the sub-stage shares the same compound profile as the top-level but at a different concentration. A `potency_multiplier` of 3.0 means the dose thresholds are divided by 3 (you need one third as many grams to hit the same effect band). A multiplier of 0.1 means the part is nearly inert relative to the reference. `null` or absent means the sub-stage inherits the top-level profile unchanged.
- **`ingestion` override** — the sub-stage has a completely different compound profile from the reference (e.g. pokeweed young shoots are safe food; pokeweed root is a distinct and severe toxin). In this case the sub-stage carries its own full `ingestion` object, which completely replaces the top-level for that part. A sub-stage with `ingestion: null` is safe to eat regardless of the top-level plant profile.

This handles the common cases cleanly:
- Plant with one toxic profile at varying concentrations across parts → top-level `ingestion` + per-sub-stage `potency_multiplier`
- Plant with some safe parts and some toxic parts → safe parts have `ingestion: null`; toxic parts have their own `ingestion` object
- Plant with no pharmacological effect → top-level `ingestion: null`; sub-stage fields not needed

**Full ingestion object schema:**

```json
"ingestion": {
  "preparation": {
    "raw_usable": true,
    "processed_methods": ["poultice", "tea", "chew"],
    "partner_prep_required_for": ["tea"]
  },
  "pharmacokinetics": {
    "onset_ticks": 20,
    "peak_ticks": 60,
    "duration_ticks": 120,
    "processing_ticks": 240
  },
  "dose_response": [
    {
      "min_grams": 5,
      "max_grams": 30,
      "effects": [
        { "type": "medicinal", "target": "wound_healing", "modifier": 1.5 }
      ]
    },
    {
      "min_grams": 31,
      "max_grams": 80,
      "effects": [
        { "type": "nausea_immediate", "vomit_threshold_grams": 50 },
        { "type": "health_drain", "rate_per_day": 0.1 }
      ]
    },
    {
      "min_grams": 81,
      "max_grams": null,
      "effects": [
        { "type": "health_drain", "rate_per_day": 0.5 },
        { "type": "incapacitated", "duration_ticks": 200 }
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

**Field reference:**

`preparation.raw_usable` — whether eating the part without processing produces any effect. If `false`, raw consumption is inert (neither harmful nor therapeutic).

`preparation.processed_methods` — list of preparation types that activate or alter the ingestion profile. A poultice is applied externally; a tea is consumed; a chew is direct but minimal processing. All methods use the same dose thresholds defined in `dose_response`.

`preparation.partner_prep_required_for` — which methods require the partner to prepare (e.g. a properly dosed tea requires partner knowledge). The player can apply a field poultice alone. Attempting a partner-required preparation without the partner produces either an inert result or an uncontrolled dose. All hallucinogenic and medicinal preparations are partner-administered; the partner is assumed to have full knowledge of all medicinal and hallucinogenic plants in the area.

`pharmacokinetics.onset_ticks` — delay between consumption and first effects. A long onset (e.g. 60+ ticks) means the player may not connect symptoms to the cause, especially for unidentified plants.

`pharmacokinetics.peak_ticks` — ticks after consumption when effects are at maximum intensity.

`pharmacokinetics.duration_ticks` — how long active effects last from peak.

`pharmacokinetics.processing_ticks` — total time the substance remains in the body. If the player consumes additional doses before this window closes, doses accumulate. Accumulated dose is checked against `dose_response` bands continuously during the processing window. When the processing window expires (reaches zero), accumulated dose resets to zero — a fresh consumption after that starts a new independent window.

**Night boundary behavior:** Processing windows persist across the nightly debrief and continue into the following day. Each day has 400 total ticks (200 player budget + 200 representing night). When the player returns to camp and ends the day, the remaining ticks in any active processing window are assumed to elapse during the night. For example: a plant with `processing_ticks: 240` consumed at tick 190 has 150 ticks remaining when the day ends at tick 200. During the night, 200 additional ticks elapse (the non-action portion of the 400-tick day), which completes the processing window with 50 ticks to spare. The effect fully resolves before the next day begins. If a processing window extends beyond the night (e.g., `processing_ticks: 500`), the remaining ticks carry into the next day and continue counting down as the player acts.

**Cross-sub-stage accumulation:** when the player consumes multiple sub-stages of the same plant while a processing window is still open, all doses are normalized to "reference compound grams" before accumulating. Each sub-stage dose is multiplied by its `potency_multiplier` (defaulting to 1.0 if absent) before being added to the running total, which is then checked against the top-level `dose_response` bands. For example: 5g of young leaves (`potency_multiplier: 0.5`) contributes 2.5 reference grams; 10g of root (`potency_multiplier: 3.0`) contributes 30 reference grams — accumulated total 32.5 reference grams. The processing window extends to the maximum of the remaining window and the new sub-stage's `processing_ticks`.

`dose_response` — ordered list of dose bands. Each band has `min_grams` / `max_grams` (null max = no ceiling) and a list of effects active within that band. Bands should not overlap. Effects available:

| Effect type | Description |
| --- | --- |
| `medicinal` | Beneficial effect on a specific condition. `target` names the condition; `modifier` is a multiplier on healing rate or symptom suppression |
| `health_drain` | Ongoing health bar damage at `rate_per_day` (fraction of full bar) |
| `tick_loss` | Reduces next day's tick budget by `value` |
| `nausea_immediate` | Triggers acute nausea; `vomit_threshold_grams` is the dose at which vomiting is induced, which ejects stomach contents and reduces accumulated dose, protecting against higher bands |
| `incapacitated` | Player cannot act for `duration_ticks`; remains on current tile |
| `hallucinogen` | See hallucinogen sub-object below |

**`treatment_tag`** *(optional string or string array, medicinal bands only)* — present on any dose band that has a `medicinal` effect and can be used as a poultice or prepared medicine by the partner. The partner's auto-treatment logic (Section 14.3) scans this field across all plants in the stockpile to find a match against the active condition's `treatable_by` array (Section 14.1). A band without a `treatment_tag` has no treatment utility even if it contains a `medicinal` effect.

Canonical tag values (extend as needed during data pipeline):

| Tag | Treats |
| --- | --- |
| `antibacterial_poultice` | Wound infection, bee stings |
| `anti_inflammatory_poultice` | Sprain |
| `fever_tea` | Fever / flu |
| `tannin_tea` | Gut illness |
| `analgesic` | Pain / tick cost debuffs |

`hallucinogen` effect — used as an effect type within a `dose_response` band. When the player's accumulated dose falls within a band containing a `hallucinogen` effect, the vision system is triggered (subject to `partner_prep_required`). The effect entry contains a sub-object with these fields:

```json
{
  "type": "hallucinogen",
  "vision_categories": ["tech", "plant", "sight"],
  "sight_duration_days": 3,
  "debuff": {
    "type": "tick_loss",
    "value": 30,
    "duration_days": 1
  },
  "partner_prep_required": true
}
```

`vision_categories` lists which revelation types this substance can offer. `partner_prep_required: true` means visions only occur through partner administration — uncontrolled raw consumption at the hallucinogenic dose band produces the other `dose_response` effects (likely toxic) with no vision benefit.

**Example — a plant with a safe therapeutic dose and a toxic overdose:**

```json
"ingestion": {
  "preparation": {
    "raw_usable": true,
    "processed_methods": ["poultice", "tea"],
    "partner_prep_required_for": ["tea"]
  },
  "pharmacokinetics": {
    "onset_ticks": 15,
    "peak_ticks": 40,
    "duration_ticks": 80,
    "processing_ticks": 160
  },
  "dose_response": [
    {
      "min_grams": 5,
      "max_grams": 25,
      "treatment_tag": "anti_inflammatory_poultice",
      "effects": [
        { "type": "medicinal", "target": "inflammation", "modifier": 1.4 }
      ]
    },
    {
      "min_grams": 26,
      "max_grams": null,
      "effects": [
        { "type": "nausea_immediate", "vomit_threshold_grams": 35 },
        { "type": "health_drain", "rate_per_day": 0.2 }
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

**Example — a hallucinogenic plant where the vision dose and the toxic dose overlap (high risk):**

```json
"ingestion": {
  "preparation": {
    "raw_usable": true,
    "processed_methods": ["tea"],
    "partner_prep_required_for": ["tea"]
  },
  "pharmacokinetics": {
    "onset_ticks": 40,
    "peak_ticks": 120,
    "duration_ticks": 400,
    "processing_ticks": 800
  },
  "dose_response": [
    {
      "min_grams": 1,
      "max_grams": 15,
      "effects": [
        {
          "type": "hallucinogen",
          "vision_categories": ["tech", "plant"],
          "sight_duration_days": 5,
          "debuff": { "type": "incapacitated", "duration_ticks": 80 },
          "partner_prep_required": true
        },
        { "type": "health_drain", "rate_per_day": 0.15 }
      ]
    },
    {
      "min_grams": 16,
      "max_grams": null,
      "effects": [
        { "type": "health_drain", "rate_per_day": 1.0 },
        { "type": "incapacitated", "duration_ticks": 600 }
      ]
    }
  ]
}
```

Note that in this high-risk example, health drain accompanies the vision even at the therapeutic dose — the partner's role is to control the dose precisely and manage the experience, not to eliminate the risk entirely.

**Sub-stage potency example — same compound, different concentrations:**

A plant whose berries are mildly toxic but whose seeds (within the same berry) are severely toxic would have the berry sub-stage inherit the top-level `ingestion` profile with `potency_multiplier: 1.0`, while the seed sub-stage carries `potency_multiplier: 8.0` — meaning the dose thresholds for seeds are divided by 8, so a small number of seeds reaches the lethal band that would require a large quantity of berries.

### 5.2b Data Schema Reference for AI Generation

This section documents the controlled vocabularies and constraints for fields that interact with gameplay systems. Fields not listed here (e.g., `plant_family`, `taste_notes`, `scent_notes`) are open-ended and inferred from the plant's botanical properties.

#### Habitat & Terrain Enums (animals/fish)

**`habitat` (animals/fish only)** — array of terrain types where the species naturally occurs:
- `"forest"` — mature closed-canopy woodland
- `"forest_edge"` — transition zone between forest and open ground; highest biodiversity
- `"meadow"` — open grassland
- `"floodplain"` — low-lying area subject to periodic flooding; rich soil
- `"wetland"` — saturated soil, standing water seasonally
- `"bog"` — acidic wetland with poor drainage
- `"scrubland"` — shrubby vegetation, dry conditions
- `"riparian"` — streamside or riverbank
- `"upland_ridge"` — dry elevated terrain
- `"disturbed_ground"` — recently cleared or heavily impacted areas
- `"pond"` — still freshwater (fish only)
- `"slow_river"` — gentle current (fish only)
- `"fast_river"` — strong current (fish only)
- `"stream"` — small watercourse (fish only)

#### Soil & Growth Fields

**`soil.drainage.tolerance_range`** — tolerated tile drainage index range:
- Array `[min, max]` with both values in `[0.0, 1.0]`
- Tile drainage index mapping: `poor=0.0`, `moderate≈0.33`, `well≈0.67`, `excellent=1.0`
- Germination is a strict gate: outside range means germination probability is 0

**`soil.fertility.tolerance_range`** — tolerated tile fertility range:
- Array `[min, max]` with both values in `[0.0, 1.0]`
- `min` = minimum tolerated tile fertility
- `max` = maximum tolerated tile fertility
- Germination is a strict gate: outside range means germination probability is 0

**`soil.moisture.tolerance_range`** — tolerated tile moisture range:
- Array `[min, max]` with both values in `[0.0, 1.0]`
- `min` = minimum tolerated tile moisture
- `max` = maximum tolerated tile moisture
- Germination is a strict gate: outside range means germination probability is 0

**`soil.shade.tolerance_range`** — light requirements:
- Array `[min, max]` with both values in `[0.0, 1.0]`
- `min` = minimum tolerated tile shade
- `max` = maximum tolerated tile shade
- Germination is a strict gate: outside range means germination probability is 0

**`growth_behavior`** — spatial distribution pattern:
- `"solitary"` — individual plants spaced apart
- `"clustered"` — grows in tight groups
- `"colonial"` — spreads vegetatively to form large patches
- `"runner"` — spreads via rhizomes or stolons

**`longevity`** — lifespan category:
- `"annual"` — completes lifecycle in one year; dies after seeding window closes; plant instance is removed from tile state at end of that day
- `"biennial"` — two-year lifecycle; year 1 is vegetative only (`first_year` stage), year 2 flowers, seeds, then dies (`second_year` stage); see §5.1a for biennial lifecycle rules
- `"perennial"` — lives multiple years; above-ground parts die back in winter but the plant instance persists; seeding window recurs annually

#### Dispersal Enums

**`dispersal.method`** — primary seed dispersal mechanism:
- `"gravity"` — seeds drop near parent
- `"wind"` — light seeds carried by air
- `"water"` — seeds float downstream
- `"animal_cached"` — squirrels/jays bury seeds (clustered distribution)
- `"animal_eaten"` — gut-passed, random scatter
- `"explosive"` — plant forcibly ejects seeds
- `"runner"` — vegetative spread, not seeds

**`dispersal.germination_season`** — when dormant seeds wake:
- `"spring"` — stratification-requiring species
- `"early_summer"` — warm-soil germinators
- `"late_summer"` — fall-seeding species

#### Processing & Material Enums

**`processing_location`** — where processing can occur:
- `"hand"` — player or partner, anywhere (immediate for player, queued for partner)
- `"camp"` — shared camp workspace (immediate for player, queueable for partner), no special station required
- `"hide_frame"` — requires Hide Frame station
- `"thread_spinner"` — requires Thread Spinner station
- `"mortar_pestle"` — requires Mortar & Pestle station (see §7.2); used for grinding seeds, nuts, and dried plant material into flour or meal
- `"leaching_basket"` — requires Leaching Basket tool in inventory; player places basket on a water tile and retrieves later (see §8.4)

Runtime processing uses a `process_item` action that resolves processing rules from the source part/sub-stage's `processing_options`. `ticks` on the processing option become action tick cost, and `location` is enforced at validation time (hand/camp/station requirement).

**`decay_rate`** — how quickly harvested items spoil:
- `"slow"` — nuts, hard seeds
- `"moderate"` — tubers, roots
- `"fast"` — leafy greens, fresh meat
- `"very_fast"` — delicate berries, fish

**`craft_tags`** — canonical vocabulary (see Section 5.2 for full descriptions):
- `"flexible_shoot"` — basketry, weaving
- `"weaving_material"` — flat pliable leaves/stems
- `"reedy_material"` — reed-like weaving material (legacy `weaving_material` and `sun_hat_reed_material` accepted)
- `"bark_sheet"` — waterproofing, structure
- `"cordage_fiber"` — cordage production (game generates cordage from fiber properties)
- `"inner_bark_cloth"` — coat shell material (can appear with `cordage_fiber` on same part)
- `"stiff_stick"` — structural, traps
- `"tinder"` — fire starting
- `"resin"` — waterproofing, adhesive
- `"insulation_material"` — coat filling
- `"large_leaf"` — wrapping, padding

#### Animal Behavior Enums

**`animal_class`** — taxonomic category:
- `"mammal"` — warm-blooded, fur/hair
- `"fish"` — aquatic, gills
- `"bird"` — feathered, flight
- `"reptile"` — cold-blooded, scales
- `"amphibian"` — dual-phase lifecycle

**`behaviors`** — array of behavioral traits:
- `"ground_dwelling"` — lives on ground level
- `"trail_runner"` — uses consistent paths (improves trap placement)
- `"crepuscular"` — active dawn/dusk
- `"nocturnal"` — active at night
- `"diurnal"` — active during day
- `"arboreal"` — tree-dwelling
- `"burrowing"` — digs dens/tunnels
- `"schooling"` — fish that group together
- `"bottom_feeder"` — feeds on substrate
- `"surface_feeder"` — feeds at water surface
- `"territorial"` — defends specific area

**`nausea_family`** — monotony grouping for animals:
- `"lean_game"` — rabbit, squirrel, beaver
- `"game_bird"` — turkey, grouse
- `"waterfowl"` — duck, goose
- `"fish"` — all fish species
- `"honey"` — bee products
- `"insect"` — larvae, grubs

#### Pharmacological Enums

**`preparation.processed_methods`** — how plant can be prepared:
- `"poultice"` — external application
- `"tea"` — steeped/boiled infusion
- `"chew"` — direct mastication
- `"tincture"` — alcohol extraction (future)

**`dose_response` effect types** — physiological effects:
- `"medicinal"` — treats a condition (requires `target` and `modifier`)
- `"health_drain"` — damages health bar at `rate_per_day`
- `"tick_loss"` — reduces next day's tick budget by `value`
- `"nausea_immediate"` — triggers vomiting at `vomit_threshold_grams`
- `"incapacitated"` — player cannot act for `duration_ticks`
- `"hallucinogen"` — triggers vision system (requires sub-object)

**`treatment_tag`** — medical application (medicinal effects only):
- `"antibacterial_poultice"` — treats wound infection, bee stings
- `"anti_inflammatory_poultice"` — treats sprains
- `"fever_tea"` — treats fever/flu
- `"tannin_tea"` — treats gut illness
- `"analgesic"` — treats pain/tick debuffs
- `"antihistamine_poultice"` — treats contact rash (poison ivy, wild parsnip); halves duration and health drain rate. Applied by partner. Relevant plants: jewelweed, plantain.

#### Numeric Field Constraints

**Plants:**
- `soil.ph_range` — array of 2 floats, valid range [3.0, 9.0]
- `dispersal.base_radius_tiles` — int, typical range 1-30
- `dispersal.germination_rate` — float 0.0-1.0
- `dispersal.viable_lifespan_days` — int, days a dormant seed survives before being discarded; required on all species; typical range 60–1800+
- `fiber_strength_modifier` — float, typical range 0.1–2.0. Combined with `average_fiber_length_cm` via the `cordage_quality` formula (see §10.2) to determine the quality tier of cordage twisted from this part. Set to 0 on non-fibrous sub-stages.
- `age_of_maturity` — int, required on all plants; must exactly match the `min_age_days` of the first non-seedling life stage. Defines when harvest yields reach 100% of specified values. Plants younger than this age have yields scaled proportionally.
- `on_tile_entry_injury` — object or null; top-level plant field; optional, omit on non-hazardous plants. Fires every time the player moves onto a tile containing this plant at any life stage. Fields: `type` (enum: `scratch` | `sting` | `contact_rash`), `base_probability` (float 0.0–1.0), `health_hit` (float; null for `contact_rash`), `infection_chance` (optional float; scratch only), `debuff` (object; contact_rash only; null otherwise — see Parts note — injury), `equipment_probability_modifiers` (object; equipment slot id → float multiplier; `0.0` = negated, e.g. `{"gloves": 0.0, "coat": 0.5}`). See Parts note — injury for full debuff schema and representative values.
- `edibility_score` — float 0.0-1.0 (≥0.85 = field-edible)
- `edibility_harshness` — float 0.0-1.0 (affects nausea accumulation)
- `stew_nutrition_factor` — float, typical range 0.0-1.4
- `harvest_damage` — float 0.0-1.0 per sub-stage; how much vitality the plant instance loses when this sub-stage is fully depleted by harvest. 0.0 = no harm (e.g. pods, nuts, fruit — removing them does not stress the plant). 0.3–0.5 = moderate stress (e.g. bark stripping, heavy leaf harvest). 1.0 = lethal if fully depleted (e.g. entire root system, complete defoliation of a delicate annual). Required on all directly-harvested sub-stages; `null` on output-only parts. See §5.1a for vitality rules.
- `harvest_yield.units_per_action` — array of 2 ints `[min, max]`; defines the per-action yield roll range. At runtime the engine uses the catalog **midpoint** `(min + max) / 2` (rounded), then scales it by plant age (see `harvest_yield_full_age_days` below) for **full** yield at the reference age. Required on all directly-harvested sub-stages; `null` on output-only parts (`available_life_stages: []`). **Design intent:** author `harvest_yield` for the mature / full-strength case; immature plants get lower per-action units automatically via scaling unless `harvest_yield_full_age_days` overrides the reference.
- `harvest_yield.actions_until_depleted` — array of 2 ints `[min, max]`; the engine uses the catalog **midpoint** as the full-strength seasonal action cap, then scales it by the same age factor (minimum scale 0.1) before splitting across ground/elevated/canopy pools. Required on all directly-harvested sub-stages; `null` on output-only parts.
- `harvest_yield_full_age_days` — optional int on the sub-stage (JSON snake_case; catalog may expose `harvestYieldFullAgeDays`). Age in days at which this sub-stage’s catalog `harvest_yield` is at **full** strength (scale 1). `0` means always full strength. Omit to use the species `age_of_maturity`. There is no per-day global rescan: pools resync when harvest validation or apply runs, using current `plant.age`, with proportional remap when the scaled cap changes.
- `harvest_unit_weight_scales_with_age` — optional bool on the sub-stage (JSON snake_case; catalog may expose `harvestUnitWeightScalesWithAge`). Default `false`. When `true`, harvested inventory mass per unit uses catalog `unit_weight_g` as the **full-maturity** per-unit weight, multiplied by the same age scale as harvest yield (minimum scale 0.1). Omit on parts where immature mass should not differ (e.g. individual berries, loose leaves) or where yield is already expressed only through `units_per_action`. Squirrel caches and other non-plant-harvest sources keep catalog full weight. Camp stew preview scales plant-part nutrition by actual stockpile stack mass vs catalog so underweight units reduce calories proportionally.
- `harvest_yield.ground_action_fraction` — float 0.0–1.0; required on all sub-stages where **effective** reach tier (after `reach_tier_by_life_stage`, below) is `"elevated"` or `"canopy"`; omitted when effective reach is `"ground"` and on all non-tree parts. Represents the fraction of total actions accessible from the ground without any tool — accounting for both physical reach and fallen/dropped material. Pool initialization uses the **age-scaled** action cap for the cycle: `remaining_actions_ground = floor(scaled_cap × ground_action_fraction)` and `remaining_actions_elevated = scaled_cap − remaining_actions_ground` (canopy uses the three-pool split rules already in §5.1a). A value of `0.0` means the entire yield is tool-gated; a value of `1.0` is equivalent to `reach_tier: "ground"` and should be flagged as a data error.
- `reach_tier` — string enum; required on all directly-harvested sub-stages of tree species (typically species whose mature life stage size is in the canopy band, usually `size >= 7`). Omitted on all non-tree, underground, and output-only parts. Values: `"ground"` (accessible without any tool), `"elevated"` (requires stool for full access), `"canopy"` (requires ladder for full access). See §8 harvest gating rules.
- `reach_tier_by_life_stage` — optional object keyed by life stage name; values are `ground` | `elevated` | `canopy`. When present, the key for the instance's current life stage **overrides** `reach_tier` for harvest validation and for how action pools are split. Example: a tree whose mature leaves are `canopy` may set `seedling` and `sapling` to `ground` so young foliage is harvestable without a ladder while reusing the same `leaf` sub-stage and part sprites.
- `unit_weight_g` — float, gram weight of one discrete unit of this sub-stage. Required on all sub-stages including output-only parts. Set to a natural per-item weight for countable things (a pod, a berry, a bean); set to `1.0` for bulk material where weight and quantity are the same (loose leaves, ground flour, fiber). Must match `output_unit_weight_g` on any output entry within `processing_options` that references this part.
- `does_blickey_help_harvest` — bool; optional, defaults to `false` if omitted. Content flag for fruit, berry, and small-seed sub-stages where a blickey is appropriate. It does **not** apply a direct yield multiplier. Any blickey gameplay effect is time-based through `harvest_tool_modifiers.blickey` when present on the sub-stage.
- `can_squirrel_cache` — bool; optional, defaults to `false` if omitted. Set to `true` on the specific sub-stage that squirrels would actually cache — the form they store, whether that is a directly-harvested part or a processing output (e.g. honey locust extracted beans, a walnut's shelled nut). The squirrel caching system reads this flag to determine which plant parts are eligible to appear in squirrel cache tiles (see §13.1).
- `on_harvest_injury` — object or null; optional, omit on non-hazardous parts. Fires once per harvest action on this sub-stage. Fields: `type` (enum: `scratch` | `sting` | `contact_rash`), `base_probability` (float 0.0–1.0), `health_hit` (float; instant Health bar damage; null for `contact_rash`), `infection_chance` (optional float; scratch only), `debuff` (object; contact_rash only; null otherwise — see Parts note — injury), `tool_probability_modifiers` (object; tool id → float multiplier on base_probability; `0.0` = negated). See Parts note — injury for full debuff schema and representative values.
- `dig_ticks_to_discover` — int, baseline ticks of digging required to discover this part before the harvest action becomes available. Present only on underground parts. Scaled by tool and temperature modifiers at runtime (see §8.5). Omitted on all above-ground parts.
- `decay_days` — float, days to full spoilage at Mild (1.0×) temperature. Required on all food sub-stages including output-only parts. Omitted on non-food parts (fiber, craft materials). See §4.5 for full decay spec and example values.

**Animals:**
- `weight_range_g` — array of 2 ints [min, max] in grams
- `base_catch_rate` — float 0.0-1.0 (daily trap success probability)
- `current_sensitivity` — float 0.0-1.0 (fish only; 0 = current-tolerant)
- `population.starting_density` — float 0.0-1.0
- `population.density_per_catch` — negative float (population impact per catch)
- `population.daily_recovery` — positive float (natural recovery rate)
- `population.season_modifiers` — object with keys `["spring", "summer", "fall", "winter"]`, values 0.0-2.0

#### Schema Validation Rules

**Required field combinations:**
- Fish must have `water_required: true` and `habitat` containing water types (`pond`, `slow_river`, `fast_river`, `stream`)
- Fish must have `current_sensitivity` field (0.0-1.0)
- Land animals must have `water_required: false`
- If `processing_options` exists, it must be a non-empty array; each option must have all four fields: `id`, `ticks`, `location`, `outputs`
- Any `processing_options.outputs[].itemId` must resolve to a known canonical item ID in the item registry (hard validation failure on unknown IDs)
- All directly-harvested animal parts must have `yield_grams` (with optional `yield_quantity` for countable items like bones); output-only parts (those with `available_life_stages: []`) omit `yield_grams`
- All sub-stages (plant and animal, including output-only parts) must have `unit_weight_g`; set to `1.0` for bulk material
- `output_unit_weight_g` on each output entry within `processing_options` must match `unit_weight_g` on the referenced output part's sub-stage
- `dig_ticks_to_discover` must be present on all sub-stages of underground parts and must be omitted on all above-ground parts
- `reach_tier` must be present on all directly-harvested sub-stages of tree species and must be omitted on all non-tree, underground, and output-only parts. For each life stage the plant can occupy, **effective** reach tier is `reach_tier_by_life_stage[stage]` when defined, otherwise `reach_tier`. `"elevated"` and `"canopy"` effective tiers must have `harvest_yield.ground_action_fraction` (including when only the mature stage is elevated/canopy and young stages override to `ground`). `"ground"` effective tier must not include `ground_action_fraction` on that sub-stage's base `harvest_yield`. A `ground_action_fraction` of `1.0` is a validation error.
- Keys in `reach_tier_by_life_stage` must match `life_stages[].stage` values for that species (no invented stage names).
- All stage names used in `available_life_stages` arrays must exactly match stage names defined in the plant's `life_stages` array. Do not use modified or prefixed versions of stage names.
- If a part's `game_description` or `field_description` mentions seasonal changes in properties (edibility, texture, toxicity, etc.), the part must have multiple sub-stages with different `seasonal_window` ranges to model those changes.
- **Sub-stage inheritance:** The first sub-stage in a part's `sub_stages` array must be fully specified with all required fields. Subsequent sub-stages automatically inherit all properties from the first sub-stage except those explicitly overridden. Always override: `id`, `seasonal_window`, `field_description`, `game_description`. Override other fields only when they actually differ from the first sub-stage. This makes seasonal variations trivial to define.
- `remaining_actions_ground` and `remaining_actions_elevated` are used (replacing `remaining_actions`) on all active sub-stage instance entries where the schema's `reach_tier` is `"elevated"` or `"canopy"`
- Directly-harvested sub-stages (those with non-empty `available_life_stages`) must have `harvest_yield` as an object with `units_per_action` and `actions_until_depleted`; output-only parts (`available_life_stages: []`) must have `harvest_yield: null`
- Plants with `dispersal.method: "runner"` should have `seeds_per_mature_plant: [0, 0]`
- All food sub-stages (any part with nutritional data) must have `decay_days`; non-food parts (fiber, craft tags only, no calories) omit it
- Medicinal `dose_response` bands must have `treatment_tag` to be usable by partner
- Hallucinogen effects require `partner_prep_required: true` for vision system integration
- **Annuals:** Must have at least one day (typically days 39-40) with no valid life stage to trigger death. Life stages should cover days 1-35 or 1-38, leaving a gap in late winter.
- **Biennials:** `first_year` stage must have `seasonal_window: null` to cover all days in year 1. Second year stages must leave a gap (typically days 36-40) to trigger death after seeding.
- **Perennials:** Must have valid life stages covering all 40 days of the year (stages can cycle seasonally, but no gaps allowed).
- **Seeding window coverage:** `seeding_window` must fall entirely within a valid life stage's seasonal window. A plant cannot seed if it dies before `seeding_window.end` is reached.

**Field interdependencies:**
- `potency_multiplier` on sub-stage requires top-level `ingestion` object to exist
- `craft_tags` array can be empty `[]` but field must be present on all plant sub-stages
- `seasonal_window` on sub-stages uses format from Section 2.3a (e.g., `"early_summer"`, `"mid_fall"`)
- Animal output-only parts (e.g. `dried_hide`) use `available_life_stages: []` — the same convention as plant processing output parts

### 5.3 Sprites & Visuals

- All plants use final, high-detail botanical drawing sprites from the moment they germinate
- A player with real-world knowledge can identify a plant visually on the map before the game labels it
- Growth stage sprites per plant: seed, seedling, alive, flowering, fruiting, dying, dead, decaying
- Individual part sprites generated per part/sub-stage combination
- **Underground parts** have no sprite until discovered via the dig action. The tile appears as bare ground. Once discovered the part's sprite is shown normally and the harvest action becomes available.
- Fungi use a different sprite format
- Visual style: botanical drawing aesthetic, generated with Nano Banana
- **Isometric orientation:** All tile and plant sprites are drawn for isometric display. Tiles are 64×64 px source art rendered at 4× scale (256×256 px on screen), producing a visible tile footprint of 256 px wide × 128 px tall (the standard 2:1 isometric diamond). Plant sprites are 64×64 px source art scaled at **2× if `size` < 8** (small plants) or **4× if `size` ≥ 8** (large plants and trees), placed with their base anchored to the tile's center-bottom ground point. Underground parts continue to show no sprite until discovered.

### 5.4 Size & Tile Rules

- `life_stages[].size` is an integer 1-10 and represents canopy/biomass scale of the active stage (1 = seedling, 10 = very large tree).
- Size drives shade broadcast (see §5.1b): size 1-2 casts none, 3-5 casts range 1, 6-10 casts range 2 with distance falloff.
- A plant never shades its own tile; only surrounding tiles receive shade contribution.
- Each movement tile is a single plant occupancy site and can hold at most one plant entity at a time.
- A tile whose only occupant is an underground plant (one with `dig_ticks_to_discover` on all its harvestable parts) shows no above-ground sprite — it appears as bare ground until the player digs and discovers the root.

**Rendering size threshold:** The `size` integer also determines sprite scale in the isometric renderer. Size 1–7 ("small") renders the plant's 64×64 source sprite at 2× (128×128 px on screen). Size 8–10 ("large") renders at 4× (256×256 px on screen). This threshold is a rendering-layer concern only — game logic uses the raw `size` value for shade and occupancy calculations unchanged.

**Tile targeting for seed scattering:** The player can click a specific tile directly when placing seeds. When using a broadcast scatter (multiple seeds in one action), seeds fill outward in a spiral from the clicked tile in nearest-first order.

### 5.5 Plant Data Pipeline

- **Source:** Indiana Flora Wiki
- **Automated:** follow links, collect reference images and descriptions
- **AI fills** all data fields including sub-stages, fiber data, scent profiles, processing times, `reach_tier` and `ground_action_fraction` for tree species, and `primary_compound` for each plant's scent data
- **AI generates** the 8×8 pixel scent particle icon per plant, based loosely on the molecular shape of the `primary_compound`. Icons are abstracted to fit the pixel grid but carry a family resemblance to the real compound structure. The icon is baked into the plant's sprite sheet at pipeline time.
- **Human revision** based on real foraging experience
- **Output:** standardized plant JSON + sprite sheet per plant (includes growth stages, part sub-stages, and scent particle icon)

### 5.6 Plant Knowledge & Identification

**Unidentified plants:** UI shows "Unknown Plant." The inspect panel shows the current life stage's `field_description` and any above-ground parts' `field_description` for their active sub-stages. No `game_description` text, no nutritional data, no processing info visible. Physical clues are always available: growth form, scent notes, and texture observations from the sub-stage data.

**Visual ID:** botanical sprites are always shown in full detail — a knowledgeable player can identify before the game does.

**Identified plants:** the inspect panel additionally shows the top-level `game_description` as a header overview, and each active part sub-stage's `game_description` alongside its `field_description`. Full data suite visible — name (common + latin), all part/sub-stage nutritional and processing data, seasonal availability, and medicinal use.

**How identification works:** The player brings a plant sample to camp to enable partner research. A sample is either the whole small plant (for herbs, seedlings) or at least 3 harvested sub-pieces of the same part (e.g. 3 leaves, 3 pods). The player selects "Submit for Research" from the item's context menu at camp, which adds the plant to the partner's research queue as a researchable task. Once the partner completes research, the plant is identified for the remainder of the run and the sample is added to the Camp Samples library. The player can also opt to ignore the in-game identification if they already know the plant from real knowledge.

**Camp Samples:** a reference library accessible as a panel during the nightly debrief. Each identified plant gets a permanent entry showing its botanical sprite, name, all part/sub-stage properties, and medicinal notes. Camp Samples persist across epochs — a plant identified in Epoch 1 remains in the library through Epoch 5 without re-identification. The library is view-only; no interaction beyond reading is needed.

**Partner medicine knowledge:** The partner has full knowledge of all medicinal and hallucinogenic plants in the area regardless of the player's identification progress. This avoids punishing a knowledgeable player who doesn't need to formally identify a plant they already know. Research unlocks data for the *player's* UI — it does not gate the partner's treatment or vision administration capabilities.

### 5.7 Animal Data Pipeline

Animal species objects follow the same AI-generated, human-revised pipeline as plant objects.

- **Source:** species accounts from wildlife databases (e.g. Animal Diversity Web, Indiana DNR species guides)
- **AI fills** all data fields: weight ranges, habitat, behaviors, diet (as a list of latin plant IDs cross-referenced against the plant list), population parameters, part yields, and nutrition values
- **AI generates** a sprite sheet per species: idle, caught/dead, and any passive-interaction state (e.g. beaver lodge, squirrel cache)
- **Human revision** for ecological accuracy and gameplay balance
- **Output:** standardized animal JSON + sprite sheet per species

The `diet` array is particularly well-suited to AI generation — species diets are factual ecological data and can be directly mapped to the Indiana plant list being generated in parallel. Diet lists should be reviewed to confirm all referenced plant IDs exist in the plant dataset.

| --- | --- | --- |
| Morning | Day start | Review stockpile, choose tools, plan route |
| Foraging | Player's tick budget (~ticks 1–200) | Move, inspect, harvest, follow scents, set/check traps, field treatment |
| Return | Budget running low | Path back to camp before dark threshold |
| Night penalty | Outside camp after darkness threshold | Reduced visibility, injury risk; see Section 6.4 |
| Nightly debrief | At camp, end of day | Eat, receive discoveries, set partner schedule, review stockpile |

### 6.2 Player Actions & Tick Costs

| Action | Tick Cost | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Move | 1 tick / tile | Standard terrain |
| Inspect | 1 tick | Same cost for all plants, identified or not |
| Harvest (bare hand) | Defined per plant part | Base value in plant data; on tree sub-stages with `reach_tier: "elevated"` or `"canopy"`, only `remaining_actions_ground` actions are available without a tool |
| Harvest (with tool) | Base × modifier | Modifier defined in plant data; stool unlocks `remaining_actions_elevated` on `"elevated"` sub-stages; ladder unlocks `remaining_actions_elevated` on both `"elevated"` and `"canopy"` sub-stages; harvest tick cost is the same regardless of tool |
| Dig | Ticks committed freely; discovery interrupts | Always available on any tile; see §8.5 for full rules |
| Set trap | ~8 ticks (snare), ~12 ticks (dead-fall) | Starting estimates; see Section 10.1 |
| Check trap | 2 ticks | Move to trap tile and select Check; see Section 12.2 for resolution |
| Scatter Seeds | 1 tick | Select action → pick seeds from inventory → click target tile; seeds spiral outward from clicked tile; repeat for precise individual placement |
| Fell tree | Large tick cost by size | Requires axe; deliberate investment; see Section 3.7 |
| Field poultice | Ticks + cordage | Any plant part in inventory has "Apply as poultice" in its context menu; no filtering; works silently per ingestion data |
| Eat (field) | 2 ticks | Consume one serving from inventory; instantly refills hunger bar based on calories/requirement ratio; base nutrition values (no cook multiplier); does not accumulate nausea; calories consumed reduce that night's stew calorie target |

### 6.3 Inventory

- **Plant harvest quantity:** For a successful plant-part harvest, inventory gain is `applied_harvest_actions × scaled_units_per_action`, where `scaled_units_per_action` is the catalog `units_per_action` midpoint scaled by the same age factor as harvest pools (minimum one unit per harvest action applied). Reference age is `harvest_yield_full_age_days` on the sub-stage when set, otherwise species `age_of_maturity`.
- **Plant harvest mass per unit:** When `harvest_unit_weight_scales_with_age` is `true` on the sub-stage, each stack unit’s weight is catalog `unit_weight_g` (full maturity) times that same age scale (minimum 0.1), stored on the stack as `unitWeightKg`. Otherwise each unit weighs `unit_weight_g` as authored. Stew meal preview uses stockpile stack mass vs catalog to scale plant-part nutrition.
- **Grid:** 6×4 (24 slots); each slot holds one stack
- **Total weight limit:** 15 kg
- **Per-slot weight cap:** 2 kg maximum per stack. This prevents filling a single slot with an unreasonable quantity of bulk material. The total 15 kg limit remains the binding constraint in normal play; the per-slot cap only applies when a single stack would otherwise exceed 2 kg (e.g. a very large pile of acorn flour or berries). Discrete items with a natural `unit_weight_g` are quantity-capped at however many units fit within 2 kg — a 40g pod caps at 50 per slot; a 1.4g bean caps at ~1,428 per slot. Oversized items (ladder, basket, leaching basket) ignore the per-slot weight cap and always occupy exactly one slot.
- **Basket equipped:** occupies a 2×2 area in the main inventory grid (4 slots consumed) and adds a 1x8 overflow area (8 slots), increasing carry capacity to 25 kg. Net gain: 4 slots, but can't carry bulky items in it (anything with 2 width). The basket is carried in the grid rather than a dedicated equipment slot — equipping it is a tradeoff against other items that could fill those 4 grid slots.
- **Sled:** The player attaches the sled as an action (1 tick). Once attached, the sled follows the player on the same tile with each move. The sled has its own separate inventory grid (large capacity, high weight limit: +30 kg). The player opens their inventory and the sled inventory side-by-side and drags items between them to load/unload. Movement costs 2 ticks/tile instead of 1 while the sled is attached. The sled can enter all terrain types the player can enter, including forested tiles.
- **Equipment slots** (worn, not in grid): Torso (coat), Head (hat)

**Stack object schema:** every inventory stack tracks both quantity and total weight:

```json
{
  "item_id": "honey_locust_pod_dry",
  "quantity": 12,
  "weight_g": 480
}
```

`weight_g` is the total weight of the stack (`quantity × unit_weight_g`). `unit_weight_g` is defined on the plant sub-stage or animal part and is the gram weight of a single discrete unit — 40g for a dry honey locust pod, 1.4g for an extracted bean. The inventory weight limit check sums `weight_g` across all stacks. Quantity displays as a count for discrete items (tools, cordage units, pods, beans) and as grams/oz for bulk material where unit boundaries are arbitrary (loose leaves, ground flour). The data pipeline should set `unit_weight_g` to a round per-item gram value for anything the player would naturally count, and to 1.0 for bulk material where weight and quantity are effectively the same.

### 6.3a Tile Item Dropping

Any inventory item can be dropped onto a tile. Dropped items coexist with any plant on that tile — they do not block germination or harvesting. Only one dropped item stack can occupy a tile at a time.

**Capacity rules for dropped items:**
- **Discrete items** (`unit_weight_g > 1.0`): quantity-capped at the same per-slot limit as inventory (however many units fit within 2 kg). Oversized items (ladder, basket, leaching basket) always fit regardless of weight.
- **Bulk items** (`unit_weight_g = 1.0`): weight-capped at 2 kg, matching the inventory per-slot cap.

**Pickup:** the player moves to the tile or adjacent and selects Pick Up from the context menu (1 tick). The stack is added to inventory if space and weight allow; otherwise the action is blocked with a plain-language reason.

**Decay:** dropped items decay at the same rate as in inventory. Sun drying continues on items left on the ground (see §8.1 for ground drying rules including rain and theft risk).

**Rendering:** dropped items are displayed as a small sprite at the bottom of the tile, behind any plant sprite. See §6.3b for marker stick exception.

### 6.3b Marker Stick

The marker stick is a simple craftable used as a visual memory aid in the field. It has no game logic — it exists only to mark a location the player wants to remember (a known root site, a patch to return to, a trap direction).

**Craft recipe:** 1 branch, 5 ticks. Craftable by player or partner at hand, no station required, no research needed.

**Rendering:** the marker stick is displayed as a vertical sprite on the right side of its tile, in front of any plant sprite. This makes it visually distinct from dropped items (which render behind plants) and from plant stems it might otherwise resemble.

**Placement:** placed via the drop action like any item; picked up via the context menu. Obeys the one-item-per-tile rule — placing a marker stick on a tile that already has a dropped item is not allowed and vice versa.

### 6.4 Night Penalty & Seasonal Darkness Threshold

The darkness threshold — the tick at which night begins — varies by season to reflect Indiana's daylight hours:

| Season | Darkness Threshold | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Summer | Tick ~230 | Longest days; generous return window |
| Spring / Fall | Tick ~200 | Baseline |
| Winter | Tick ~160 | Short days; player must return much earlier |

The threshold shifts gradually day-by-day across the season rather than snapping at season boundaries.

**Dusk window (10 ticks before threshold):** A grace period — visibility dims but no penalties apply yet. Gives the player a reaction window to begin pathing home without immediate punishment.

**Visibility after dark:**
- Tiles beyond a 5-tile radius are fully dark (sprites hidden)
- Tiles within the 5-tile radius are dim (sprites visible but no mouseover data)

**Injury risk:** each tick spent past the darkness threshold has an independent per-tick injury roll. The probability starts low and increases with each overtime tick, capped at two darkness injuries per day regardless of how long the player stays out.

**Per-tick injury probability:**

| Ticks past threshold | Injury chance per tick |
|---|---|
| 1–10 | 2% |
| 11–30 | 4% |
| 31–55 | 6% |
| 55+ | 8% (cap) |

**Injury type** is resolved at the moment the roll succeeds — the player is injured mid-movement, not at end of day:

| Roll (d100) | Injury | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 1–70 | Scratch / minor cut | Small Health hit; 10–50% infection risk if untreated (see Section 14.1) |
| 71–100 | Sprain | Severity and duration depend on existing sprain state; see Section 14.1 |

The two-per-day cap means a player who has already taken two darkness injuries that night will receive no further injury rolls for the remainder of the day, regardless of ticks remaining. Night injury does not trigger immediately on the first overtime tick — the 2% floor gives the player a small reaction window after the dusk warning to begin pathing home before meaningful risk accumulates.

**Night penalty & camp tasks:** Night penalties (injury risk, visibility reduction) apply only to the player while **in the field** after the darkness threshold. A player who has returned to camp before dark — or who uses remaining budget ticks at camp after returning — can perform camp tasks freely by firelight: crafting tools, processing materials, field processing of carried items, and any other action that can be performed at camp. Camp tasks done after dark cost ticks normally from the player's remaining budget but incur no injury penalty. The campfire radius defines "at camp" — the player must be within the 3×3 camp tile area.

## 7. Camp & Partner System

The camp is the player's home base and the partner's operational domain. While the player forages, the partner works through a daily task queue — crafting, researching, cooking, and processing materials. The camp grows over epochs as new stations are built and comforts are added.

### 7.1 Camp Layout

- 3×3 tile area on the world map
- Wigwam: 1 full tile sprite — fits full family, no capacity complexity. The wigwam serves as the camp stockpile; storage is effectively unlimited for now and can be expanded with constraints in a future iteration.
- Other structures: quarter-tile sprites, up to 4 per tile
- Campfire + pot share a tile

### 7.2 Camp Stations

Functional stations that enable new capabilities or improve efficiency. All crafted by partner (or children when old enough). Require significant tick and material cost.

| Station | Function | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Campfire + Pot | Cooking, boiling | Starting equipment |
| Drying Rack | Preserve food by drying; protects food from rain and overnight animal theft; 4 inventory-slot capacity | Essential for winter prep; see Section 8 for ground-drying rules |
| Workbench | Speeds up crafting of traps, cordage, and small tools | ~20% tick reduction on eligible crafting tasks |
| Thread Spinner | Cordage production from plant fiber | See Section 10.3 |
| Hide Frame | Stretch and dry animal hides | Built from poles + cordage. One hide at a time; build a second frame to process in parallel. See Section 8.1 for full processing chain. |
| Mortar & Pestle | Grinding seeds, nuts, and dried plant material into flour or meal; required for `processing_location: "mortar_pestle"` tasks | Partner task; no special positioning required. Enables chenopod flour, acorn meal, and similar ground outputs. |
| Sugar Boiling Station | Boil down tree sap into canonical `tree_sugar` | Researchable; late-game; hide bag over fire; significant partner tick cost per batch; see §8.6 |

**Station Durability:** Unlike tools, stations have no durability and do not degrade.

**Station Build Costs:**

| Station | Partner Ticks | Materials | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Drying Rack | 120 ticks | 4 branches + 6 cordage units | 4-slot capacity; each slot holds one inventory-slot-equivalent stack |
| Workbench | 150 ticks | 6 poles + 4 cordage units | Mid-game; speeds trap and tool crafting |
| Thread Spinner | 200 ticks | 5 poles + 8 cordage units | Mid-game |
| Hide Frame | 80 ticks | 3 branches + 4 cordage units | One frame per hide lane; build multiples to parallelize |
| Mortar & Pestle | 60 ticks | 2 large stones (gathered from rocky tiles) + 2 cordage units | Early-mid game; stones require no tools to gather |
| Sugar Boiling Station | 100 ticks | 4 poles + hide (1,600g) + 4 cordage units + pitch (×2) | Researchable; late-game; hide bag suspended over fire; converts sap into tree sugar; see §8.6 |

*Branches can be gathered from deadwood without tools (see Section 8). Poles are produced by processing felled wood with an axe. All costs are starting estimates subject to balancing.*

### 7.3 Camp Comforts

Comforts don't give new abilities — they make the family more efficient and resilient.

Camp comforts are always-unlocked build actions (no research requirement) and are available from day 1.

| Comfort | Benefit | Build Cost | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Raised Sleeping Platform | +10 ticks to daily tick budget | 100 ticks partner; 5 branches + 4 cordage units | Comfort bonus only; no disease mechanic tied to it |
| Windbreak / Reflector Wall | Partner spends fewer ticks tending fire | 80 ticks partner; 4 branches + 6 cordage units | Camp maintenance cost drops from ~50 to ~40 ticks/day |

> ℹ️ Design tension: "Do I have the partner spend 2 days building a bed, or 2 days researching a better fishing net?" Comforts compete directly with tech research for partner time.

### 7.4 Partner Role & Schedule

The partner stays at camp handling time-intensive background tasks, freeing the player to focus on outdoor decisions.

**Partner Health:** The partner has the same three-bar system as the player (Hunger, Health, Thirst). The partner eats and drinks automatically from the stockpile each night at debrief, drawing the same 2,000 cal/day and ~2L/day requirements as an adult. If stockpile is insufficient, the relevant bar degrades. Partner death is a game-over condition.

**Partner tick budget:** Same as the player. **Camp maintenance** — covering fire-tending, cooking, water-carrying, and general upkeep — is auto-scheduled first as a single daily cost and does not need to be manually assigned. The remaining budget flows into the task queue.

| Condition | Camp Maintenance Cost |
| --- | --- |
| Spring / Summer / Fall | ~20 ticks |
| Winter / Freezing days | ~35 ticks |
| Winter with Windbreak built | ~25 ticks |

**Water at camp:** Water-carrying is included in the camp maintenance cost. All family members begin each day with a full thirst bar; no water inventory or capacity is tracked at camp. The player must manage their own thirst actively in the field via water tiles or a waterskin.

**Partner tasks:**
- Tool & camp station crafting
- Camp maintenance (fire, cooking, water — auto-scheduled; not manually queued)
- Plant research (~75 ticks per plant, starting estimate) — **partner exclusive; children cannot pull research tasks from the queue**. Research is queued automatically when the player submits a plant sample (see Section 5.6). Research unlocks the full data suite in the player's UI for that plant; the partner's medicinal and vision knowledge is unconditional and not gated by research.
- Medicine preparation from stockpiled plants
- Material processing: fiber extraction, drying, Thread Spinner operation, etc.

**Scheduling:** Each night at debrief, the player sets a prioritized task queue for the partner. Each item in the queue is a single task — one research project, one crafting job, or one processing run. The partner works through the queue in order; if a task completes mid-day, the next task begins automatically. The queue is locked to player edits until the following debrief — the player cannot change task order or add/remove tasks during the day. Workers (partner and children) still hand off tasks between themselves mid-day as normal when one finishes and picks up the next item in queue order. Each queued task displays an estimated tick cost and a progress bar at debrief. Progress persists for all task types — research and crafting alike — if a worker is reassigned mid-task: switching saves progress, resuming picks up where it left off. In multiplayer, any player can edit the queue or meal plan at the debrief screen; changes are visible live on all players' screens (see Section 17).

### 7.5 Medicine

- **Field treatment:** player applies poultice in the field, costs ticks, requires correct plant + cordage
- **Camp treatment:** partner requests specific plants from stockpile; prepares and administers
- Partner can advise on hallucinogen risk levels

## 8. Harvesting & Processing

The set of harvestable parts is not a fixed list — it is the union of all parts defined across all plant JSON files. Each plant defines its own parts and sub-stages. Illustrative part types include:

- Leaves, needles, fronds
- Stalk, stem, cane
- Bark (inner, outer), cambium
- Flowers, buds, catkins
- Fruits, berries, drupes
- Seeds, nuts, pods
- Roots, tubers, corms, rhizomes
- Whole plant (requires shovel; can transplant small plants)
- Fungi: cap, stalk, mycelium

Harvest tick costs and tool modifiers are defined per plant part in plant data. Different processing methods for the same plant reduce nausea similarity between meals.

**Poles** are a crafting intermediate produced by processing felled wood with an axe. They are the primary structural material for camp stations, tools like the ladder, and the hide frame. Pole yield per tree scales with tree size. Poles occupy inventory slots and stockpile space as a distinct item type.

**Branches** are gathered from fallen deadwood, standing dead snags that can be pushed over bare-handed (Stage 3 decay), or harvested from living trees without felling them. Branches serve as a substitute for poles in early-game recipes (simple tools and small structures) but cannot be used for major camp stations or advanced tools. Gathering branches costs 3–8 ticks depending on source and requires no tools.

### 8.1 Drying Food

Food items with `can_dry: true` can be dried to slow their decay significantly. Drying accumulates a dryness value (0.0–1.0) on the item stack that directly scales the decay multiplier down — a fully dried item decays at ~0.05× its fresh rate. There is no conversion to a separate "dried variant" item; the same stack simply becomes increasingly shelf-stable as dryness rises. At dryness 1.0 the item gains a **(dried)** name suffix and a sepia sprite tint. See §4.5 for the full drying modifier and stacking spec.

**Thin-slicing (knife required):** Some large, dense plant parts (e.g. large tubers, thick roots) have `can_dry: false` in their sub-stage data — they are too thick to dry before spoiling under normal conditions. If the player has a knife in inventory at the time of harvest, the harvest action automatically yields a sliced version of the part with `can_dry: true`. Without a knife, the item is harvested whole and cannot be dried. The sliced variant has identical nutrition but is a distinct inventory representation (slightly lower `unit_weight_g` due to exposed surface area enabling drying). The data pipeline should set `can_dry: false` on any root or tuber sub-stage where the thickness would realistically prevent drying in time.

**Drying advances once per day at the day transition**, using the daily sun value (0.0–1.0) for that day (see §4.5). Weather is a full-day state fixed at day start (see §15.1) — a rainy day is rainy all day, a clear day is clear all day. There is no per-tick drying; what matters is where the item was placed when the day closed, not when during the day it was placed. In Freezing temperatures the daily sun value is treated as 0 — items freeze rather than dry.

**Drying Rack (camp station):** Items on the rack at day close receive the full daily sun value as their drying increment, multiplied by 2× for airflow. The rack provides two additional protections ground-drying does not:
- **Rain** — rack keeps items dry during wet weather; a rainy day (sun value = 0) resets drying progress on ground-laid items but not rack items
- **Overnight animal theft** — rack items are secured; see Ground Drying below

The rack has **4 slots**, each holding one inventory-slot-equivalent stack (same capacity rules as §6.3 — discrete items quantity-capped at 2 kg worth, bulk items weight-capped at 2 kg, oversized items take one slot). Partner assigns items to the rack as a queue task.

**Ground drying:** Items left on the ground at day close receive the full daily sun value as their drying increment (same as rack, but without the 2× multiplier). This is slow and risky:
- On a rainy day, drying progress on all ground-laid items is reset to 0 at day transition
- Each night, high-calorie items left on the ground have a **~40% chance of being taken by a wild animal** (scavengers, birds). Low-calorie foliage and non-food items are ignored. Items on the drying rack are never stolen.
- Ground drying is viable early-game before the rack is built, or as overflow when the rack is full — but it is unreliable for valuable stockpile items.

### 8.2 Hide Processing

Fresh hides are harvested from trap catches. All hides are the same quality regardless of animal but yield different quantities of finished material, defined in each animal species object's `hide` part. Hide weight is measured in grams; a coat shell requires ~3,200g. Representative yields: squirrel 400g, rabbit 800g, turkey ~800g (feathers are a separate harvested part), beaver 2,400g, deer 6,400g (future hunting).

**Processing Steps:**

1. **Fresh hide** — harvested from trap catch via field butchering (see §12.2). `decay_days: 3` — spoils quickly if not processed.
2. **Scrape** — partner task, 40 ticks. **Knife required in inventory.** Removes fat and membrane. Produces scraped hide; fat is a separate output part added to the stockpile.
3. **Stretch and dry on frame** — partner task, 60 ticks spread over multiple days. The Hide Frame station is occupied for the duration, blocking other hide processing. Produces dried hide.
4. **Dried hide** — stable, no decay. Usable for coat shell, waterskin, hat, and other future recipes.

**Knife requirement for leatherworking:** the knife is required in inventory for all hide crafting tasks — scraping, and cutting hide for any finished item (waterskin, hat, coat). Without a knife, these processing and crafting actions are unavailable.

### 8.3 Crafting Intermediates

Crafting intermediates are materials produced through processing that serve as inputs for tools, camp stations, and equipment. Unlike consumables, they do not decay.

**Canonical intermediate item registry (runtime source of truth):**
- Cross-species intermediates (for example `cordage`, `hide`, `dried_hide`, `fat`) are defined as canonical item IDs in a centralized item registry.
- Processing outputs can reference these canonical IDs directly via `outputs[].itemId`.
- In this phase, harvested plant identity remains species-specific (`species:part:sub_stage`) and is not collapsed into canonical IDs.

**Processing pipeline semantics (runtime):**
- `process_item` consumes source stack quantity from actor inventory.
- Outputs are resolved from catalog `processing_options.outputs`; entries can target canonical `itemId` or part-based outputs.
- For station locations (`hide_frame`, `mortar_pestle`, `sugar_boiling_station`, etc.), validation requires actor in camp bounds and station built.
- Output stacks are added to inventory with overflow dropped nearby, matching existing inventory overflow behavior.

**Poles**
- **Source:** Felled trees processed with an axe
- **Yield:** Scales with tree size (1–10 poles; see Section 3.7)
- **Uses:** Primary structural material for camp stations, ladder, hide frame
- **Storage:** Distinct item type in stockpile

**Branches**
- **Source:** Fallen deadwood, Stage 3 dead snags (bare-handed), or harvested from living trees
- **Yield:** 2–4 per source
- **Tick cost:** 3–8 ticks depending on source
- **Uses:** Early-game substitute for poles in simple recipes; cannot replace poles in major stations
- **Storage:** Distinct item type in stockpile

**Cordage**
- **Source:** Plant fiber processed via hand-twisting or Thread Spinner station
- **Quality tiers:** Determined by `cordage_quality = fiber_strength_modifier × length_factor` at twist time (see Section 10.2)
- **Uses:** Tool crafting, trap construction, binding, camp station assembly
- **Storage:** Measured in units; stacks in stockpile

**Dried Hide**
- **Source:** Fresh hide → scraped → stretched and dried on Hide Frame (see Section 8.2)
- **Yield:** 85% of fresh hide weight after moisture loss (squirrel ~340g, rabbit ~680g, beaver ~2,040g)
- **Uses:** Coat shell (~3,200g required), waterskin, hat, future leather crafting
- **Storage:** Stable, no decay; stacks by weight

**Inner Bark Cloth**
- **Source:** `cordage_fiber` processed by partner (soaking, beating, drying)
- **Processing time:** ~120 ticks (partner task)
- **Uses:** Coat shell material (alternative to hide), future textile crafting
- **Storage:** Measured in units; stacks in stockpile

**Bone**
- **Source:** Harvested from trap catches (all game animals yield 1 bone)
- **Dual use:** Added directly to stew as an ingredient — nutrients are extracted during cooking and the bone is discarded afterward. Alternatively saved as a crafting material.
- **Crafting uses:** Fishing hooks, needles, awls, future bone tools
- **Storage:** Raw bones stack in stockpile; player chooses when to consume vs. craft
- **Notes:** Strategic choice — early game may favor calories; mid-game favors tool crafting

**Thread** (future)
- **Source:** Fine plant fiber processed via Thread Spinner
- **Uses:** Sewing, fine cordage for specialized tools
- **Storage:** Measured in length units

**Pitch**
- **Source:** Pine bark or birch bark heated at the campfire — a partner task (~40 ticks per batch). Pitch-producing species have a `pitch_yield` property on their bark sub-stage (see §5.1); the data pipeline flags pine and birch species accordingly. **Harvesting pitch-yielding bark requires a knife in inventory** to score the bark before stripping — the harvest action is unavailable without one.
- **Yield:** Small batch per processing run; requires meaningful bark input
- **Uses:** Waterproofing (waterskin seams, bark vessels, leaching basket), tool hafting (flint knife, axe head adhesive), Sugar Boiling Station construction. Additional uses stubbed for future implementation: sealing bark containers, waterproofing cordage.
- **Storage:** Stable, no decay; stacks in stockpile as discrete units

### 8.4 Leaching Basket

The Leaching Basket is a crafted tool item carried in inventory. It is the primary method for processing acorns and other high-tannin plant material into edible flour or meal. The player fills the basket with cracked or ground material at camp (partner task, ~10 ticks), moves to any water tile, and places it with a single action (2 ticks). The basket leaches passively from that point — no player or partner attention required until retrieval.

**Tannin reduction rate:**
- Flowing water tile: −0.25 tannin per day
- Still water tile: −0.10 tannin per day

Each day the world tick checks all placed baskets and reduces `tannin_remaining` by the appropriate rate. The player retrieves the basket by moving to its tile and selecting Retrieve (2 ticks). The output item's `edibility_harshness` is derived directly from the `tannin_remaining` value at retrieval — the player can retrieve early at any tannin level and the output reflects whatever state the batch is in. If `tannin_remaining` is above ~0.3, the inspect panel notes "still bitter — needs more time."

Oak species carry a `tannin_level` float (0.0–1.0) on their acorn sub-stage. This value initializes `tannin_remaining` when the player loads the basket. White oak species typically 0.4–0.6 (1–3 days in flowing water); red oak species typically 0.7–1.0 (3–6 days). A player who knows their oaks can prioritize white oak patches in fall for faster turnaround — this is a direct expression of the real-knowledge-matters pillar.

**Basket state schema** (when placed in the world):
```json
{
  "item_id": "leaching_basket",
  "state": "placed",
  "species_id": "quercus_alba",
  "tannin_remaining": 0.55,
  "water_tile_id": [142, 88]
}
```
When carried in inventory, `state` is `"held"` and `water_tile_id` is `null`.

The basket occupies a quarter-tile slot on its water tile while placed (distinct sprite). It does not block movement or harvesting on that tile.

**Craft recipe:** 3 `flexible_shoot` material + 4 cordage units. Crafted by player or partner at hand (no station required), 30 ticks.

### 8.5 Digging & Underground Parts

Some plant parts exist entirely below ground — roots, rhizomes, tubers, corms, and bulbs — and are invisible until dug out. These parts carry `dig_ticks_to_discover` in their sub-stage data. Above-ground parts of the same plant are always harvestable normally; below-ground parts require the dig action first, even when the above-ground plant is fully visible.

**The dig action** is always available on any tile. The player selects Dig, chooses a tool (or bare hands), and commits ticks freely. Digging is interrupted automatically the moment discovery occurs — the player does not need to predict or manually stop. Any amount of digging immediately applies a disturbed soil state to the tile, which interacts with the pioneer/disturbance germination system (see §3.9).

**Discovery** — the tile tracks `ticks_dug` as digging accumulates. When `ticks_dug` (adjusted by tool and temperature modifiers) reaches the part's `dig_ticks_to_discover` threshold, digging is interrupted and the player is notified of the find. The part's sprite becomes visible on the tile and the harvest action becomes available for that part specifically.

**Max dig depth** — each tile has a global cap of **40 baseline dig-ticks** (before modifiers). If this is reached with no plant found, the player receives an "empty ground" notification and further digging on that tile is blocked until the disturbed soil recovers. The 40-tick cap ensures the player always gets a resolution and never digs indefinitely on a barren tile.

**Tool modifiers for digging:**

| Tool | Dig tick modifier | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bare hands | 1.0× (baseline) | Slow; practical only for shallow roots |
| Digging stick | 0.6× | Early-game tool; makes most roots practical |
| Shovel | 0.35× | Makes deep roots worth the time investment |

The same modifier applies to both tick cost toward the cap and tick cost toward `dig_ticks_to_discover`, so a shovel compresses both uniformly.

**Temperature modifiers for digging** (applied on top of tool modifier, multiplicative):

| Temperature band | Dig tick modifier | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Mild / Warm / Hot | 1.0× | No penalty |
| Cool | 1.2× | Slightly firmer ground |
| Cold | 1.8× | Hard ground; roots still viable with shovel |
| Freezing | 3.5× | Near-prohibitive; deeply frozen ground |

Combined modifiers stack: a shovel (0.35×) in Cold (1.8×) = 0.63× net — still faster than bare hands in Mild. Bare hands in Freezing (3.5×) = effectively prohibitive for any but the shallowest roots.

**Post-discovery harvest** — once a part is discovered it is harvested using the normal harvest action with its own `harvest_base_ticks` and `harvest_tool_modifiers` defined in plant data. Dig tool modifiers apply to digging, not automatically to harvest.

**Tile state** — each tile tracks:
```json
{
  "ticks_dug": 12,
  "underground_discovered": false,
  "disturbed": true
}
```
`disturbed` is set to `true` on the first dig tick and persists until the tile recovers naturally (same recovery timeline as other disturbance sources). `underground_discovered` flips to `true` on discovery and remains true until the part is harvested.

**Worm harvesting** — earthworms are a secondary yield from digging, available on any non-frozen non-water tile outside the Freezing temperature band. They are not a plant sub-stage and have no `dig_ticks_to_discover` — instead, each dig-tick on an eligible tile has a small probability of yielding 1–3 worms, scaled loosely by soil moisture (wet soil = higher chance). Worms are an inventory item with `decay_days: 1` — they die quickly and must be used promptly. They are not edible but are highly effective fishing bait (see §12.4). Bee larvae from a beehive harvest (see §13.1) are also excellent fishing bait. Worm state is not stored on the tile — they are a procedural drop from digging with no world representation.

### 8.6 Tree Tapping & Sap

Tree sap is an early spring resource available from tappable mature trees before most other foraging is viable. Tappable species: sugar maple, silver maple, red maple, birch, black walnut, sycamore. Each species has a `sap` part in its plant object with a `seasonal_window`, a `sap_profile`, and full nutritional data generated by the AI pipeline. Sugar maple yields the most and sweetest sap; birch yields least but has a distinctive flavor profile; walnut sap is drinkable but low-value.

`sap_profile` on the sap sub-stage is:

```json
"sap_profile": {
  "is_tappable": true,
  "sugar_percent": 2.4,
  "daily_yield_range_g": [300, 900]
}
```

`sugar_percent` is a concentration percentage (brix-style) used by sap boiling yield math. `daily_yield_range_g` is grams per day into a placed vessel.

**Sap flow** — fixed seasonal window (late winter / early spring). A small trickle is available outside the primary window year-round but at significantly reduced `daily_yield_range_g`. Only mature trees can be tapped; saplings have no sap part.

**Tapping flow:**
1. Player crafts a **Carved Wooden Spout** (knife required in inventory to carve; see §10.1) and carries one or more **Hollowed Log Vessels** (empty) to a tappable mature tree.
2. Player inserts the spout into the tree (2 ticks). **Knife must be in inventory** to make the tapping cut. The spout stays in the tree tile as a placed item until explicitly removed. Without a knife, the insert action is unavailable.
3. Player places an empty vessel under the spout (2 ticks). The vessel fills passively at the tree's `daily_yield_range_g` rate.
4. Player returns later, retrieves the full or partial vessel (2 ticks), and optionally places a fresh empty immediately — the spout keeps running continuously.
5. To stop tapping, player removes the spout (2 ticks) and it returns to inventory for reuse.

A tree can only hold one spout at a time. One vessel per spout. The carved spout is reusable indefinitely.

**Hollowed log vessel** — 2×2 inventory footprint, not stackable. Comes in two states: empty (placeable) and filled/partial (carries sap, not placeable until emptied). Bringing a sled load of empties and returning with fulls is the intended rhythm for serious sap harvesting.

**Sap uses:**
- **Drink in field** (2 ticks): refills thirst bar partially; provides minor calories. Same mechanic as eating field food.
- **Add to stew**: emptied into the stew pot at camp like any food ingredient; contributes calories and sugar content.
- **Boil at Sugar Boiling Station**: converts sap into generic `tree_sugar` (see below).

**Sap decay** — accelerated in Mild, Warm, and Hot temperature bands. Fresh sap left in a vessel decays noticeably faster than other food items once temperatures rise above Cold, reinforcing the urgency of the early spring harvest window.

**Sugar Boiling Station** — a researchable late-game camp station. A hide bag suspended from a pole frame over the campfire, sealed with pitch. Significant partner tick cost (~150 ticks per batch). Sap-to-sugar ratio is roughly 40:1 by volume — a meaningful investment of accumulated sap yields a small amount of high-calorie, shelf-stable tree sugar. Output is always canonical `tree_sugar` (not species-split). Tree sugar does not decay and stacks in the stockpile.

Sap boiling uses one shared processing contract (not repeated per plant JSON):

```json
{
  "id": "boil_sap",
  "location": "sugar_boiling_station",
  "ticks": 150,
  "output_item_id": "tree_sugar",
  "yield_inputs": ["input_sap_grams", "sap_profile.sugar_percent"]
}
```

The station requires research before the build recipe is available. Build cost: 4 poles + hide (1,600g) + 4 cordage units + pitch (×2).

## 9. Nutrition & Nausea

### 9.1 Nutrition Tracking

Each food item tracks: calories, protein, carbs, fat. Meeting protein and fat requirements in the nightly stew grants the bonus tick budget to every family member (see Section 2.2).

**Daily calorie requirements:**

| Person | Daily Calories |
| --- | --- |
| Adult (player/partner) | 2,000 cal/day |
| Child age 5 | 800 cal/day |
| Child age 10 | 1,200 cal/day |
| Child age 15–20 | 1,600 cal/day |

**Hunger bar drain:** The hunger bar depletes based on the global day-clock tick counter at a rate of **0.5 bar per 400 active day-ticks** (0.00125 bar per tick). All family members' hunger bars drain at the same rate as the day advances. In multiplayer, hunger drains based on the shared global day clock — a player waiting for others still gets hungrier as the day advances. No hunger ticks occur during the night.

**Thirst bar drain:** The thirst bar depletes at the same rate as hunger — **0.5 bar per 400 active day-ticks**. Characters at camp are assumed to have access to stored and boiled water as part of camp maintenance; thirst only drains for characters who are out in the field (the player, and children old enough to forage). The partner's thirst is covered by camp maintenance and does not drain. Thirst is replenished by drinking from a waterskin or water source in the field, which instantly raises the bar by the amount consumed. No thirst ticks occur during the night.

**Hunger bar refill:** Eating food instantly refills the hunger bar based on calories consumed relative to daily calorie requirements. The refill amount is calculated as: **calories_consumed / daily_requirement**. For example, an adult eating 400 calories gains 400/2,000 = 0.2 bar (20%) instant refill. This applies identically to field eating and stew eating — there is no mechanical advantage to either approach beyond timing and efficiency (stew benefits from cooking multipliers and costs no ticks). The hunger bar caps at 100% during field eating and cannot be overfilled in the field. At the nightly stew, the bar can be raised to 100% regardless of its current level — if a member's bar has been partially depleted over multiple days, the stew can fill the full deficit in one sitting, and there is no penalty for eating more than the day's drain would require.

If the hunger bar reaches zero, the Health bar begins draining immediately at 1/3 bar per 400 active day-ticks (see §1.3). The drain stops the moment food raises the hunger bar above zero.

**Hunger-based debuffs at day start:**

Hunger debuffs are evaluated once at day start based on the bar's current level at that moment. Changes to the bar during the day take effect at the following day's debuff check.

| Hunger Bar Level at Day Start | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| 50–100% | No debuff |
| 25-50% | −10 ticks from daily budget |
| 1–24% | −20 ticks from daily budget
| 0% (empty) | −40 ticks from daily budget; Health drains 1/3 bar per day |

**Protein + fat threshold for tick bonus:** The bonus is determined solely by the properties of the nightly stew, and applies to every family member equally — player, partner, and all children — regardless of whether any individual ate field food during the day. The stew must meet **≥30g protein AND ≥20g fat** in total to qualify. This is a single household check against the adult thresholds — there is no separate per-child requirement. Meeting this threshold grants +10 ticks to the next day's budget.

Tick reductions from hunger debuffs are applied at day start before any other modifiers. They stack with the protein/fat bonus and other modifiers — a malnourished player who also came home late and accumulated overdraft could open a day significantly below their base 200.

### 9.2 Nausea Mechanics

Nausea limits how much of a given food the player can eat at one sitting — it does not reduce the calories obtained per unit eaten. A high-nausea food simply has a lower effective serving cap, which makes it harder to meet full calorie needs if most available foods are nauseating.

**Field eating:** Plant parts with `edibility_score >= 0.85` (ripe fruit, berries, nuts) can be eaten directly from the player's inventory while foraging. Field eating instantly refills the hunger bar based on calories consumed (calories/requirement ratio), modified by `raw_extraction_efficiency` (defaults to 1.0). Items with `raw_extraction_efficiency: 0.0` provide no calories when eaten raw even if they have nutritional data. Field eating reduces what the stew needs to cover at night. Field eating has no interaction with the nausea system — it does not accumulate nausea bars, does not affect the Monotony Score, and does not apply the edibility ceiling.

**Shared household nausea:** The nausea system is household-wide. There is a single shared set of per-ingredient nausea bars for the entire family. Everyone eats from the same pot; tolerance accumulates once per meal per ingredient regardless of family size or composition.

There are 2 components to nausea: the Monotony score, which increases per-ingredient nausea bars from eating the same plant parts over and over, and the Edibility score, which caps intake when ingredients are too bitter, fibrous, or otherwise unpalatable in quantity. Maximum calories extractable from a stew is governed by:

```
max_intake = min(edibility_ceiling, nausea_ceiling)
```

Both are expressed as a fraction of each individual's daily calorie need. The ceiling is applied per-person: a child with an 800 cal/day requirement and a `max_intake` of 0.3 can extract at most 240 cal from the stew; an adult at 2,000 cal/day extracts at most 600 cal. Each family member's effective calorie intake is their proportional share of the pot (see §9.4) capped by their individual `max_intake`.

**Edibility ceiling:**

Each plant part sub-stage has two edibility fields:

- `edibility_score` (0.0–1.0): the maximum fraction of daily calories that this ingredient can contribute before the stew becomes unpalatable. A potato-equivalent at 0.85 can dominate a stew without issue. Inner bark at 0.15 makes the stew nearly inedible if added in any meaningful quantity.
- `edibility_harshness` (0.0–1.0): how quickly exceeding the edibility score degrades palatability. A mildly bitter green at 0.5 — going 10% over its limit costs 5% ceiling. Inner bark at 1.0 — going 10% over its limit costs the full 10%. Default value if omitted: `0.5`.

For each ingredient, excess share beyond its edibility score drives down the stew ceiling:

```
excess_i = max(0, caloric_share_i − edibility_score_i)
edibility_ceiling = 1.0 − sum(excess_i × edibility_harshness_i)
```

Ceiling clamped to [0.0, 1.0].

**Suggested defaults for the data pipeline:**

| Part type | edibility_score | edibility_harshness |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ripe fruit / berries | 0.90 | 0.4 |
| Nuts | 0.85 | 0.4 |
| Starchy roots / tubers | 0.85 | 0.5 |
| Legumes | 0.75 | 0.5 |
| Tender greens / leaves | 0.65 | 0.6 |
| Fibrous stalks | 0.35 | 0.8 |
| Inner bark | 0.15 | 1.0 |
| Bitter medicinal parts | 0.10 | 1.0 |

Edibility only applies to stew. Food eaten raw from inventory in the field bypasses the stew system and uses base nutrition values directly.

Each food item has three tags that drive similarity scoring: `plant_family` (e.g. allium, apiaceae, grass), `part_type` (e.g. root, leaf, seed, fruit), and `flavor_profile` (e.g. starchy, bitter, sweet, fatty).

**Monotony Score:** when building the evening stew, each ingredient pair is scored for similarity. Same `plant_family` = high similarity hit; same `part_type` = medium hit; same `flavor_profile` = low hit. The meal's overall Monotony Score (0–100) is the sum of pairwise similarity hits, weighted by caloric proportion. A stew that is 80% cattail root and 20% cattail seed is nearly as monotonous as pure cattail root; a stew with a root, a fruit, a leaf, and a nut from four different plant families scores near zero.

Each player has a per-ingredient nausea bar that accumulates when that ingredient appears in the stew. The rate of accumulation is driven by the meal's Monotony Score — a varied stew barely moves ingredient nausea bars; a mono-diet stew slams them.

Per-ingredient nausea bars cap at 100. Above 50, the per-serving cap for that ingredient shrinks. Above 80, humans refuse to eat that ingredient at all so adding it to a stew becomes impossible in the UI.

**Nausea UI:** each ingredient's nausea bar is displayed using the plant's botanical sprite as the bar icon. This applies to identified plants (showing the named sprite) and unidentified plant parts (showing the unidentified sprite silhouette). The meal planner also shows a separate edibility ceiling indicator — if any ingredient's caloric share exceeds its `edibility_score`, the ceiling is shown as suppressed with a plain-language note (e.g. "too much bitter bark — stew unpalatable").

When adding plants to the pot in the UI, parts from identified plants that have a low edibility score will show a warning when added in large proportion. Unidentified plant parts don't do this.

**Nausea accumulation rates by Monotony Score band:**

| Meal label | Score range | Nausea added per ingredient per day | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Varied | 0–25 | 0 | No accumulation |
| Repetitive | 26–55 | +3 | Cap reached in ~33 days of pure repetition |
| Monotonous | 56–100 | +17 | Cap reached in ~6 days of pure mono-diet |

Only ingredients that actually appear in the meal accumulate nausea that day.

**Nausea decay:** Each ingredient's nausea bar decays by 10 points per day it is absent from the stew entirely. There is no decay on any day the ingredient appears in the meal, regardless of how varied the meal is. Accumulation and decay never apply on the same day. Rotating ingredients out of the stew is the only recovery mechanism — the benefit of a varied meal is slower accumulation, not active recovery of the ingredients it contains.

**Spices** add a distinct `flavor_profile` tag to the stew while maybe or maybe not contributing meaningful calories, lowering the Monotony Score even when core ingredients are repetitive. Finding and stockpiling spices (wild garlic, bergamot, spicebush berries) is a meaningful quality-of-life upgrade.

Camp Comfort (Seated Work Area) also reduces the Monotony Score contribution to nausea accumulation slightly.

After a meal there is some text feedback about it. This might be: "too fibrous," "I'm tired of cattail rhizomes" "too bitter," "too tough."

### 9.3 Meal Planning UI

The player selects stew ingredients manually at the nightly debrief. The preview updates live as ingredients are added or removed.

While selecting, the player sees a live preview:
- Total calories, protein, carbs, fat
- **Each member's current hunger bar level** — shown as the fill target; the stew raises bars toward 100%
- Effective calories after `max_intake` ceiling (edibility and nausea), with the binding constraint labelled: *"Nausea limited"* or *"Edibility limited"*
- Nausea warnings per identified ingredient with plain-language explanations
- Whether the meal meets the protein/fat threshold for the +10 tick bonus
- Overall variety score shown as a qualitative label: **Varied / Repetitive / Monotonous**

### 9.4 Cooking & Eating

The stew is prepared by the partner each evening and consumed by all family members at the nightly debrief. Everyone in camp eats from the same pot — total calories are divided among family members in proportion to their daily calorie requirements (adults 2,000 cal; children scaled by age per Section 9.1). Each family member's hunger bar is immediately raised based on their portion (calories received / daily requirement). If the stew falls short of full requirements, each person receives a proportional share of what is available — no priority ordering, shortfall is shared equally by weight. The protein/fat tick bonus is determined by the stew composition alone and applies to the whole household if the threshold is met (see Section 9.1).

**Over-eating to cover deficits:** If a member's hunger bar is below 100% at stew time (from the day's drain or from prior days' shortfalls), the stew fills as much of the deficit as it can. There is no penalty for eating more than a single day's drain would require — a player who had a bad day or two of low hunger can fully recover at stew time if the pot is large enough. The bar is simply raised toward 100%; no waste or penalty results from eating beyond the day's nominal requirement.

**Leftover stew:** Calories are consumed from the stew until all family members' hunger bars are full (100%) or until the nausea/edibility ceiling is reached, whichever comes first. Any calories remaining after that point are destroyed — they do not carry over to the next day. 

If hunger bars are depleted (e.g., from skipping previous meals or health drain), the stew can exceed the daily calorie requirement to refill them. For example, if the household's daily requirement is 4,000 cal but their combined hunger bars are at 50%, a 6,000 cal stew would fully restore them. However, if the nausea/edibility ceiling caps intake at 5,000 cal, the remaining 1,000 cal is lost as waste. The meal planning UI shows both the hunger bar refill target and the nausea ceiling before the player confirms.

**Stew calorie target:** The stew needs to raise every family member's hunger bar to 100%. The meal planning UI shows each member's current bar level and how much of the deficit the planned stew will cover. Field eating earlier in the day already raised bars directly — the stew only needs to cover whatever remains. If the player ate 600 cal in the field, those calories already moved their bar up; the stew covers the rest.

**Partial and empty stew:** The stew can be any size, including empty. If the stockpile is depleted or the player intentionally skips a meal, the stew contributes zero calories and the household's hunger bars degrade normally. The meal planning UI shows the expected shortfall clearly before the player confirms.

The player can eat food with `edibility_score >= 0.85` (ripe fruit, berries, nuts) directly from their inventory at any point during the day while foraging (see Section 9.2). Field eating draws from carried items, does not interact with the nausea system, and reduces that night's stew calorie target.

Cooking increases caloric value of some ingredients like tubers (multiplier defined in plant object's `cook_calorie_multiplier`). When items are added to stew, `stew_extraction_efficiency` determines what fraction of nutrition is extractable by boiling (defaults to 1.0 for full extraction; set to 0.0 for items like whole pods that can't be eaten by boiling). If present, `cooked_edibility_score` and `cooked_harshness` override raw values when the item is cooked in stew. Partner handles all camp cooking as part of the daily camp maintenance cost.

**Deferred implementation note (fish + daytime camp cooking):** Fish catches are intended to flow directly into stew planning; basic fish cleaning is abstracted into the partner's normal stew preparation (no separate player cleaning step for stew intent). A future stew-mechanics pass will also add a **camp-only daytime cook-and-eat** action so the player can consume otherwise non-field-edible foods while at camp by paying a tick cost. That daytime camp-cooked intake will be resolved with the same stew extraction/edibility rules and will contribute to the same shared household nausea accounting (ingredient monotony + nausea bars) rather than using the raw field-eating path.

### 10.2 Cordage Quality

Cordage quality is a derived value calculated from two fields on the source plant's sub-stage:

```
length_factor = clamp(average_fiber_length_cm / 15.0, 0.5, 1.5)
cordage_quality = fiber_strength_modifier × length_factor
```

`average_fiber_length_cm` is normalized around 15cm as a neutral midpoint. Fibers shorter than ~7.5cm produce a 0.5× length penalty; fibers longer than ~22cm produce up to a 1.5× bonus. `fiber_strength_modifier` reflects the tensile strength of the fiber itself. Both dimensions matter: a plant with strong but short fibers (e.g. some grasses) produces mediocre cordage, as does one with long but weak fibers. The premium materials — dogbane, basswood, stinging nettle — combine both.

The resulting `cordage_quality` value maps to quality tiers:

| Tier | `cordage_quality` range | Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Coarse | < 0.5 | High snare escape rate; high line snap probability; tools break faster |
| Medium | 0.5 – 1.0 | Standard durability and success rates |
| High | > 1.0 | Low snare escape rate; low line snap probability; full durability |

`cordage_quality` is computed at the point of twisting and stored on the resulting cordage unit. The data pipeline should target these approximate values for representative Indiana species: dogbane ~1.4, basswood inner bark ~1.3, stinging nettle ~1.1, elm inner bark ~0.9, cattail leaf ~0.5, common grass ~0.3.

### 10.3 Thread Spinner

The Thread Spinner is a camp station that converts raw plant fiber into usable cordage. It is the primary cordage production method mid-to-late game, replacing slow hand-twisting.

**Input:** Raw fiber — harvested fibrous plant parts (stalks, inner bark strips, leaves with fiber content). `cordage_quality` is computed at twist time from the source part's `average_fiber_length_cm` and `fiber_strength_modifier` (see §10.2).

**Output:** Cordage units. One cordage unit is a standard length suitable for one snare, one binding, or one tool repair.

**Operation:** The Thread Spinner is a partner task. The partner loads fiber from the stockpile and runs a spinning session. Fiber is consumed and cordage units are added to the stockpile.

**Production rate:** The Thread Spinner produces cordage at roughly **2× the speed of hand-twisting** for medium fiber, with quality modifying that ratio. Both the player in the field and the partner at camp can hand-twist at 15 ticks/unit regardless of fiber quality. The Thread Spinner rates reflect that it eliminates the most time-consuming step (initial fiber alignment).

| Fiber Quality | Hand-Twist (ticks/unit) | Thread Spinner (ticks/unit) | Speedup |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Coarse / Short | 15 | 12 | 1.25× — marginal gain; fiber breakage limits efficiency |
| Medium | 15 | 7 | ~2× — primary use case |
| High / Long | 15 | 4 | ~3.75× — high-quality fiber benefits most from the station |

The Thread Spinner's value is pure throughput — the partner produces significantly more cordage per tick budget than hand-twisting allows, which matters most when building up a large snare network or crafting multiple high-cordage items back to back.

**Hand-twisting (no station):** The player can produce cordage in the field without the Thread Spinner by hand-twisting fibrous material. Cost: ~20 ticks per unit regardless of fiber quality. This is the only cordage production method before the Thread Spinner is researched. Hand-twisting in the field costs player tick budget; assigning the partner to Thread Spinner spinning costs partner tick budget instead, freeing the player.

## 11. Research & Tech Unlocks

### 11.1 Philosophy

All unlockable items (tools, camp stations, recipes) are organized into a **tech forest** — a collection of small graphs, each 3 nodes deep (tunable), where random tech is gated behind other random tech. The forest is generated fresh each run from the map seed: techs are shuffled and distributed across trees, so the path to any given unlock varies run to run.

The forest is fully visible to the player from the start of the run. Players can see what is locked behind what and plan accordingly, even if the layout is unfamiliar. Because groupings are random, players are regularly forced to research items they might otherwise ignore in order to reach something they want — this is intentional. Adaptation and variety are the primary design goals.

A small number of essential early-game techs are always placed at depth 1 (ungated) to prevent unplayable starts. Everything else is fair game for randomization.

Research times remain randomized per run as before — the forest structure adds gating variety on top of the existing time variance.

### 11.2 Research Mechanics

Each researchable item has two data fields: `base_ticks` and `max_variance`. At run start, actual research time is rolled from the seed:

```
actual_ticks = base_ticks × (1 + variance_roll)
```

where `variance_roll` is drawn uniformly over `[−max_variance, +max_variance]`. Hard floors and ceilings apply regardless of variance: never below `base × 0.5` or above `base × 1.6`. This means essential early-game items can be tuned to a tight range (low max_variance), while others create interesting run variety when they come early or late.

**Research times:**

Early-game essentials use low variance so they're reliably available. Mid-game items use higher variance to create run-to-run variety. Items that are powerful but not survival-critical can swing widely.

### 11.3 Hallucinogens & Visions

Taking certain hallucinogenic plants is a high-risk research shortcut. In visions, the player is presented with a choice of three revelations — one from each category — ensuring value for all player skill levels:

| Category | Revelation | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Plant Knowledge | Instantly identify 2–3 unknown plants currently on the map | Useful for players without real foraging knowledge |
| Tech Knowledge | Instantly unlock 1 random tool or camp station recipe | Useful for all players |
| The Nature Sight | Reveals hidden tile data for a duration; see below | Useful regardless of botanical expertise |

**Nature Sight**

The Nature Sight is an enhanced perception state that overlays rich environmental data on the map for its duration. When activated, the player chooses one overlay type for that day; the overlay can be changed to a different type each subsequent day while the buff remains active. This makes the Nature Sight a flexible and strategically valuable tool — the right overlay at the right moment can dramatically accelerate planning and resource acquisition.

**Available overlays:**

| Overlay | What it shows | Example use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Calorie Heatmap | Tile brightness = true calorie content of all harvestable food, including unknown plants, hidden caches, and beehives. Toxic plants glow equally — no safety information implied. | Quickly locate nut caches, beehives, or dense food patches in unexplored territory |
| Animal Density | Heat map of current animal population density per tile zone | Find optimal trap placement; locate turkey concentrations |
| Mushroom Zones | Highlights active log fungus and ground mushroom fruiting zones | Plan a mushroom harvest route |
| Plant Tile Compatibility | Overlay showing each tile's soil match score for a selected species | Identify where to hoe and seed a plant you want to establish |
| Fishing Hotspots | Water tile brightness = fish density | Find the best fishing spot on the river |

*(Additional overlays may be added; the system is extensible.)*

Key properties:
- Each day while the Nature Sight is active, the player selects which overlay to use from the available list. The choice is made at day start and persists for that day.
- Unknown plants are fully visible on the Calorie Heatmap. A bright cluster in unexplored territory is a signal worth investigating.
- Hidden food sources (squirrel caches, beehives, etc.) contribute their calorie value to their tile's heat on the Calorie Heatmap.
- The Calorie Heatmap reflects current harvestable calories, not seasonal potential. A pawpaw tree in spring shows low heat; the same tree in fall shows high heat. Bright canopy tile values include tool-gated portions — realising that full value requires returning with a stool or ladder.
- Soil fertility (nutrient level) and pH are revealed on mouseover for all tiles while any overlay is active.
- **Duration stacking:** if a vision grants the Nature Sight while it is already active, the new duration is added to the remaining duration. There is no cap — two back-to-back Sight visions in the same season produce a combined window of up to 10 days.

> ℹ️ Design note: The Nature Sight guides the player toward *where* to focus effort, not *what* to do. Identifying plants and assessing poison risk remain the player's responsibility. A glowing tile is an invitation to investigate, not a guarantee of safe food.

**Requesting a Vision**

The player requests a vision during the nightly debrief, provided the partner's cooldown is not active (maximum 2 per season; resets at season start). A "Request Vision" option appears on the debrief screen when the cooldown is available.

If the required hallucinogenic plant is already in the stockpile, the partner prepares and administers it immediately. If not, the partner requests the plant in the same way as a medicinal plant request — showing the plant's sprite with "Partner needs this plant to induce a vision." The partner never requests a plant that doesn't exist on the map.

**Cost and effects:** taking a vision always costs the player **50 ticks from the following day's budget** (the global timer starts at tick 50 the next morning, representing lost time to the experience). Additional side effects — health drain, incapacitation duration, tick losses — depend on the specific plant used and are defined in its `dose_response` band's `debuff` field (see Section 5.2a). Mild species may have only the base 50-tick cost; high-risk species like Datura carry significant additional effects even at the therapeutic dose.

**Self-administration:** eating hallucinogenic plants directly in the field (without partner preparation) follows the normal ingestion path defined by the plant's `ingestion` object. Dose accumulates against the `dose_response` bands; if the hallucinogenic dose band is reached without partner preparation, the `hallucinogen` effect does not trigger — only the accompanying health effects apply. No vision is produced from self-administration under any circumstances. An identified hallucinogenic plant shows a warning before the player eats it; an unidentified one does not.

**Multiplayer:** in multiplayer, each player can request a vision independently during the debrief. A player who takes a vision is stunned at camp (cannot act) until the global timer reaches tick 50 the following day. Other players proceed normally. For the purpose of global time advancement (Section 17.4), a stunned player is treated as if they have already spent all ticks up to tick 50 — they do not hold up other players. The stunned player's UI shows the vision revelation screen; they make their choice and are released at tick 50 when the global timer catches up.

**Substance data:** all hallucinogen properties are defined within the plant part sub-stage's `ingestion.dose_response` bands in plant JSON (see Section 5.2a for the full schema). There is no separate substance registry. The partner reads the `ingestion` object when advising the player on risk and when preparing the substance at the correct dose. A mild species might offer all three vision categories with only the base tick-loss; a high-risk species like Datura sits in a dose band where the vision effect and significant health drain overlap — the partner's role is to control the dose precisely, not eliminate the risk. The full plant list and their specific ingestion profiles are determined during the data pipeline phase.

**Psilocybe caerulipes as a vision ingredient:** *Psilocybe caerulipes* (Blue-footed Psilocybe) is a ground mushroom (§3.8.7) with the `psilocybin` tag. It can be stockpiled and used by the partner in the vision ritual exactly like hallucinogenic plants. When partner-administered it produces a standard vision. When consumed raw or in stew without partner preparation, it produces only the accidental effect (−20% tick budget, mild UI scrambling) with no vision — identical to the self-administration rule for all hallucinogens above.

**`nausea_family`** is the animal equivalent of `plant_family` in the nausea similarity system. Three categories are used for initial species: `lean_game` (rabbit, squirrel, beaver), `game_bird` (turkey and any future birds), and `fish`. This means eating rabbit, squirrel, and beaver on consecutive days is recognised as monotonous; mixing lean game with fish or game bird is not.

**Bait mechanic:** when a bait item is placed with a trap, the system checks whether the bait's plant `id` appears in the target species' `diet` array. If it does, the bait's `scent_strength` value is used as a catch rate multiplier (e.g. `scent_strength` 0.8 → 1.8× base catch rate). If the plant is not in the animal's diet, bait has no effect. No separate bait effectiveness field is needed — scent strength and diet together determine it entirely.

**`current_sensitivity`** (fish only): a 0.0–1.0 value representing how much river current degrades rod fishing for this species. It applies in two places: it reduces the bite attraction rate on fast-moving water tiles, and it reduces the hook rate (the second-stage catch roll) by making hook-setting harder in current. A value of 0.2 means fast water has minimal effect; a value of 0.9 (e.g. sucker) makes the species very difficult to catch by rod in fast water. Does not affect weir catch rates.

**Lodge bonus** (beaver only): placing any trap on a tile adjacent to a visible beaver lodge grants +0.3 to the effective zone density for catch probability calculation, reflecting territorial behaviour. No bait needed.

### 12.2 Trap Types

Traps define which animals they can catch via a `max_catch_weight_g` threshold — any species whose `weight_range_g` overlaps below that threshold is catchable. New animal species require no trap changes; new trap types require no animal changes.

| Trap | Max Catch Weight | Placement | Trap Catch Modifier (`trap_catch_modifier`) | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Simple Snare | Passive animal trap | 3 cordage units + 1� `stiff_stick` | 8 ticks | Place on trail; check next day; cordage quality affects reliability |
| Dead-fall Trap | Passive animal trap, more reliable | 2 cordage units + 1 `heavy_rock` + 1 pole | 12 ticks | `heavy_rock` carried from any glacial erratic tile (3 ticks to harvest; occupies 2�2 inventory slots; sled recommended if moving multiple). More materials but higher catch rate than snare |
| Fish Trap / Weir | — (fish only) | Moving water tile (`fast_river`, `slow_river`, `stream`) | 1.0× | Catches any fish species at placement tile; 2 catch attempts per night; accumulates up to 3 fish before full; uncollected catches disappear after 2 days; poach rate ~half that of land traps |
**Trap resolution:** All traps resolve overnight, once per night. At the nightly tick-over, each placed trap rolls against its catch probability. The outcome is determined immediately and persists until the player checks the trap:

- **Catch** — the trap holds an animal waiting to be collected. The trap is flagged as sprung.
- **Escape / no catch** — nothing happens; the trap resets and will roll again the following night.
- **Poached** — a small independent chance that a predator takes any catch before the player retrieves it, leaving the trap sprung but empty. Probability scales with days since the catch resolved (fresh catches are rarely poached; a week-old forgotten trap is very likely empty).

**Checking a trap:** The player moves to the trap tile and selects **Check Trap** (2 ticks). If the trap is sprung with a catch, the player can collect the animal in two ways:

- **Field butcher (knife required):** If a knife is in inventory, the player can butcher the carcass on the spot, selecting which parts to take (meat, hide, bone, fat). This is the efficient path — discarding the hide or bone at the trap site prevents carry weight from being wasted on parts the player doesn't need right now. Each part selected costs an additional 3–5 ticks. The raw carcass itself is not added to inventory; only the chosen parts are.
- **Carry whole carcass (no knife):** Without a knife, the player retrieves the carcass as a single unprocessed item. Full carcass weight must be carried back to camp for processing. Carcass weight is the sum of all part yields — significantly heavier than a selective field-butcher load. Processing at camp still requires a knife; without one, only the hide scraping step is blocked until a knife is crafted.

If the trap is sprung but empty (poached or escaped), the player sees a sprung trap and the trap resets. An unsprung trap shows its current state on inspect — the player does not need to check it to see that it hasn't fired. There is no penalty for not checking a trap daily other than potential poaching loss.

### 12.3 Animal Population Model

Animal populations are tracked as density values (0.0–1.0) per zone. The map is divided into roughly 6×6 zones (~50×50 tiles each). No individual animals are simulated — they materialise only when a trap catches one or the player triggers a passive interaction (beehive, squirrel cache).

```
Trap catch probability = base_catch_rate × zone_density × bait_modifier × season_modifier × trap_catch_modifier
```

where `trap_catch_modifier` is the value in the Trap Catch Modifier column of the trap table in Section 12.2. For a simple snare (1.0×) with no bait on a zone at 0.7 density in summer (1.0×): `0.25 × 0.7 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 1.0 = 0.175` — a ~17.5% daily catch chance per trap.

All per-species population values are defined in the animal species object (see 12.1). Resolved values for the initial species:

| Species | Starting Density | Density/Catch | Daily Recovery | Spillover | Depletion Threshold |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *sylvilagus_floridanus* (Cottontail) | 0.7 | −0.03 | +0.015 | 0.05 | 0.2 |
| *sciurus_carolinensis* (Grey Squirrel) | 0.6 | −0.025 | +0.012 | 0.04 | 0.2 |
| *meleagris_gallopavo* (Wild Turkey) | 0.4 | −0.04 | +0.008 | 0.02 | 0.2 |
| *castor_canadensis* (Beaver) | 0.3 | −0.06 | +0.004 | 0.01 | 0.2 |

**Season modifiers** are defined per-species in the animal object. Key behaviours: squirrels reduce to 0.4× in winter (deeper in burrows); turkeys peak at 1.3× in spring and fall; beaver are unavailable (0.0×) when their water tile is frozen; rabbits are available year-round at 1.0×.

**Hibernation flag:** setting `"hibernates": true` on a species reduces its catch rate to 0.0 for winter regardless of density. No current Indiana species use this but it supports future additions.

**Depletion:** if zone density drops below `depletion_threshold`, catch probability degrades sharply. Full recovery from 0.0 to baseline takes roughly 2 in-game seasons for fast-reproducing species (rabbit, squirrel) and 1–2 epochs for slower ones (beaver).

**Migration:** each day, density bleeds from high-density adjacent zones into low-density neighbours: `spillover_rate × (neighbour_density − local_density)`. A depleted zone near a healthy one recovers faster.

**Epoch rollover:** at epoch transition, each zone's density is projected forward 5 years using accumulated trapping pressure, recovery rates, and landscape changes.

### 12.4 Fishing

Fish use the same density model but keyed to individual water bodies rather than map zones. Each river segment and pond has its own fish community drawn from the species list below.

**Fish density mechanics:**
- Healthy rivers start at `starting_density: 1.0` — a full, untouched population. Ponds start at 0.8.
- Recover roughly 3–4× faster than mammals, but heavy weir or rod pressure depletes a water body quickly. The density model provides natural diminishing returns — stacking many weirs or auto-rods on the same body tanks local density within days.
- Drought concentrates fish into shrinking water bodies, multiplying effective density in remaining tiles while reducing total accessible area.
- Flood disperses fish briefly, then concentrates them as waters recede.
- Frozen ponds: density unchanged; ice fishing gives access via axe hole (see §15.5). Rivers stay liquid and weirs remain operable in winter at reduced catch rate.
- `current_sensitivity` on the species object modifies rod bite attraction rate and hook rate on fast-moving water tiles; weir catch rates are unaffected.

UI communicates fish presence via surface clues: ripples, jumping fish, water clarity. High-density tiles show more surface activity. Partner can research fish communities to reveal species composition and seasonal peaks.

**Weir mechanics:**
- Weirs may only be placed on moving water tiles: `fast_river`, `slow_river`, or `stream`. Ponds do not support weirs.
- Each weir makes **2 catch attempts per night**, each rolling the standard catch probability formula independently. A weir can therefore catch 0–2 fish per night under normal conditions.
- Catch formula: `base_catch_rate × water_body_density × season_modifier` (capped at 1.0 per attempt).
- A weir accumulates up to **3 fish** before it is full and stops rolling. The player must check and clear it to resume catching.
- **Uncollected catches disappear after 2 days** — fish die or escape if not retrieved.
- **Poach rate** is roughly half that of land traps — a submerged weir is less visible to scavengers than a sprung snare on a trail.
- **Durability:** weirs degrade from current stress. A weir on a `fast_river` lasts ~10 days before becoming non-functional; `slow_river` ~15 days; `stream` ~20 days. Durability degrades faster during flood events. A degraded weir shows as "damaged" on inspect and makes no catch rolls until repaired. **Repair cost:** 2 cordage units + 2× `stiff_stick`, 15 ticks. Maintenance is a routine chore, not a rebuild.

**Fishing bait** — bait can be attached to a fishing rod or auto-rod before placing/using it. Two bait types are available:
- **Earthworms** — harvested as a secondary yield from digging on non-frozen ground outside the Freezing band (see §8.5). `decay_days: 1`; must be used same day. Effective for most species.
- **Bee larvae** — harvested as part of a beehive harvest (see §13.1). Also short shelf life. Effective for most species.

Both bait types carry a `fish_bait: true` flag, which bypasses the standard plant-diet check and applies a flat **1.5× bite attraction multiplier** and a **+15% hook rate bonus**. Bait is consumed on any bite (successful catch or escape) — the fish takes it either way. Fishing without bait is viable but noticeably slower and less reliable.

**Active fishing rod — mechanics:**

Active rod fishing uses a two-stage bite → catch resolution. The player stands on any tile adjacent to a water tile and selects **Fish**. They input a tick investment (minimum 5, up to their remaining budget). The session then runs tick-by-tick until either a bite occurs or the budget is exhausted.

*Stage 1 — Bite:* Each tick, the system rolls for a bite event. The expected ticks-to-bite is derived from:

```
bite_attraction = base_catch_rate × water_body_density × season_modifier × bait_multiplier × current_attraction_penalty
```

where `current_attraction_penalty = 1.0 − (current_sensitivity × current_strength)` (0.0 on still water; scales up on `slow_river`, `fast_river`). This produces an expected ticks-to-bite roughly between 15 (optimal conditions) and 80 (poor conditions). If the tick budget runs out with no bite, the session ends and the invested ticks are spent.

When a bite occurs, the session pauses and the player is notified ("A fish is biting!"). The ticks up to that point are spent. The player then resolves the catch immediately.

*Stage 2 — Catch:* A single catch roll is made:

```
hook_rate = base_hook_rate × bait_hook_bonus × (1.0 − current_sensitivity × current_hook_penalty)
```

`base_hook_rate` is 0.65. Bait adds +0.15 (making baited hook rate 0.80 in still water). `current_sensitivity` degrades both terms in fast water.

- **Catch succeeds:** the fish is added to inventory. A line snap roll is then made (see below). The session ends regardless of snap outcome. The player can choose to start a new fishing session with their remaining tick budget.
- **Catch fails (escape):** the fish got away and took the bait. The rod is functional but needs re-baiting before the bait bonus applies again. The player can immediately start a new session with remaining budget (baitless, or re-bait first).

The bite/escape outcome gives the player meaningful information: frequent bites indicate a productive spot; no bites at all suggest sparse fish or bad conditions worth moving on from.

*Line snap:* resolved after every successful catch. Probability:

```
snap_probability = 0.10 × (fish_weight_g / 500) × (1.0 / cordage_strength_modifier)
```

At baseline (500g fish, medium cordage at 1.0×): 10% snap chance. A 3kg catfish on coarse cordage (~0.5×) reaches ~120% — near-certain snap. A 3kg catfish on high-quality dogbane cordage (1.2×+) drops to ~50%. This makes premium cordage a concrete priority for fishing in pike or catfish habitat.

On snap: the rod loses its bone hook and 1 cordage unit and becomes non-functional. **Repair:** 1 bone hook + 1 cordage unit, ~5 ticks (can be done in the field).

**Auto-rod — mechanics:**

An auto-rod is placed on any land tile cardinally adjacent to a water tile (not on the water tile itself). Ponds and moving water are both valid. It uses the same two-stage bite → catch resolution as the active rod, but fires autonomously and continuously while in the **Live** state.

*Attraction rate:* Auto-rods use **half the bite attraction rate** of active rods — a static bait in water is less effective than a worked lure. All other formula terms (density, season, bait, current penalty) apply normally.

*Auto-rod states:*
- **Live** — baited, armed, making continuous bite attempts. Attempts fire during both `advanceTick` (daytime) and `advanceDay` (overnight simulation).
- **Triggered (catch)** — a bite occurred and the catch roll succeeded. Fish is on the line, bait is gone. Rod makes no further attempts. Player must visit to collect the fish and re-bait to return to Live.
- **Triggered (escape)** — a bite occurred but the catch roll failed. Bait is gone. Rod makes no further attempts. Player must visit to re-bait to return to Live.
- **Broken** — a snap occurred on a successful catch. Fish was still landed (added to a pending collection on the rod). Rod makes no further attempts until repaired and re-baited.

In all non-Live states the rod is inert until the player visits. This creates a natural daytime management loop: place several auto-rods in the morning along your foraging route, check and re-bait them as you pass, collect catches opportunistically.

*Overnight simulation:* `advanceDay` simulates the full ~200 overnight ticks for each Live auto-rod as a Poisson process — rolling bite events in sequence until either a bite fires (transitioning the rod to Triggered or Broken) or the window ends with no bite. The same formula and fish pool are used as in daytime resolution. Density is decremented on catches, not on escapes. No separate overnight code path is needed — it is the same resolution logic run headlessly.

*Population impact:* Only successful catches reduce water body density. Escapes (bait taken, fish not landed) do not hit the density model.

*Re-bait cost:* 1 bait item per re-bait. The player re-baits by visiting the rod tile and selecting Re-bait (2 ticks). A rod without bait remains Live but uses the no-bait attraction rate.

*Line snap:* same formula as active rod, resolved on each successful catch. On snap, the rod transitions to Broken. Repair requires a visit: 1 bone hook + 1 cordage unit, 5 ticks.

**Initial Indiana fish species:**

| Latin ID | Common Name | Habitat | Seasonal Peak | Weight Range | Yield | Calories | Rod? | Current Sensitivity | Notes |

| *micropterus_salmoides* | Largemouth Bass | Pond, lake edge | Spring, Fall | 500–3,000g | 1,140g | 1,480 | Yes | 0.3 | High-value catch; occasional windfall in weir |

**The White Sucker spawning run** is a real spring phenomenon. Dense concentrations of suckers move into shallow river reaches to spawn across the full 10-day spring season. The run is modeled with a tapering season modifier rather than a flat bonus, reflecting peak density early in spring that thins as the run progresses:

| Spring days | Season modifier | Effect at full density (2 weirs) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Days 1–3 | 5.0× | ~4 suckers/night, near-guaranteed |
| Days 4–6 | 3.5× | ~4 suckers/night, still very reliable |
| Days 7–10 | 1.5× | 2–3 suckers/night, tapering off |

A player with a weir placed before spring begins captures the full run. One caught mid-run still benefits from the tail end. Because suckers are `rod_compatible: false` and have `current_sensitivity: 0.9`, they cannot be meaningfully caught by rod — the weir is the only viable method. This is the primary mechanical incentive to research and build the weir early.

**Balance across epochs:** At full investment (2–3 river weirs + 2 pond auto-rods), fishing contributes roughly 15–20% of family calorie needs in epochs 1–2, declining proportionally as the family grows in epochs 3–5. It is a meaningful supplement and the sucker run is a genuine annual windfall, but fishing alone cannot sustain the family at any epoch. A challenge event that disrupts local fish populations (disease, drought, upstream disturbance) can threaten players who have leaned heavily on this food source — see §15 for event hooks.

**Squirrel Caches**

Squirrel caches are tile properties placed at world-gen time on dead tree and hollow trunk tiles within high squirrel density zones. They are not animal objects — they do not use the population model. The species and sub-stage eligible to appear in a cache are determined by the `can_squirrel_cache: true` flag on plant part sub-stages (see §5.2 schema) — only flagged parts are considered. The flag should be set on whichever form the squirrel would actually store, whether a directly-harvested part or a processing output.

**Cache properties per tile:**
- `nut_content_kg` — set at world-gen and at each epoch refill, range 0.3–1.2 kg, drawn from the map's `can_squirrel_cache`-eligible species

**Discovery:** Inspecting a dead tree or hollow trunk tile directly reveals any cache present: "You find a squirrel cache — [X] kg of [nut species]." No special tool or prerequisite needed. The Nature Sight shows cache tiles as elevated heat.

**Harvesting:** 5–10 ticks, no tool required. Partial harvest allowed — remaining content persists until the next epoch. A fully emptied cache tile becomes a normal dead tree tile until the next epoch refill.

**Epoch refill:** At each epoch transition, caches are regenerated based on local squirrel zone density. Fewer squirrels means fewer cache tiles are placed across the zone — but each individual cache that is placed is full-sized, since the remaining squirrels have more nuts to themselves. A heavily trapped zone may have zero caches at epoch start; a lightly hunted zone regenerates its full complement.

## 14. Disease & Medicine

### 14.1 Injury & Illness Types

Injuries and illness deal direct damage to the Health bar rather than a separate bar. Health recovers naturally at 5% per day when Hunger and Thirst are both above zero; treatment speeds this recovery or prevents further degradation.

| Condition | How Contracted | Effects | Treatment | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Scratches & cuts | Night movement, storms, field hazards | Small Health hit on injury | Poultice (field or camp) | Untreated cuts have infection probability; see below |
| Wound Infection | Untreated cut after 1–2 days; probability scales with wound severity | Health drains 0.1 bar/day; duration scales with wound size | Antibacterial poultice (plantain, yarrow) by partner; halves duration | Small scratch ~10% infection risk; deep cut ~50% |
| Fever / Flu | Random event; higher risk when health bar is low or weather is wet+cold | Health drains 0.08 bar/day; −30 ticks/day; lasts 4–6 days | Fever-reducing tea (willow bark) by partner; halves duration | Most dangerous when stockpile is lean |
| Gut Illness | High chance from drinking from pond, or tiny chance drinking from running water | Nausea ceiling drops to 0.5 for 2-4 days; | Tannin-rich tea (oak bark, blackberry root) by partner; increases ceiling to .75 and halves duration | Ever-present risk of drinking water outside of camp |
| Sprain | Night movement; storm event | Movement and tick penalties scale with severity; see below | Anti-inflammatory poultice (Solomon's seal root) by partner; reduces duration by 1 severity tier | Stacking sprains escalate severity rather than adding a new condition |
| Bee stings | Beehive harvesting | Small Health hit | Antibacterial poultice by partner | Smoker reduces base sting chance from 60% to 15% |
| Amatoxin poisoning | Eating *Amanita bisporigera* or *Galerina marginata* | Day 0–1: asymptomatic. Day 2: nausea bar spikes, Health drains 15%/day. Day 3+: Health drains 25%/day | **No antidote.** No cure exists — the only factor is whether the tribe member's health bar is high enough to outlast the drain, which it will not be at standard starting health. | The 2-day asymptomatic window makes the cause nearly impossible to trace without prior species knowledge. See §3.8.7e. |
| Orellanine poisoning | Eating *Cortinarius rubellus* (Deadly Webcap) | Onset 5–8 days after ingestion: persistent Health drain 8%/day, −15 ticks/day; lasts 6–10 days | **No antidote.** Long delay makes attribution extremely difficult without knowledge of the species. | Not reliably fatal at full health but creates compounding vulnerability. See §3.8.7e. |
| Gyromitrin poisoning | Eating raw or undercooked *Gyromitra esculenta* (False Morel) | Immediate: nausea bar spikes to high band, −20% Health | No antidote; condition resolves in 1–2 days. **Preventable:** dried + cooked preparation fully neutralises the toxin. | Single exposure rarely fatal at full health; dangerous if repeated or health already low. See §3.8.7e. |
| GI irritant poisoning | Eating Jack-o'-Lantern or Earthball | Nausea bar spikes sharply; no lasting health damage in a healthy tribe member | Resolves in 1–2 days; no treatment required | Informative rather than lethal. See §3.8.7e. |

**Infection duration by wound severity:**

| Wound Size | Untreated Duration | Treated Duration |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Small scratch | 3–4 days | 1–2 days |
| Moderate cut | 6–8 days | 2–3 days |
| Deep cut / large wound | 10–14 days | 3–4 days |

Health drains at 0.1 bar/day for the full untreated duration. Infection resolves on its own without treatment but drains health the entire time — a 14-day deep infection drains 1.4 bars total. Wound severity is set when the injury condition is created and determines both infection probability and maximum untreated duration.

**Sprain severity tiers:**

A sprain is a single condition with three severity tiers rather than a stackable condition. When a sprain is inflicted, if no sprain is currently active, it is added at Light severity. If a sprain is already active, its severity escalates by one tier instead — Light → Medium → Severe. A second sprain injury while already at Severe has no additional effect.

| Severity | Movement penalty | Tick budget penalty | Duration | Treated duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Light | +0 ticks/tile | −10 ticks/day | 1 day | — (resolves overnight regardless) |
| Medium | +1 tick/tile | −20 ticks/day | 2 days | 1 day |
| Severe | +2 ticks/tile | −30 ticks/day | 3+ days (roll 3–5) | 2 days |

**Duration counting:** When a sprain escalates to a higher severity tier, the duration counter resets and counts from the day of escalation, not from the original injury day. A Light sprain contracted on Day 1 that escalates to Medium on Day 2 will last until Day 4 (2 days from Day 2), not Day 3.

Treatment by the partner (anti-inflammatory poultice) reduces the active severity by one tier at the next nightly debrief — Severe becomes Medium, Medium becomes Light, Light resolves entirely. A Light sprain always resolves after one night regardless of treatment. The player can field-apply a poultice with the same `anti_inflammatory_poultice` treatment tag; this works silently per the normal field treatment rules (see Section 14.2) and has the same tier-reduction effect, applied immediately rather than at debrief.

The sprain condition object uses a `treatable_by` field like all other conditions, enabling the partner's standard stockpile scan (§14.3) to locate the correct plant without a special code path:

```json
{
  "condition_id": "sprain",
  "instance_id": "sprain_8_1",
  "day_contracted": 8,
  "severity": "Medium",
  "treated": false,
  "effects": [
    { "type": "movement_penalty", "ticks_per_tile": 1 },
    { "type": "tick_budget_penalty", "ticks_per_day": 20 }
  ],
  "treatable_by": ["anti_inflammatory_poultice"]
}
```

`severity` replaces `duration_days_remaining` for sprains — duration is derived from the severity tier table above. Escalation increments `severity` and resets `treated` to `false` so the partner can treat the new tier.

```json

}
```

`instance_id` is a unique identifier for this specific condition instance, used to distinguish multiple simultaneous wounds of the same type (e.g. two separate scratches). Format: `{condition_id}_{day_contracted}_{sequence}`. The partner treats each active instance independently at debrief.

The `treatable_by` field is a tag matched against a `treatment_tag` field on `dose_response` entries in plant data. The partner finds matching plants by scanning that tag across all plants in the stockpile.

**Condition stacking:** Most conditions (scratches, cuts, wound infections, fever, gut illness, bee stings) are per-instance — each new injury or illness creates a separate condition object in the family member's active conditions list, with its own `treated` flag, `duration_days_remaining`, and `day_contracted`. Multiple simultaneous wounds are tracked and treated independently. Sprains are the exception: they use a single escalating condition object rather than stacking (see Section 14.1).

The `treated` flag is set to `true` when any treatment is successfully applied to a condition instance — whether by the player in the field (Section 14.2) or by the partner at debrief (Section 14.3). Once `true`, no further treatment is accepted for that condition instance from either source. Sprains are a partial exception: escalation resets `treated` to `false` on the sprain object, so the partner can treat each newly elevated severity tier. A sprain that has been treated and then escalates is treatable again at the new severity.

### 14.2 Field Treatment

**Any plant part in the player's inventory has an "Apply as poultice" option in its context menu, regardless of what the plant data says.** No UI filtering; the option is always visible. The player applies it and the game silently processes the result using the `ingestion` data — if the part has `poultice` in `preparation.processed_methods` with a `medicinal` effect matching an active condition, it works. If not, nothing happens and no feedback is given. A player with real botanical knowledge uses the right plant; one without just wastes ticks trying things.

Before applying, the game checks the target condition's `treated` flag. If `true`, the application has no effect and no feedback is given — ticks and cordage are still consumed. If `false` and the plant matches, the effect is applied and `treated` is set to `true`.

Cordage is consumed on application (binding the poultice). Costs ticks from player budget.

### 14.3 Camp Treatment

Partner knows all medicinal plants unconditionally — no identification gate applies to partner medicine knowledge.

At nightly debrief the partner scans all family member health bars for active conditions where `treated` is `false`:

1. If a suitable plant part (matching `treatable_by` tag) is in the stockpile in sufficient quantity: partner prepares and administers automatically. A brief notification appears ("Partner treated [person]'s [condition] with [plant]"). The condition's `treated` flag is set to `true`.
2. If no suitable plant is in stockpile: the partner shows the plant's sprite with the text *"Partner needs this part from this plant to treat [person]'s [condition]."* No location is given. Background checks confirm the plant exists on the map in sufficient quantity before the request appears — the partner never asks for something unobtainable.

Conditions where `treated` is already `true` are silently skipped — the partner will not attempt re-treatment or request additional plants for them.

### 14.4 Plant Medicinal & Toxicity Data

All pharmacological data for plant parts lives in the `ingestion` object on each sub-stage (see Section 5.2a for the full schema). Section 14 describes the gameplay consequences; the data pipeline is the source of truth for specific values.

The partner reads `ingestion` data to:
- Identify which plants treat which conditions (via `dose_response` bands with `medicinal` effects and `treatment_tag`)
- Determine correct preparation method and dose for safe therapeutic use
- Advise on hallucinogen risk before administration
- Recognise overdose symptoms and recommend treatment

The player reads `ingestion` data (for identified plants) to:
- See what conditions a plant can treat in the inspect panel
- Receive a warning before eating a plant with a toxic dose band

Unidentified plants show no ingestion data. Eating an unidentified plant with a toxic profile gives no warning — the onset delay (`pharmacokinetics.onset_ticks`) means symptoms may appear well after the cause, making diagnosis harder without partner knowledge.

*Note: Hallucinogens as research shortcuts are covered in Section 11.3. The ingestion system handles them uniformly — there is no separate hallucinogen mechanic, only a `hallucinogen` effect type within the standard `dose_response` structure.*

## 15. Weather & Challenge Events

### 15.1 Base Weather Variables

**Temperature** — continuous value per day, driven by a seasonal sine curve with a gradual daily variance offset layered on top. Seasonal curve runs from a winter minimum (~15°F / −9°C) to a summer peak (~88°F / 31°C) for Indiana. The variance offset persists between days and drifts by up to ±3°F each morning, clamped to a [−6, +6] range relative to the seasonal baseline. This means cold snaps and warm spells build and fade over several days rather than flickering randomly — a stretch of cold days is a recognisable weather event, not noise. The UI shows a qualitative label only (Freezing / Cold / Cool / Mild / Warm / Hot) rather than raw numbers. Temperature affects: decay rates, plant growth speed, thirst modifier, cold protection requirement, partner camp maintenance cost, and water tile freeze state.

**Rain / Snowfall / Cloud cover** — all weather conditions are resolved as a single full-day state, determined at day start and fixed for the entire day. Weather does not change intraday — if it is raining at the start of the day, it rains all day. This is intentional: the player can see the day's weather when they leave camp and make decisions accordingly, with no mid-day surprises. Weather states affect soil moisture, movement cost, plant availability, the daily sun value (used for drying; see §4.5), and challenge event probability. Inter-day weather forecasting (e.g. a UI warning that rain is likely tomorrow) is a candidate for future implementation but is not in scope for initial release.

**Wind** — direction and strength (see scent system, Section 4.3)

### 15.2 Temperature Bands

| Label | Range | Effects |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Freezing | <25°F | Cold protection required outdoors; decay and drying stop; still water freezes after 2+ consecutive days; thirst modifier −20% |
| Cold | 25–40°F | Cold protection required outdoors |
| Cool | 40–55°F | No penalty; coat optional |
| Mild | 55–75°F | No penalty |
| Warm | 75–85°F | Thirst modifier +25%; hat halves the modifier |
| Hot | >85°F | Thirst modifier +50%; hat halves the modifier; future heat debuff hook |

### 15.3 Thirst

Thirst is one of the three status bars (alongside Hunger and Health). At camp, the partner handles water as part of daily maintenance — there is no tracked water storage and it cannot run out. All family members begin each day with a full thirst bar; no camp-side water management is required from the player.

The player must manage their own thirst actively while foraging in the field. The thirst bar drains during the day based on conditions.

**Baseline thirst mechanics:**

- **Base thirst drain:** 1.0 bar per 180 ticks of activity. This is a constant drain that occurs regardless of temperature. A player using their full 200-tick budget will deplete the thirst bar and need to drink at least once during the day.
- **Warm band (+25% modifier):** thirst drains at 1.25 bars per 180 ticks
- **Hot band (+50% modifier):** thirst drains at 1.5 bars per 180 ticks
- **Freezing band (−20% modifier):** thirst drains at 0.8 bars per 180 ticks — a subtle danger, as dehydration remains possible and cold suppresses the sensation of thirst
- **Hat equipped:** halves the temperature modifier (Warm becomes +12.5%, Hot becomes +25%, applied on top of the base 1.0 per 180 ticks rate)

**Thirst drain is tick-based, not time-based.** A player who stands still and uses zero ticks does not get thirsty from the passage of global time alone — thirst accumulates based on actions taken (local ticks spent), not on the day clock advancing. This means an AFK player in multiplayer does not suffer thirst drain while waiting for others.

If the thirst bar reaches zero, the Health bar begins draining at the same rate as hunger (1/3 bar per day per depleted bar, per Section 1.3).

The player's thirst bar is replenished by drinking. At camp it resets automatically overnight; in the field the player must seek water actively.

**Water Sources:**
- In the field — any river or pond tile: player can drink for free (0 ticks, instant). Fully restores thirst bar.
- **Waterskin** (craftable — hide + cordage): holds ~3 drinks. Player can drink anywhere without pathing to water. Refill at any water tile costs 2 ticks. Fills automatically at camp with safe water. Can fill at river (small risk) or pond (big risk) with gut illness risk.
- **Frozen ponds** (winter): player must use axe to break a hole (~10 ticks) before drinking or fishing. Hole refreezes overnight.

### 15.4 Cold Protection & Clothing

Camp is always warm enough due to the fire. The partner is never at cold risk. Only the player foraging outdoors requires cold protection. The coat and hat each occupy a dedicated equipment slot (torso and head respectively) — worn, not stored in the inventory grid.

**Coat**

Required below 40°F outdoors.

Cold exposure uses a simplified per-tick model:
- Health drain from cold is applied each tick while the player is outdoors in **Cold** or **Freezing**.
- Drain target is approximately **1/3 health bar per full in-game day** of continuous exposure.
- Wearing a coat fully suppresses this cold exposure drain in both Cold and Freezing.
- Being at camp is always safe from cold exposure regardless of equipment.

Coat implementation is a single-quality item (no Poor / Adequate / Good tiers).

Recipe requires three material slots: insulation, shell, and cordage binding.

*Insulation options (pick one):*
- Goose/duck down
- Cattail fluff
- Milkweed fluff

*Shell options (pick one):*
- Animal hide (~3,200g)
- Inner bark cloth (`inner_bark_cloth`)
- Bark sheet (`bark_sheet`)

**Hat**

Crafted from hide or bark material + cordage. Provides no cold protection. During Warm and Hot bands: halves the thirst modifier from heat.

> ℹ️ Partner camp maintenance costs (which include fire-tending) by season are listed in Section 7.4.

### 15.5 Ice & Frozen Water

Still water (ponds, bogs) freezes when temperature is in the Freezing band for 2+ consecutive days. Rivers stay liquid — flowing water does not freeze. Frozen tiles are walkable (same movement cost as normal ground). Rod fishing and fish trap / weir placement are unavailable on frozen tiles.

**Ice fishing:** player uses axe on a frozen tile to break a hole (~10 ticks). Hole stays open for the remainder of the day, then refreezes overnight. Active rod fishing only through the hole — no trap placement. Drinking from a frozen pond also requires breaking a hole first.

### 15.6 Challenge Events

Full in-gameplay challenge events (storms with warnings, player-visible floods, drought UI, camp station damage) are deferred — see Section 21. The following inter-epoch background events run silently during the epoch skip simulation and require no player-facing implementation:

| Event | Simulation effect |
|---|---|
| Storm / lightning | Strikes forested tiles; struck tree becomes standing dead snag; adjacent canopy has 25% spread chance; disturbed tiles flagged |
| Flood | River tiles expand temporarily; silt deposit boosts soil fertility on adjacent tiles; fish density redistributes |
| Drought | Soil moisture drops across affected zones; plant productivity reduced; fish concentrate in remaining water |

These fire as probability rolls inside `advanceDay` during the epoch simulation (see §3.9) with rendering off. The Traveler event is live-gameplay only and is not deferred — see Section 15.7.

### 15.7 Traveler Event

A wandering individual from another group passes through the map. The interaction is entirely optional — walking past the traveler tile skips it with no consequence.

**Frequency:** 1–2 per year, random season, never in winter.

**Behavior:** The traveler arrives at a tile near camp and remains for 1 full day before leaving. They will not move to the player; the player must visit their tile.

**Trade currency:** The traveler accepts any item from the player's stockpile. Value is assessed in a simple caloric-equivalent unit — rare processed items trade well, raw bulk material trades poorly.

**Traveler inventory:** 3–5 randomly selected items drawn from a pool:
- Rare plant seeds (not present in your current biome)
- Pre-researched tool recipe (saves research time; acts as an instant tech unlock)
- Dried food from another region (novel food type; resets the nausea monotony score)
- Single plant identification (names one currently unknown plant on your map)

### 16.2 Child Task Capability & Health

**Camp tasks:** tool/station crafting, material processing. **Plant research is partner-exclusive — children cannot pull research tasks from the queue and will skip over them to the next available crafting or processing task.** A child's daily tick budget for camp tasks scales with age at the epoch anchor points — the only ages children ever are at epoch start. Speed percentages apply to both tick budget and task execution rate.

| Age | Tick Budget | % of Adult |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5 | 90 ticks | 50% |
| 10 | 120 ticks | 67% |
| 15 | 180 ticks | 100% |
| 20 | 180 ticks | 100% (+ field foraging, see below) |

**Health:** Children use the same three-bar health system (Hunger, Health, Thirst) as adults with identical mechanics, just scaled to their calorie requirements (see Section 9.1). Thirst is never a concern for children since they are always in camp — water-carrying is included in the partner's daily camp maintenance cost, covering all family members automatically. Children eat automatically from the stockpile at nightly debrief. The protein/fat tick bonus applies to children on the same stew-based condition as adults. Nutritional deficiency debuffs (Section 9.1) apply to children using the same consecutive-day logic against their own calorie requirement. Children can contract illness from the same events as adults but at lower base probability. Children do not forage or incur field injuries until age 20.

**Task queue:** Children pull tasks from the same shared queue as the partner, with one exception: research tasks are skipped by children — when a child reaches a research task in the queue, they skip it and take the next non-research task instead. The partner and all children capable of camp tasks otherwise work in parallel — each worker takes the next available task in queue order. If a child's tick budget is exhausted mid-task, progress is saved and the task remains in the queue. The partner resumes the first incomplete task in queue order after finishing their own current task — a child's partially completed task is picked up naturally since the queue is just an ordered list with saved progress and any worker takes the next available item.

**Task distribution rules:** **Processing tasks** (fiber extraction, hide scraping, material drying, bean extraction, etc.) can be worked on by multiple workers simultaneously — the family can gang up on a large processing job to finish it faster. **Crafting and construction tasks** (tools, camp stations, etc.) can only be worked on by one person at a time — a partially completed workbench must be finished by one worker before another can start a different task. Multiple children can each work on different tasks simultaneously, but cannot collaborate on the same crafting task.

**Field foraging (age 20 / Epoch 5):** The child accompanies the player foraging, displayed on the same tile and moving with the player exactly — they do not path independently. They provide a 100% speed bonus to foraging tasks (harvest tick costs are halved while the child is present).

The foraging child has the same tick budget as the player (200 base, same stew-based bonuses apply). Their smaller inventory (4×3, 12 slots) and their own health bars are subject to the same field rules as the player (night penalty, injury risk). In multiplayer, the Epoch 5 child functions as an additional field companion for the group.

### 16.4 Landscape Changes Between Epochs

The landscape evolves during the 5-year skip via the full plant dispersal simulation defined in §3.9. In summary:

- **Perennial plants** survive or thin based on how heavily they were harvested during the player's year
- **Annual plants** die off and must re-establish from seed
- **Seeds spread** according to each plant's `dispersal` profile — method, radius, wind sensitivity, and animal involvement all vary by species
- **Cultivated patches** around camp grow denser if the player scattered seeds and harvested sustainably; over-harvested patches thin out
- **Pioneer species** rush into disturbed ground (felled tree gaps, bare soil from heavy harvesting)
- **Soil pH** shifts gradually from plant influence, compounding across epochs
- **The epoch transition screen** shows the player the updated map before resuming

See §3.9 for the complete simulation specification.

## 17. Multiplayer

### 17.1 Structure

This is a cooperative game. All players share the same camp, eat from the same stew at nightly debrief, and work toward the same win/loss condition. Each player controls their own independent character with their own tick budget, inventory, and health bars — they are additional foragers in the same family group. The partner remains an NPC; no player plays as the partner. One player is the host; others connect and send action requests. Host processes all actions and returns local area state changes to each player.

### 17.2 Nightly Debrief in Multiplayer

The nightly debrief screen is shared and fully collaborative — any player can modify the partner task queue, the meal plan, or any other debrief setting at any time. All changes appear live on every player's screen simultaneously. Players are expected to coordinate via the in-game chat (see Section 17.5); there is no locking or confirmation step. Last write wins.

### 17.3 In-Game Chat

A simple text chat interface is available at all times during multiplayer — accessible as a collapsible panel during foraging and always visible during the nightly debrief screen. Chat is not used by the game engine for any mechanical purpose; it is purely a coordination tool. No voice, no emotes — text only for initial implementation.

### 17.4 Time Synchronization & Local vs Global Ticks

All players can act freely in parallel. Global time advances at the rate of the slowest player (fewest actions taken). Prevents one fast player from burning through the day while others wait.

**Local vs Global Tick Systems:**

The game tracks two separate time values:

- **Global Tick Counter** — the shared world clock, advancing from 0 to 400 each day. Weather, daylight/darkness, and all environmental state are driven by this value. All players see the same global tick value.
- **Local Player Tick Budget** — each player's personal action budget (200 base + bonuses). This tracks how many actions each individual player has taken, independent of the global clock.

Global time advances at the rate of the slowest player — when all players have spent at least N ticks of their personal budgets, the global clock advances to tick N.

**System-specific tick rules:**

- **Pharmacology (drug onset, duration, processing windows)** — tracks each player's **Local Ticks**. If Player A consumes a toxic plant with `onset_ticks: 30` and then takes 40 actions while Player B is AFK, Player A experiences the effects at their local tick 30 regardless of the global clock position. Drug effects are tied to the individual's body state, not the world clock.
- **Daylight / Darkness threshold** — driven by **Global Ticks**. The darkness threshold (Section 6.4) is checked against the global clock. If the global clock is at tick 150 (still daylight in spring), no player suffers night penalties regardless of their local tick count. However, if a player's **Local Ticks** exceed the darkness threshold (e.g., Player A is at local tick 210 while global time is tick 180 in spring, where the threshold is 200), that player suffers night penalties even though the global clock says it's still light — they have exhausted themselves past the safe window.
- **Movement, injury risk, and all player actions** — consume **Local Ticks** from the player's personal budget.
- **Weather, animal movement, plant growth** — driven by **Global Ticks**.

### 17.5 Animal Handling

Active animals are deferred to a future implementation. In the initial release, animals only materialise when caught in a trap or found via passive interaction (beehives, caches). All such passive resolutions are tied to global tick advancement — they trigger when the slowest player's actions advance time past the relevant tick. No special per-player animal reactivity logic is needed for initial implementation.

### 17.6 Implementation Notes

- Turn-based action model keeps synchronization straightforward
- Each action: send request → host processes → return state delta
- Nightly debrief is a natural full synchronization point
- AFK handling: host has ability to kick players as needed; no in-game mechanic for AFK timeout

## 20. Technical Architecture

### 20.1 AI-First Design

Game is built for fast functional testing. Rendering is fully separated from logic so the engine runs headlessly for debugging and scenario validation. Target platform: Steam (desktop). Built with React, packaged for desktop via Electron/Capacitor. Shares project scaffolding with an existing game project. UI layer made in react, rest in pure JS.

Gemini 3.1 flash + nano banana for sprite generation (generate 4x4 sheet for each plant, regen for mistakes (make new sheets until we get all sprites we need for each plant by picking sprites from various sheets if the first gen didn't give us everything correct), validation, and atlas creation.

Gemini 3.1 flash for creation of plant objects.

Codex 5.3 for actual coding of game logic.

Kimi K2.5 for UI design and basic implementation, then integrate using Codex

### 20.2 Layer Separation

- **Game Logic Layer** — pure data, no rendering dependencies. All state as plain JSON. Deterministic: same inputs always produce same outputs. Core API: `advanceTick(gameState, action) → newGameState` for player actions; `advanceDay(gameState, context) → newGameState` for world progression (plant growth, weather, events, dormant seed germination). The simulation is `advanceDay` called in a loop with `context.rendering = false` — no separate simulation code path exists. Can simulate a full year in seconds without rendering.
- **Rendering Layer** — reads game state, never modifies it, fully swappable. gameState should include a list of any changes that require animation (something moving etc.) so the renderer can perform any animations needed. **Tile background color** is derived entirely in the rendering layer from each tile's `moisture` value: high moisture maps to a deep forest green (~`#2d4a1e`), low moisture maps to a dusty tan (~`#c8a96e`), linearly interpolated between those anchors. Plant sprites are composited on top of this background. No game logic references tile color.
- **Testing Interface** — scriptable scenarios, seed-based map reproduction.

### 20.3 Save / Load

Save = serialize full game state to JSON. Load = deserialize and resume. Enables sharing specific test scenarios between sessions.

### 20.4 Scent Simulation

Background particle system tied to wind vector + plant `scent_strength` field. Particles computed from game state; rendered as subtle botanical-coded symbols. Scent layer is part of rendering, not game logic — does not affect simulation.

### 20.5 Multiplayer

Host runs authoritative game logic. Clients send discrete action requests; receive state deltas. Nightly debrief is full synchronization point.

### 20.6 Resolution

Game targets **1920×1280**. The renderer uses an isometric projection with 64×64 px source tiles scaled to **4× (256×256 px)**, producing a visible tile footprint of 256 px wide × 128 px tall (2:1 isometric diamond).

At this scale the screen fits approximately **7.5 tiles wide × 10 tiles tall** in the visible area (1280 ÷ 128 = 10 tile rows; 1920 ÷ 256 = 7.5 tile columns). The half-tile at the right (and left) edge is a natural consequence of the isometric geometry — the camera anchors to the player's tile center, so clipping is symmetric on both sides. Near map edges the camera does not scroll past the boundary; it stops so that the visible area is padded with the map edge rather than rendering outside the tile array.

During normal gameplay there is no zoom or pan — the player sees only the area around their current position. Full-map overview is only available during the epoch-start camp relocation screen.

### 20.7 Isometric Rendering

**Coordinate system:** The game logic operates on a standard 2D integer grid (`[row, col]`). The rendering layer converts to screen space using the standard isometric transform:

```
screen_x = (col - row) × (tileWidth / 2)
screen_y = (col + row) × (tileHeight / 2)
```

where `tileWidth = 256` and `tileHeight = 128` at 4× scale. The origin of this transform is the center of the screen, offset by the camera position (which tracks the player's tile).

**Camera clamping:** The camera center is clamped so that no tile outside the 300×300 map array is requested. Near a map edge the camera shifts toward the interior — the player's tile is no longer screen-center, and the screen is instead filled with map tiles on one side and the edge tile row on the other. No tile rendering occurs beyond the hard map border.

**Draw order:** Tiles and their contents are rendered back-to-front (painter's algorithm) sorted by `row + col` ascending, then by `col` ascending within the same diagonal. This ensures correct occlusion of tall sprites (trees, large plants) without a separate z-buffer.

**Plant sprite anchoring:** Plant sprites are positioned with their horizontal center aligned to the tile's screen center-x and their bottom edge aligned to the tile's screen center-y (the "ground point" of the tile diamond). This keeps plants visually rooted to their tile regardless of sprite scale.

**Tile background:** The moisture-derived tile color (§20.2) is rendered as the filled isometric diamond polygon, not a rectangle. The rendering layer draws and fills the diamond before compositing plant sprites on top.

**Playable area shape (deferred):** The logical play area is intended to appear as a rectangle from the player's perspective, which requires the underlying tile selection to be a diamond in array space (a rotated square mask over the rectangular tile array). Defining this boundary and constraining map generation, pathfinding, and the camp placement UI to it is deferred — the initial implementation uses the full rectangular tile array with no boundary shaping. See §21.
